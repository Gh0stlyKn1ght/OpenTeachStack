## The Teacher Problem

Finishing an orientation course should not create false confidence.

You should leave with better questions, safer habits, and enough vocabulary to keep learning.

## The Idea

OTS-000 does not make you a developer.

It makes you harder to fool, easier to support, and more prepared to protect your curriculum work.

## Classroom Scenario

A teacher moves into OTS-101. They now understand what a project folder is, why source of truth matters, what GitHub is for, what AI agents can access, and why safety checks belong before publishing.

That is the on-ramp doing its job.

## Weak Version

```text
I know all the tools now.
```

No one knows all the tools.

## Better Version

```text
I know enough to navigate, ask better questions, protect my work, and keep learning.
```

## Build Step

Write a final reflection:

```text
I can now explain:
I can now navigate:
I will be careful with:
I still need help with:
My next OpenTeachStack course should be:
```

## Quality Check

Your reflection should include at least one remaining question. Real readiness includes knowing what is still unclear.

## Safety / Accessibility / Source Note

Keep using the safety checklist. The point of the course is not to remove caution. It is to make caution practical.

## Reflection

What can you build or inspect now that felt out of reach before?

## Capstone Connection

Save the Starter Kit and use it as your reference when entering OTS-101, OTS-240, OTS-301, or OTS-320.

## Checkpoint Decision Language

Use this checkpoint to make a plain decision about **Final Reflection**:

- **Ready** means the artifact can be used by another teacher without a side explanation from you.
- **Revise** means the idea is right, but the artifact still needs clearer steps, labels, evidence, or safer wording.
- **Blocked** means you cannot honestly finish the artifact yet because a tool, source, permission, example, or decision is missing.

## Evidence to Save

Save evidence that another teacher could inspect: a screenshot, copied command output, file path, short reflection note, or before-and-after excerpt. If the evidence is only in your memory, the checkpoint is not ready yet.

## Next Revision Trigger

Name the owner and trigger before you move on. Write who will revise it, when they will revisit it, and what would cause the next revision: a broken link, confusing student direction, missing source, unsafe permission, or a teammate review note.
