## The confusion

Students do not experience your curriculum map directly.

They experience whatever you put in front of them:

- the lesson page
- the lab handout
- the prompt
- the video
- the quiz
- the discussion question
- the LMS instructions
- the submission box

If that content is unclear, the course feels unclear even when the plan behind it is strong.

## Side-by-side comparison

Student-facing course content is the material learners actually use.

Instructional materials support teaching but may not belong in the student view.

| Piece | Student-facing course content? | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Lesson page students read | Yes | Students use it to learn and act. |
| Assignment instructions | Yes | Students use it to complete work. |
| Worked example | Yes | Students use it as a model. |
| Teacher answer key | No | It supports grading, not student action. |
| Equipment setup notes | Usually no | They are teacher preparation. |
| Slide deck | Sometimes | It depends whether students use it directly. |
| LMS post | Sometimes | It may be delivery text, not the source lesson. |

## Concrete example

For a robotics startup lesson, student-facing content might include:

- learning target
- short safety explanation
- four-step startup routine
- worked example
- practice scenario
- quick check
- submission instructions

Teacher-only materials might include:

- equipment setup notes
- answer key
- risk notes
- modification plan
- private class reminders

## Weak version

> Read about robotics safety and complete the activity.

This tells students almost nothing.

They cannot see the first action, the expected evidence, or the success criteria.

## Better version

> Read the four-step startup routine. Then label each step in the sample scenario and write one sentence explaining which step prevents accidental motor movement. Submit your labeled scenario and explanation.

This is student-facing because it names what learners read, do, and submit.

## Decision rule

Use this test:

> If students need it to learn, practice, decide, submit, or check their work, it is probably student-facing course content.

If the material helps the teacher prepare, grade, troubleshoot, or facilitate, it is probably instructional material.

Some pieces can be both, but they still need labels.

Platform location does not decide the audience by itself. A Google Classroom post can be student-facing, teacher-only, or only an export note depending on what it contains and who should rely on it.

## Practice

Open one lesson, assignment, slide deck, or LMS post.

Label each visible part:

- `student-facing`
- `teacher-only`
- `shared support`
- `platform delivery`
- `unclear`

Anything marked `unclear` is a candidate for revision.

## Takeaway

AI can help draft student-facing content, but only after the teacher names the audience and task.

Otherwise AI may mix student directions, teacher notes, answer keys, and platform copy into one polished blob.

OTS-000 may tell you where the piece lives. OTS-101 asks whether students should use it, teachers should keep it private, or reviewers should treat it as evidence.
