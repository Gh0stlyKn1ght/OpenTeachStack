A lesson can make sense to the teacher and still fail students.

That happens when the planning is clear in the teacher's head, but the course page does not tell students what to do, why it matters, what to use, what to make, or how to know they are on track.

This chapter is about the student-facing layer.

Not the whole unit plan.

Not the private teacher notes.

The actual lesson page students see.

OTS-000 helped you recognize files, Markdown, links, platforms, and project folders. In this chapter, those are only containers. The real question is whether the student-facing lesson inside the container gives students enough purpose, direction, materials, evidence, and quality checks to work without guessing.

## Why this chapter matters

AI can draft a lesson outline quickly.

But an outline is usually written for the teacher, not the student.

Students need a different kind of clarity. They need the learning path translated into action.

A good student-facing lesson answers:

- What am I learning?
- Why does this matter?
- What do I need?
- What do I do?
- What do I make or submit?
- How do I know if I am doing it well?
- What should I do if I get stuck?

## The chapter path

1. First, we separate teacher-facing planning from student-facing lesson content.
2. Then we build clear student directions.
3. Then we add examples, non-examples, and success checks.
4. Then you draft a complete student-facing lesson page.
5. Finally, you review whether a student could actually complete it without reading your mind.
