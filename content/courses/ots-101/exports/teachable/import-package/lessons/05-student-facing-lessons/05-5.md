> OTS-101 frame: AI supports teacher-approved course content that becomes student-facing only after review.

Before publishing a student-facing lesson, run the tired-student test.

Imagine a student opens the course page without you standing next to them.

They are not trying to be difficult. They are just tired, distracted, or unsure where to start.

Can they still understand the task?

<FrameworkBlock title="The tired-student test" eyebrow="Checkpoint">
  <ConceptCard title="Start">
    Can the student tell what to do first?
  </ConceptCard>
  <ConceptCard title="Materials">
    Can the student find the sources, examples, tools, or files?
  </ConceptCard>
  <ConceptCard title="Product">
    Can the student tell what to make or submit?
  </ConceptCard>
  <ConceptCard title="Quality">
    Can the student tell what strong work includes?
  </ConceptCard>
  <ConceptCard title="Support">
    Can the student tell what to do if they get stuck?
  </ConceptCard>
</FrameworkBlock>

## Read it like a student

Do not read the lesson like the person who wrote it.

Read it like someone seeing it for the first time.

Ask:

- What is the first click or action?
- What words might confuse students?
- What materials are missing or unnamed?
- What step depends on teacher explanation?
- What does the student submit?
- What does good work look like?
- What should a student try before waiting for the teacher?

## Find hidden teacher knowledge

Hidden teacher knowledge is anything the teacher knows but the course page does not say.

Examples:

- "They know which document I mean."
- "I will explain that part in class."
- "They should remember yesterday's chart."
- "They know where to submit it."
- "They know what a good answer looks like."

Maybe they do.

But if the lesson is meant to live in a course, the page should carry enough clarity to support students when memory or attendance fails.

## Review the lesson status

Choose one status.

## Ready to publish

The lesson is clear, aligned, accessible enough for the intended students, and verified by the teacher.

## Needs revision

The core lesson works, but students may get stuck because directions, materials, examples, or submission details are unclear.

## Not ready

The lesson does not match the target, depends too much on missing teacher explanation, or contains unverified AI content.

## Final student-facing review note

Use this note before moving on:

```text
Student-facing review:
- First action is clear: yes/no
- Materials are visible: yes/no
- Product is clear: yes/no
- Success check is included: yes/no
- Stuck-point support is visible: yes/no
- Teacher verification complete: yes/no
- Status: ready / revise / not ready
```

<TakeawayStrip tone="teacher">
  If a student cannot tell what to do without guessing, the lesson is not student-facing yet. It is still teacher-facing planning in disguise.
</TakeawayStrip>

## Evidence to keep

Save your student-facing review note along with one concrete piece of evidence: a sentence a first-time reader could not follow, and how you fixed it. Set the next review for after the first time students actually use the lesson.

## Weak Version

Weak student-facing review:

> I understand the lesson, so students probably will too.

That reviews the page from the teacher's memory, not from the student's first read.

## Better Version

Better student-facing review:

> A first-time reader can identify the first action, required materials, product, success check, and submission step. The only unclear sentence was revised.

That gives the teacher evidence that the page can stand on its own.

## Safety / Accessibility / Source Note

The tired-student test should include access: links open, videos have captions or alternatives, files are named clearly, and directions do not rely on color, memory, or hidden teacher explanation.

## Reflection

What did the page assume students remembered, knew, or could access?

## Capstone Connection

Save the student-facing review note with the lesson draft. The capstone packet should show that someone checked whether the page works for students.
