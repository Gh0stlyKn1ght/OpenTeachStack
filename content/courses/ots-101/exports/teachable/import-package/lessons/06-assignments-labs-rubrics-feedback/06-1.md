> OTS-101 frame: AI supports teacher-approved course content that becomes student-facing only after review.

Students can be busy without producing evidence.

They can click, sort, discuss, copy, highlight, drag, build, or submit something without showing the skill the lesson was meant to teach.

That is why every assignment or lab needs an evidence check.

## Why This Matters

AI can generate activities that keep students busy while hiding whether learning happened. The teacher's job is to name the evidence before accepting the activity.

## The common trap

A teacher asks AI:

> Create an activity for students about online source credibility.

The AI returns:

- a warm-up question
- a short article
- a group discussion
- a reflection prompt
- an exit ticket

That might be useful.

But the teacher still has to ask:

> What evidence proves students can evaluate source credibility?

If the answer is unclear, the activity is not ready yet.

## Activity language can hide weak evidence

Watch for activity words that sound useful but do not name evidence:

- explore
- discuss
- learn about
- engage with
- reflect on
- understand
- become familiar with
- investigate

These words are not bad.

They just need to be connected to a visible product.

For example:

Weak:

> Students explore source credibility.

Stronger:

> Students compare two sources and write a three-sentence decision explaining which source is more trustworthy and why.

The stronger version creates evidence the teacher can review.

Completion is not the same as evidence. A finished worksheet, uploaded photo, or submitted form only matters if it shows the target skill.

## Evidence should match the target

If the learning target says students will classify, the evidence should show classification.

If the target says students will justify, the evidence should include a justification.

If the target says students will build, the evidence should include the build or documentation of the build.

If the target says students will revise, the evidence should include before-and-after thinking.

## Quick evidence check

Before accepting an AI-drafted activity, ask:

- What will students turn in, show, record, build, or say?
- Can I see the target skill in that evidence?
- Would two teachers understand what counts as successful work?
- Does the evidence require students to think, or only complete steps?
- Is the task small enough for the available time?

If you cannot answer those questions, revise the assignment before publishing.

  A good activity keeps students engaged. A good assignment also leaves behind evidence the teacher can inspect.
</TakeawayStrip>

## Classroom Scenario

A teacher asks AI for a source-credibility activity. The draft includes a warm-up, a group conversation, and a reflection question.

Those pieces may help students practice, but the teacher still needs evidence that students can judge a source using clues from the source itself.

## Weak Version

Weak evidence plan:

> Students discuss which source seems better.

Discussion can be useful, but it disappears unless the task asks students to produce something the teacher can inspect.

## Better Version

Better evidence plan:

> Students choose the more trustworthy source and write four sentences naming two credibility clues and explaining why those clues support the choice.

Now the activity leaves evidence of the target skill.

## Build Step

Take one activity and name the evidence it will leave behind.

```text
Activity students do:
Learning target:
Evidence students produce:
What I can inspect:
What would not count as enough evidence:
```

That last line matters. It protects the assignment from accepting completion when the target requires reasoning, explanation, revision, performance, or source use.

## Quality Check

If the evidence could be produced without using the target skill, the assignment needs revision.

## Safety / Accessibility / Source Note

Evidence should not require students to disclose private experiences unless that disclosure is clearly optional, safe, and appropriate. Use fictional or source-based evidence when possible.

## Capstone Connection

Save one activity-to-evidence revision in the assignment and feedback packet. It shows the final task measures something real.
