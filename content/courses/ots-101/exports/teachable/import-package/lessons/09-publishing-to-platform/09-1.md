> OTS-101 frame: AI supports teacher-approved course content that becomes student-facing only after review.

A platform copy can look close enough to the source that nobody checks it carefully.

That is the trap.

Small publishing changes can break the student experience.

## The common scenario

A teacher has an approved lesson source.

It includes:

- learning target
- short explanation
- source link
- student directions
- example
- success check
- submission instructions
- teacher verification notes

Then the content is copied into the LMS.

After copying, the page looks mostly fine, but several things changed:

- the bullets became plain lines
- the source link requires teacher permission
- the example image has no explanation
- the submission instructions no longer match the LMS assignment type
- the rubric is in a separate place students cannot see
- the platform page does not say which source version it came from

Now the lesson is technically published, but not truly ready.

## Formatting can change meaning

Formatting is not decoration.

If a numbered process loses its numbers, the task gets harder. If a checklist becomes a paragraph, students may miss requirements. If headings disappear, students cannot scan the page.

After publishing, check:

- headings
- bullets
- numbered steps
- tables
- links
- callouts
- examples
- success checks

## Access can fail silently

A teacher may be able to open a link because they own the file.

Students may not.

Check the student view or use a student-access account when available.

Ask:

- Can students open the file?
- Does the link require permission?
- Is the resource blocked by school settings?
- Does the task require a tool students do not have?
- Does the file work on student devices?

## Submission settings matter

If the directions say "upload your chart" but the platform assignment only accepts text entry, students get stuck.

If the directions say "submit in the assignment box" but the page is only an announcement, students do not know where to go.

The platform settings are part of the lesson.

## Version drift is real

Once content is copied into a platform, it can drift.

Someone edits the platform page but not the source. Someone updates the source but forgets the platform. Someone copies last year's LMS page and misses an old link.

That is why platform copies need source notes and update dates.

<TakeawayStrip tone="warning">
  A published page is not ready just because it exists. It is ready when the student-facing platform version still matches the approved lesson intent.
</TakeawayStrip>

## What Happened

A teacher copies an approved source lesson into the LMS and uses AI to help shorten directions for the platform page.

The page looks cleaner, but the student view now has two problems: the rubric is hidden behind a teacher-only setting, and the AI-shortened directions no longer mention the required scenario response.

## What Looked Fine

The platform page was published. The headings were cleaner. The text sounded less crowded.

From the teacher account, the page looked ready.

## What Was Actually Broken

Students could not see the success criteria, and the directions no longer matched the evidence the teacher needed to collect.

The problem was not the platform itself. The problem was that the platform copy was judged by surface polish instead of student-view evidence.

## Root Cause

The source lesson, platform settings, and student-facing directions were not checked together after the move.

## The Fix

Restore the missing evidence direction, make the rubric visible to students, add a source/export note, and run the student-view check again.

The publishing decision should come after the student path works, not after the page looks published.
