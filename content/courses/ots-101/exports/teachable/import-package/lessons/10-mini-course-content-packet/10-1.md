> OTS-101 frame: AI supports teacher-approved course content that becomes student-facing only after review.

The packet should not include everything you created.

It should include the artifacts that prove the packet is teachable, inspectable, and ready for review.

That means approved or review-ready artifacts go in.

Loose drafts, abandoned AI outputs, stale exports, and mystery files stay out.

## The packet is a curated set

A Mini Course Content Packet is not a storage folder.

It is a curated set of teaching materials and review evidence.

It should help a teacher answer:

- What is the course segment about?
- What will students learn?
- What will students do?
- What evidence will students produce?
- How will quality be judged?
- What has the teacher verified?
- What still needs revision?

## What should stay out

Keep these out of the final packet unless they are clearly labeled as appendices:

- unreviewed AI drafts
- duplicate final versions
- old platform exports
- abandoned prompts
- private student data
- teacher-only notes mixed into student pages
- screenshots with sensitive information
- source material without permission or access notes
- anything that looks official but has not been reviewed

The packet should build trust, not confusion.

## Separate teacher-facing and student-facing pieces

The packet may include both teacher-facing and student-facing materials, but they should be labeled.

Student-facing pieces include:

- lesson page
- directions
- examples
- success checks
- assignment or lab
- submission instructions

Teacher-facing pieces include:

- verification notes
- answer key
- source notes
- rubric rationale
- revision plan
- platform publishing checklist

Labeling prevents accidental publishing of private teacher notes.

## Include status

Every major artifact should have a status.

Use simple labels:

- ready for teacher review
- approved for student use
- needs revision
- export only
- archive

If the status is unclear, the artifact is not ready for the capstone packet yet.

<TakeawayStrip tone="teacher">
  A good packet does not show everything AI or the teacher tried. It shows the version the teacher is willing to review and improve.
</TakeawayStrip>

## Classroom scenario

A teacher has twenty files from the build process: prompts, lesson drafts, rubrics, exports, source notes, screenshots, and old versions.

Putting every file into the packet would technically show the work, but it would make review harder. The packet needs the materials another teacher can actually inspect and improve.

## Weak version

```text
Final packet:
- all AI prompts
- all drafts
- old rubric
- new rubric
- LMS copy
- screenshots
- student lesson
- notes from planning
```

This packet is full, but not clear. A reviewer has to guess what is current, what is private, and what students should see.

## Better version

```text
Final packet:
- overview and status
- approved learning target and lesson map
- student-facing lesson
- assignment or lab packet
- rubric or success criteria
- feedback prompts
- verification checklists
- source and safety notes
- publishing note
- revision plan
```

This packet shows the teachable version and the evidence behind it.

## Build step

Create a packet inventory with three columns:

```text
Artifact | Status | Include in packet?
```

Use simple status labels:

- approved for student use
- ready for teacher review
- needs revision
- teacher-only
- archive
- exclude

Add one more column if local tool, access, source, platform, or safety context matters:

```text
Artifact | Status | Include in packet? | Local context needed?
```

Use that column only for tool, platform, access, source, or safety notes that help a reviewer understand the packet. Do not make the OTS-101 packet depend on a separate inactive course or a private setup document.

## Quality check

Before adding an artifact, ask:

- Does this help another teacher understand the course segment?
- Is it the current version?
- Is it labeled teacher-facing or student-facing?
- Does it avoid private student information?
- Does it show a real review decision?
- Would including it make the packet clearer or noisier?

## Safety and source note

Do not include screenshots, copied source material, or student examples unless they are safe to share in the packet context.

If an artifact is useful but risky, summarize the decision instead of including the raw material.

## Reflection

Which artifact in your current folder looks official but should not be trusted yet?

Rename it, archive it, or leave it out of the packet.

## Capstone connection

The packet is the capstone evidence for OTS-101. It should show that the teacher can build with AI, verify the result, and keep student-facing material separate from drafts and private notes.
