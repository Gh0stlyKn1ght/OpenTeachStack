## Why this matters

In the first chapter, many teachers build the whole lesson sequence and realize too late that the class has no clear "start point."

This lesson makes the **Chapter Plan** concrete so a teacher can begin the next one or two lessons on Monday without rewriting everything during prep time.

## Classroom scenario

A ninth-grade robotics teacher plans a one-week sequence but the team keeps changing the order each Friday.
Students sit through strong lessons, then lose track of what they are expected to produce.
Their artifacts are scattered across folders, and no one can agree on what "done" means.

This lesson gives the team one practical fix before the first release draft goes into the course content system.

## Core idea

A good chapter plan is small, testable, and reusable.
It tells everyone what the teacher will do, what students will produce, and how the result will be reviewed before students see it.

In this lesson, the goal is to align **Chapter Plan** to one real learning pressure point, one artifact, and one revision date.

## Weak example

Weak, and hard to trust:

> "Build a chapter about teaching routines and make sure students complete all activities."

This sounds supportive, but no one can tell who owns the plan, what success looks like, or which part needs correction before Monday.

## Better example

Better, and reusable:

> "For this section, we will teach one routine: **planning a simple investigation in small groups**.  
> One objective: "Students can use one shared template to draft a 3-step plan in 12 minutes."  
> One student-facing artifact: one shared notes board with initials only.  
> One review trigger: peer-review at 4:00 PM before lesson launch."

This version identifies audience, ownership, and a specific checkpoint.

## Teacher action (10-30 minutes)

1. Pick one learner outcome for this chapter and write it as a one-sentence promise: what a student can do at the end.
2. List the three artifacts students must touch:
   - One planning artifact (for example: shared notes template)
   - One revision artifact (for example: checklist or tracker)
   - One review artifact (for example: peer feedback note)
3. Write one **revision trigger**: date, time, and person responsible for approval.
4. Add one accessibility boundary:
   - ensure headings and labels are screen-reader-friendly and time estimates are clear
5. Add one privacy/safety boundary:
   - no full names, no personal contact details, no class pictures unless school policy permits sharing.
6. Save the Chapter Plan in your course content system and ask one colleague to sign off before the next lesson.

- Review owner:
- What counts as revision-needed:

## Quality check

- Can a substitute or partner teacher pick up this chapter plan and teach it in one pass?
- Is there exactly one clear student-facing artifact and one clear review artifact?
- Is the revision trigger specific (day, time, owner)?
- Does the artifact include both accessibility and privacy boundaries?
- Can this plan be inspected from the course content system without asking the creator for context?

## Privacy, safety, and accessibility note

Place this sentence in your working copy:

- "Student work links in the shared folder are visible to the class team only, and all names are initials until parent/guardian permission is confirmed."

## Reflection question

What single checkpoint in this Chapter Plan would prevent an avoidable confusion you have seen in class this month?
