# The Scattered Files Problem

The scattered files problem starts when every lesson decision lives in a different place. A prompt is in one chat. A source link is in a browser tab. The slide deck is in Drive. The rubric is copied from last year. The revision note is in your head. Nothing is technically lost, but the workflow becomes expensive because every future use requires rediscovery.

For a teacher-builder, file organization is not just housekeeping. It is instructional infrastructure. The question is not "Did I save the file?" The question is "Could I or another teacher understand what this was for, what source shaped it, what changed, and what should happen next?"

## Why Scattered Files Happen

Scattered files usually come from normal teacher pressure:

- A useful idea appears while you are grading, not while you are organizing.
- A copy of a copy feels faster than opening the source file.
- A resource gets downloaded because the original page might disappear.
- A colleague sends a helpful document, but it lands outside the course folder.
- An AI draft seems temporary, so it never gets named or reviewed properly.

None of these are character flaws. They are workflow signals. The system needs a place for active drafts, source notes, finished student-facing materials, and after-teaching revisions.

## Do This

Pick one unit or recurring lesson workflow and list the places where its materials currently live. Include Drive folders, local downloads, LMS pages, AI chats, email attachments, shared docs, browser bookmarks, and handwritten notes.

Then sort each item into one of four buckets:

- Source: original references, standards, examples, and citations.
- Draft: teacher-only planning, AI drafts, rough notes, and experiments.
- Teach: student-facing directions, slides, handouts, links, and media.
- Improve: reflections, student confusion notes, revisions, and next-year changes.

## Evidence of Completion

You should have a short map of one messy workflow and at least three concrete cleanup decisions. Good decisions are small and reusable: rename a folder, move source links into one note, separate teacher drafts from student-facing files, or create a revision log next to the lesson.

## Verification Check

Before keeping anything in a course folder, check that it does not include student-identifiable information, private account details, or copied material you do not have permission to reuse.
