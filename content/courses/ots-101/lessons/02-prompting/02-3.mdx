# Bad Prompts vs Useful Prompts

## Why This Matters

A teacher building a first reusable mini-unit needs a system another educator can inspect. Bad prompts hide the teacher's intent. Useful prompts make the intent, constraints, and review criteria visible.

A bad prompt is not always short. A long prompt can still be bad if it asks the tool to make decisions the teacher has not made.

## Compare The Prompts

Bad:

> Create a fun sensor lesson.

Why it fails:

- "Fun" is not a learning target.
- The grade level is missing.
- The student product is missing.
- The source or standard is missing.
- The teacher review step is missing.

Useful:

> Act as a grade 8 robotics planning assistant. Draft a 45-minute activity where teams collect five ultrasonic sensor readings in centimeters, choose a robot stop threshold, and justify the threshold with evidence. Use plain language. Include a materials list, student directions, a data table, and a teacher verification checklist. Do not invent standards or include private student information.

Why it works:

- The role is clear.
- The student action is visible.
- The evidence is named.
- The output format is specific.
- The teacher still verifies the result.

## Practice

Rewrite this prompt:

> Make a rubric for my project.

Ask what is missing:

- What project?
- What grade?
- What product?
- What criteria?
- How many levels?
- What should the rubric avoid?
- What must the teacher verify?
