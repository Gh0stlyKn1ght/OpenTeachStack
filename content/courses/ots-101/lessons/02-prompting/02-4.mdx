# Prompting for Lesson Plans

## Why This Matters

A teacher building a first reusable mini-unit needs a system another educator can inspect. Lesson-plan prompts are useful only when they begin with teacher decisions: learning target, student task, materials, time, evidence, and review criteria.

If the prompt asks AI to create the whole lesson from nothing, the response may sound finished while hiding weak alignment.

## A Safer Lesson-Plan Prompt

Use this structure:

> Act as a curriculum planning assistant. I am teaching [grade/course]. The learning target is [target]. Students have [time] and will use [materials]. Draft a lesson outline with launch, work time, check for understanding, and close. The student product should be [evidence]. Use plain student-facing language. Do not invent standards, sources, or facts. End with a teacher verification checklist.

Robotics example:

> Act as a curriculum planning assistant for grade 8 robotics. The learning target is "I can use sensor data to justify a design decision." Students have 45 minutes and will use ultrasonic sensors and a shared data table. Draft a lesson outline with launch, work time, check for understanding, and close. The student product is a data table and a two-sentence threshold justification. Do not invent standards, sources, or facts. End with a teacher verification checklist.

## Teacher Review

After AI drafts the outline, review it for:

- realistic timing,
- accurate materials,
- student-readable directions,
- evidence aligned to the target,
- accessibility and safety needs,
- missing teacher setup steps.
