# checkpoint review

## Why This Matters

A teacher building a first reusable mini-unit needs more than a tool list or a checklist. The goal in **Prompting for Teachers** is to make a classroom system that can be used, inspected, revised, and handed to another educator without needing a meeting first.

For **checkpoint review**, the practical pressure is simple: accepting a polished lesson draft before the sources, student task, assessment evidence, and classroom routine are inspectable. This page turns that pressure into a visible update to **Reusable prompt library**.

## Classroom Scenario

Use this situation as the working example: an ultrasonic sensor mini-unit where students collect readings, explain uncertainty, revise a threshold, and submit a short design reflection. By the end, the work should support this habit: source-backed planning, plain-language decisions, and visible revision notes.

In this scenario, the learner should be able to point to:

- what the teacher prepares before students see the work,
- what students will read, build, submit, or discuss,
- what evidence shows the work is ready,
- what gets revised after the first use.

## Key Idea

A useful prompt is a work order, not a wish. It names the learner, task, source material, output format, constraints, review criteria, and what the teacher will verify.

Watch for this mistake: Asking for a complete lesson without giving the standard, materials, student product, time limit, or review rule.

## What You Will Do

Make this page concrete by writing three small decisions:

- Before class, connect checkpoint review to one setup decision in the reusable prompt library: what the teacher prepares, where it lives, and which source or safety check keeps it honest.
- During class, connect checkpoint review to one student action in middle school robotics and technology: what learners read, build, submit, discuss, or revise while working through an ultrasonic sensor mini-unit where students collect readings, explain uncertainty, revise a threshold, and submit a short design reflection.
- After class, review checkpoint review through the prompting for teachers lens: what evidence proves the move worked, which mistake to avoid (asking for a complete lesson without giving the standard, materials, student product, time limit, or review rule), and what revision supports source-backed planning, plain-language decisions, and visible revision notes.

## What Good Work Looks Like

A strong **checkpoint review** entry is concrete enough that another teacher can inspect it. It names the class context, the artifact location, the student product, the source or safety check, and the revision trigger.

For this lesson, a strong entry might say:

> Prompt entry: Draft student directions for a 45-minute sensor data table. Use grade 8 language, include three readings, one reliability question, and a note that the teacher checks setup safety before class.

That level of detail is enough to review. It is also small enough to revise after one classroom use.

## Quality Check

- The next revision has an owner and a realistic date or trigger.
