## What this chapter is really about

> OTS-101 frame: AI supports teacher-approved course content that becomes student-facing only after review.

AI can draft a lesson before the teacher knows what the lesson is supposed to prove.

That is the problem.

A standard, district goal, or rough teaching idea is not a lesson yet. It becomes a lesson when the teacher can name the student target, the practice path, and the evidence students will produce.

## The sequence we use

> OTS-101 frame: AI supports teacher-approved course content that becomes student-facing only after review.

Do not start with the tool.

Start with the target.

## A real classroom version

> OTS-101 frame: AI supports teacher-approved course content that becomes student-facing only after review.

The robotics teacher starts with a broad need:

> Students need to stop rushing robot startup.

That is real, but it is not yet a lesson.

A stronger target is:

> Students can choose the next safe action in a robot startup scenario and explain which checklist step supports that choice.

Now the teacher can build a lesson around scenario practice, not a generic safety lecture.

The map connects:

| Piece | What it answers |
| --- | --- |
| Source goal | Where did the learning need come from? |
| Student target | What should students be able to do? |
| Practice | How will students rehearse the skill? |
| Evidence | What will students produce? |
| AI support | What can AI draft safely? |
| Teacher verification | What must the teacher check? |

## What to watch for

> OTS-101 frame: AI supports teacher-approved course content that becomes student-facing only after review.

The trap is asking AI for the final lesson too early.

If the target is vague, the AI output will probably be vague too.

A better move is to ask AI for options only after the teacher names the target and evidence.
