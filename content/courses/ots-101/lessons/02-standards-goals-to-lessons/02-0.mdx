## Chapter Purpose

Teachers often start with standards, district language, old unit goals, or a classroom problem that needs attention.

AI can turn any of that into a lesson-looking page in seconds.

That is useful only after the teacher has named what students should actually do, what practice they need, and what evidence will prove the learning happened.

Chapter 2 is about slowing down at that exact point. Before AI drafts the student-facing lesson, the teacher builds a small map that connects source goal, student target, practice, evidence, AI support, and teacher verification.

Use your OTS-000 Teacher Builder Starter Kit as background support here. It can help you find the real files, links, platform constraints, and safety notes around the unit, but this chapter is not about the tools. It is about the instructional decision those tools should serve.

## The Teacher Problem

The pressure is real. A teacher needs something for tomorrow, the standard is broad, and AI is ready to produce slides, a quiz, a handout, and an exit ticket.

The problem is that a full set of materials can hide a missing instructional decision.

If the teacher cannot say what students should be able to do by the end of the lesson, the AI draft may only decorate the confusion.

## The Idea

A standard or rough goal becomes useful course content when it is translated into three classroom decisions:

- the student-friendly target
- the practice students need before assessment
- the evidence the teacher will inspect

Everything else comes after that.

## Classroom Scenario

A robotics teacher starts with a rough need:

> Students keep rushing robot startup.

That is a real classroom problem, but it is not yet a lesson.

The teacher turns it into a target:

> Students can choose the next safe action in a robot startup scenario and explain which checklist step supports that choice.

Now the lesson has a shape. Students need scenario practice, a visible checklist, and an explanation the teacher can inspect.

## Weak Version

Weak lesson direction:

> Teach robot startup safety.

That may produce slides, reminders, or a quiz, but it does not say what students will do with the safety idea.

## Better Version

Better lesson direction:

> Students use the approved startup checklist to choose the next safe action in two fictional scenarios and explain the checklist step that supports each choice.

That version gives the teacher a target, a practice path, and evidence.

## What You Will Build

You will build a learning target and lesson map with these fields:

| Map field | What it prevents |
| --- | --- |
| Source goal | AI inventing the lesson purpose |
| Student-friendly target | vague student directions |
| Practice path | disconnected activities |
| Evidence | busy work with no proof |
| AI support | over-broad prompting |
| Teacher verification | unsafe or unreviewed publishing |
| Revision trigger | calling the first draft final |

## Lessons in This Chapter

1. Start with the learning target, not the tool.
2. Translate standards or goals into student-friendly outcomes.
3. Turn messy classroom problems into teachable sequences.
4. Build the learning target and lesson map.
5. Check whether a student can understand what they are doing.

## Exit Criteria

Before moving to Chapter 3, you should have:

- one completed learning target and lesson map
- one student-friendly target that names visible action
- one practice path that matches the target
- one evidence statement the teacher can inspect
- one narrow AI support task
- one teacher verification note

## Safety / Accessibility / Source Note

If the source goal comes from a required standard, district document, safety rule, or policy, do not ask AI to invent or reinterpret it. Provide only material you are allowed to use, then verify the target against the source before students see it.

## Reflection

Where are you most tempted to let AI skip the decision: target, practice, evidence, or verification?

## Capstone Connection

This map becomes one of the first pieces in the final Mini Course Content Packet. It proves the student-facing lesson came from a teacher-owned instructional decision, not from a blank prompt.

It also keeps OTS-101 in the right lane after OTS-000: the tool stack may help you locate and organize the work, but the learning target tells the work what to do.

## Scan Break

Use this chapter opener as a quick map:

- Standards tell you what the course must respect.
- Goals translate that pressure into teacher-readable direction.
- Lessons turn the direction into something students can actually do.

If one of those pieces is missing, the chapter is not ready to carry a real course build.
