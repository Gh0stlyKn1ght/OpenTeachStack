## The Teacher Problem

The tool is loud.

AI makes it easy to ask for the visible thing first:

- make me slides
- make me a worksheet
- make me a quiz
- make me a lesson
- make me a rubric

Those requests can produce useful drafts, but they can also produce material that does not prove the learning target.

## The Idea

A learning target names what students should be able to do by the end of the lesson.

It should be clear enough that:

- students understand the goal
- the teacher can plan practice
- the assignment can collect evidence
- the rubric can score the right thing
- AI can draft a narrow support piece

The target decides the tool. The tool should not decide the target.

In OTS-101, that matters because AI-supported course content eventually becomes student-facing work. The teacher has to name the learning before asking for the artifact.

## Concrete Example

Classroom scenario:

A robotics teacher needs a quick lesson for startup safety.

The teacher could ask:

> Create a quiz about robot startup safety.

That request may produce ten reasonable-looking questions. It still might miss the point.

If the real target is safe decision-making during startup, a quiz about vocabulary is not enough.

## Common Misunderstanding

The mistake is treating the requested tool as the instructional decision.

Slides, worksheets, quizzes, and rubrics are containers. They are useful only when the learning target tells them what evidence to collect.

## Weak Version

Tool-first request:

> Make me a worksheet about safe robot startup.

This asks for a format before naming the learning.

## Better Version

Target-first request:

> My learning target is: students can choose the next safe action in a robot startup scenario and explain which checklist step supports that choice. Suggest one practice activity and one quick check that would produce evidence of that target. Do not invent safety rules.

This gives AI a lane and gives the teacher a way to reject output that drifts.

## Build Step

Take one planned AI request and rewrite it before you prompt.

| Tool-first request | Learning target | Better AI task |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Make a quiz about robot startup safety. | Students choose the next safe startup action using the approved checklist. | Draft two fictional scenarios and one student explanation prompt. |
| Make slides about source credibility. | Students decide whether a source supports a claim. | Draft one comparison activity using the two sources I provide. |
| Make a rubric for the project. | Students explain a design choice using evidence from constraints. | Draft one rubric row for evidence-based design explanation. |

## Quality Check

Your revised request is ready when it answers:

- What should students be able to do?
- What evidence will show that they can do it?
- What single piece may AI draft?
- What must the teacher verify?

If you cannot answer those questions, do not ask AI for the lesson yet.

## Safety / Accessibility / Source Note

When the target involves safe physical action, student privacy, grading, disability supports, or school policy, AI may help draft wording. It must not decide the rule.

Use your approved source, and mark anything that needs human confirmation.

## Reflection

Which tool do you usually ask for first when you are under pressure? What target should come before that request?
