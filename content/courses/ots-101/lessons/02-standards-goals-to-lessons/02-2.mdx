## The Teacher Problem

Standards are usually written for adults.

They help with alignment, planning, and accountability, but they do not always tell students what to do today.

If a teacher drops adult-facing wording straight into a lesson page, students may see official language without a clear action.

## The Idea

Student-friendly outcomes preserve the standard's intent while naming visible student work.

A useful outcome includes:

1. an action students can perform
2. the concept, source, tool, or process they use
3. evidence the teacher can review

The goal is not to make the standard cute. The goal is to make the learning usable.

For this course, the teacher is preparing AI-supported course content, so the adult source has to become a target students can actually use.

## Classroom Scenario

A CTE robotics standard says students should demonstrate safe operating procedures.

Weak student-facing target:

> I can understand robot safety.

Better student-facing target:

> I can choose the next safe startup step in a scenario and explain which checklist item supports my choice.

The second target keeps the safety intent, but it also names the student action and the evidence.

## Common Misunderstanding

Student-friendly does not mean vague, cute, or watered down.

The outcome can use plain language and still stay demanding. In fact, the plainer wording should make the required student action easier to inspect.

## Weak Version

| Source wording | Weak student outcome | Why it fails |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Demonstrate safe operation procedures. | I can understand safety. | Understanding is invisible unless students do something with it. |
| Evaluate information for credibility. | I can learn about good sources. | It names a topic, not a judgment students make. |
| Communicate technical reasoning. | I can explain my work. | It does not say what counts as evidence or reasoning. |

## Better Version

| Source wording | Better student outcome | Evidence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Demonstrate safe operation procedures. | I can choose the next safe startup step using the checklist. | Scenario choice plus checklist explanation |
| Evaluate information for credibility. | I can decide whether a source supports an AI-generated claim. | Source decision with quoted evidence |
| Communicate technical reasoning. | I can explain one design choice using a constraint from the task. | Two-sentence design justification |

## Build Step

Choose one standard, goal, or required outcome.

Fill this table:

| Source wording | Student action | Concept or tool | Evidence | Student-friendly outcome |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
|  |  |  |  |  |

Then ask AI for two alternate phrasings only after your table is filled.

If the source wording is tied to a district document, approved standard, safety rule, or course requirement, add where the teacher will verify it. AI can help phrase the student-friendly target, but it should not become the alignment authority.

## Quick Check

Read the student-friendly outcome out loud.

If a student could not tell what to do, or you could not tell what evidence to collect, revise it before asking AI for support materials.

## Quality Check

Keep the outcome only if:

- it preserves the source meaning
- students can understand the action
- the action can be practiced
- the evidence can be inspected
- AI did not invent alignment that the teacher has not verified
- the verification source is named when the outcome depends on required language

## Safety / Accessibility / Source Note

Do not paste private standards documents or district-only curriculum language into a public AI tool unless your school has approved that use. If needed, paraphrase the allowed part and verify the final target against the original source yourself.
