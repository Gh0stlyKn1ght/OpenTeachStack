## The Teacher Problem

Not every lesson starts from a clean standard.

Sometimes the starting point is messier:

- students keep skipping setup steps
- the last lab was chaotic
- students copy AI output without checking it
- the rubric does not match the assignment
- students can do the task live but cannot explain it

Those are real teaching signals. They still need to become a sequence.

## The Idea

A messy classroom problem can become a teachable sequence when the teacher names:

1. the problem students are having
2. the target the problem points toward
3. the practice students need before assessment
4. the evidence that will prove progress
5. the part AI may draft
6. the part the teacher must verify

The point is to prepare student-facing course content that AI can help draft later, not to turn a frustration into a polished but unfocused lesson.

## Classroom Scenario

Messy idea:

> Students keep rushing robot startup.

Better diagnosis:

> Students are treating the checklist as a poster, not as a decision tool.

Teachable target:

> Students can choose the next safe startup action in a scenario and explain the checklist step that supports it.

Now the lesson can focus on scenario decisions instead of a general safety talk.

## Weak Version

Weak sequence:

1. Talk about safety.
2. Show a video.
3. Give a quiz.

This may fill the period, but it does not create a clear path from the classroom problem to evidence.

## Better Version

Better sequence:

1. Show one fictional startup scenario.
2. Ask students to choose the next safe action.
3. Have students cite the checklist step that supports the choice.
4. Practice the routine with powered-off equipment.
5. Collect one written explanation before live equipment use.

The sequence now matches the problem.

## Common Misunderstanding

The classroom problem is not automatically the lesson target.

"Students rush startup" is a signal. The teachable target is the smaller skill inside the signal: choosing the next safe action and explaining why.

## Build Step

Turn one messy classroom signal into a teachable sequence.

```txt
Messy idea:
What students are actually struggling with:
Student-friendly target:
Practice before assessment:
Evidence students produce:
AI may draft:
Teacher must verify:
```

Example:

| Field | Filled example |
| --- | --- |
| Messy idea | Students rush startup. |
| Struggle | They skip decision points in the checklist. |
| Target | Choose next safe action and cite the checklist. |
| Practice | Two fictional scenarios, then powered-off rehearsal. |
| Evidence | Scenario choices plus short explanation. |
| AI may draft | Fictional scenarios. |
| Teacher must verify | Checklist accuracy and equipment safety. |

## Quick Check

Trace the sequence in one sentence:

> Because students are struggling with `___`, they will practice `___`, and I will collect `___` as evidence.

If the three blanks do not line up, the lesson is not ready for AI drafting yet.

## Quality Check

Your sequence is ready for AI support when:

- the target names a visible student action
- practice comes before assessment
- evidence matches the target
- AI is asked for one bounded draft
- the teacher verification step is named

## Safety / Accessibility / Source Note

For physical safety, lab, tool, account, privacy, or policy-related lessons, use fictional scenarios unless approved local material is safe to share. AI can draft practice cases, but the teacher checks the actual rule.
