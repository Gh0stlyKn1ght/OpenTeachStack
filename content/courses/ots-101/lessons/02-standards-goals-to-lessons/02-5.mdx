## What You Are Checking

You are checking whether the learning target and lesson map can support student-facing course content.

Teachers can understand their own intent even when a page is unclear. Students cannot read the teacher's mind.

This checkpoint asks you to read the map as if you are a student opening the lesson without a live explanation.

## Classroom Scenario

A teacher has a solid map in their planning notes:

> Students choose the next safe startup action and cite the checklist.

That is useful for the teacher, but the student-facing lesson still has to answer the student version of the question:

> What do I do first, what source do I use, and what do I turn in?

The checkpoint catches that translation gap before AI turns the map into a full lesson draft.

## Review Criteria

A student should be able to answer:

| Student question | Evidence in the map |
| --- | --- |
| What am I learning? | Student-friendly target |
| What am I doing first? | Practice path |
| What example or source should I use? | Scenario, source, or model plan |
| What do I submit or show? | Evidence |
| What does success look like? | Quality or rubric direction |
| What if I am stuck? | Support path or teacher note |

## Weak Version

Weak student view:

> Today we will learn about robot startup safety.

This is clear as a topic, but it does not tell students what action to take or what evidence they will produce.

## Better Version

Better student view:

> Today you will use the startup checklist to choose the next safe action in two fictional scenarios. For each scenario, write one sentence naming the checklist step that supports your choice.

Students can now see the action, source, evidence, and success condition.

## Self-Audit Checklist

- [ ] I can name the first student action.
- [ ] I can name the source, scenario, tool, or material students use.
- [ ] I can name what students submit, show, or explain.
- [ ] I can name what good work includes.
- [ ] I can name what the teacher must verify before students see it.

## Evidence to Save

Save:

- the completed learning target and lesson map
- one revision you made after the student-view audit
- one narrow AI prompt you can use in Chapter 3

## Next Step

Write the sentence a student should be able to say after reading the lesson target:

```txt
Today I am learning to __________ so I can __________.
```

If that sentence is fuzzy, revise the target before asking AI for a full lesson draft.

## Safety / Accessibility / Source Note

For equipment-related lessons, make the student-facing task clear about when students are reading, when they are practicing with powered-off materials, and when teacher supervision is required.

For reading-heavy lessons, check whether the target needs vocabulary support, chunked directions, or an alternate way to show evidence.

## Reflection

Where did the student-view audit expose a gap: target, action, evidence, success criteria, or safety boundary?

## Capstone Connection

Keep the revised map and the student-view sentence. They will help the final mini-course packet show that the lesson is teachable, not just planned.
