# What Not to Paste Into AI

## Why This Matters

A teacher building a first reusable mini-unit needs more than a tool list or a checklist. The goal in **AI Literacy and Verification** is to make a classroom system that can be used, inspected, revised, and handed to another educator without needing a meeting first.

For **What Not to Paste Into AI**, the practical pressure is simple: accepting a polished lesson draft before the sources, student task, assessment evidence, and classroom routine are inspectable. This page turns that pressure into a visible update to **AI verification checklist**.

## Classroom Scenario

Use this situation as the working example: an ultrasonic sensor mini-unit where students collect readings, explain uncertainty, revise a threshold, and submit a short design reflection. By the end, the work should support this habit: source-backed planning, plain-language decisions, and visible revision notes.

In this scenario, the learner should be able to point to:

- what the teacher prepares before students see the work,
- what students will read, build, submit, or discuss,
- what evidence shows the work is ready,
- what gets revised after the first use.

## Key Idea

AI output is usable only after the teacher checks the source, the claim, the classroom fit, and the student-facing consequence.

Watch for this mistake: Using AI language because it sounds confident before checking sources, classroom fit, privacy, and student evidence.

## What You Will Do

Make this page concrete by writing three small decisions:

- Before class, connect what not to paste into AI to one setup decision in the AI verification checklist: what the teacher prepares, where it lives, and which source or safety check keeps it honest.
- During class, connect what not to paste into AI to one student action in middle school robotics and technology: what learners read, build, submit, discuss, or revise while working through an ultrasonic sensor mini-unit where students collect readings, explain uncertainty, revise a threshold, and submit a short design reflection.
- After class, review what not to paste into AI through the AI literacy and verification lens: what evidence proves the move worked, which mistake to avoid (using AI language because it sounds confident before checking sources, classroom fit, privacy, and student evidence), and what revision supports source-backed planning, plain-language decisions, and visible revision notes.

## What Good Work Looks Like

A strong **what not to paste into AI** entry is concrete enough that another teacher can inspect it. It names the class context, the artifact location, the student product, the source or safety check, and the revision trigger.

For this lesson, a strong entry might say:

> Verification entry: AI suggested a sensor threshold activity; teacher checked the datasheet, removed an unsupported claim, and added a student data-check question.

That level of detail is enough to review. It is also small enough to revise after one classroom use.
