# Why Generic Prompts Create Fake Lessons

A generic prompt is tempting because it feels efficient.

You type one sentence. AI gives you a full page. The page has headings. The headings sound educational. There is probably even a closing reflection question.

But a finished-looking page is not the same thing as teachable course content.

## The classroom problem

Imagine a teacher types this:

> Create a lesson on digital citizenship for middle school students.

The AI returns:

- an objective about responsible technology use
- a short explanation of digital citizenship
- a group discussion
- a worksheet
- an exit ticket

At first glance, that looks fine.

The problem is what is missing.

No one knows what standard or skill the lesson is aligned to. No one knows whether students are supposed to identify, evaluate, create, compare, or reflect. No one knows what prior knowledge students have. No one knows what tools are available, what reading level is appropriate, or what the teacher will accept as evidence.

The AI filled the silence with generic school language.

## Why fake lessons are hard to spot

Fake lessons usually do not look absurd.

They look familiar.

They use phrases teachers recognize: engage students, facilitate discussion, check for understanding, encourage reflection. Those phrases can be useful when they are attached to a real decision. They become filler when they hide the absence of one.

A fake lesson often has three warning signs.

## Warning sign 1: The activity could fit any class

If the lesson could be pasted into any grade, any subject, and any room without changing much, it is probably not specific enough yet.

Real course content has fingerprints.

It carries the teacher's sequence, the students' current needs, the course vocabulary, and the evidence the teacher actually wants to see.

## Warning sign 2: The student task is vague

A fake lesson might tell students to discuss, explore, reflect, or analyze without saying what they should use, produce, or submit.

A student-facing task should answer:

- What am I doing?
- What am I using?
- What am I making or turning in?
- How will I know if I did it well?

## Warning sign 3: The teacher review step is missing

AI drafts need inspection.

If the prompt does not ask for a review checklist, revision notes, assumptions, or places where the AI needs teacher confirmation, the output may act more certain than it should.

That is how invented confidence slips into course materials.

## A better direction

Instead of asking AI to make the whole lesson, ask it to help with one bounded part of the lesson.

For example:

> Using this learning target and these teacher notes, draft three student-facing practice tasks. Each task should include directions, expected evidence, and one teacher review note. Do not invent standards or tools.

That prompt is not magical.

It is just more honest about the job.

<TakeawayStrip tone="warning">
  A generic prompt asks AI to pretend it knows your course. A useful prompt gives AI the course conditions it is allowed to work inside.
</TakeawayStrip>

## What Happened

A teacher uses AI to speed up Reusable course-content prompt, and the draft looks polished enough to move forward.

## What Looked Fine

The wording is smooth, the sections are present, and the material appears complete at a glance.

## What Was Actually Broken

The draft does not yet prove that students know what to do, that the teacher can verify the outcome, or that the content is safe to reuse.

## Root Cause

The artifact was judged by surface polish instead of instructional evidence.

## The Fix

Name the missing teaching decision, revise only that part, and save the before/after note so the improvement can be reviewed later.
