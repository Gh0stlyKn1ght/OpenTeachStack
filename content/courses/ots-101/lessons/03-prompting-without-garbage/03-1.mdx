## What Happened

A teacher is tired, behind, and needs something usable for tomorrow.

They type:

> Create a lesson on digital citizenship for middle school students.

AI returns:

- an objective about responsible technology use
- a short explanation
- a discussion activity
- a worksheet
- an exit ticket
- a reflection question

At first glance, it looks like a lesson.

## What Looked Fine

The output has school-shaped parts.

It has headings. It sounds positive. It uses familiar language like discuss, reflect, explore, and check for understanding.

That is why fake lessons are hard to spot. They usually do not look absurd. They look familiar.

## What Was Actually Broken

No one knows:

- what source or standard the lesson is anchored to
- what students already know
- what action students should be able to take
- what evidence the teacher will inspect
- what reading level or access needs matter
- what the teacher must verify before publishing

The AI filled silence with generic school language.

## Root Cause

The prompt asked AI to act like it knew the course.

It did not.

A generic prompt creates a generic lesson because the model has to invent the missing classroom conditions. The output may be smooth, but the teacher cannot tell whether it fits the actual course.

## The Fix

Ask AI for one bounded course content draft inside teacher-owned conditions.

Better prompt:

> Using this learning target and these teacher notes, draft three student-facing practice tasks. Each task should include directions, expected evidence, and one teacher review note. Do not invent standards, links, tools, or policy.

That prompt is not magic. It is honest about the job.

## Weak Version

Weak prompt:

```text
Make a lesson about online safety.
```

Why it fails:

- no source
- no grade or context
- no student action
- no evidence target
- no teacher check

## Better Version

Better prompt:

```text
Use the fictional scenarios and learning target below. Draft two student-facing practice tasks where students identify the unsafe online choice and explain a safer next step. Return a table with directions, expected evidence, and teacher-check notes. Do not use real student data or invent school policy.
```

Why it works:

- the source is bounded
- the student action is visible
- the evidence is inspectable
- the teacher review is required

## Rule We Keep

If the prompt could work for any class, it is not specific enough for your course yet.

## Build Step

Take one generic prompt you have used or might use.

Rewrite it with:

1. source
2. context
3. task
4. format
5. teacher check

## Quality Check

The revised prompt is stronger if a colleague can tell what AI is allowed to draft and what the teacher still owns.

## Safety / Accessibility / Source Note

Never use real student details to make a prompt feel specific. Specific does not mean private. Use fictionalized context, approved sources, and classroom constraints instead.

## Reflection

What part of your original prompt invited AI to pretend it knew more than it did?

## Capstone Connection

Save the before-and-after prompt. The final packet should show not just the polished prompt, but the decision that made it safer and more useful.
