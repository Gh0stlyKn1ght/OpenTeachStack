## The Confusion

Teachers often give AI directions but no model.

Then the output comes back polished, vague, and hard to fix.

That is familiar teaching work. Students also need examples and non-examples. AI does better when it can see the difference between output you would revise and output you do not want in the course.

## The Idea

A prompt pattern gives AI structure.

Examples give AI a standard.

Non-examples mark the edge of what you want to avoid.

When you are building student-facing course content, the contrast matters because AI will often imitate the surface of a sample before it understands the teaching purpose.

<FrameworkBlock title="Example vs non-example" eyebrow="Prompt calibration">
  <ConceptCard title="Example">
    A short model of output you would actually revise and use.
  </ConceptCard>
  <ConceptCard title="Non-example">
    A short model of generic, vague, inflated, unsafe, or unreviewable output.
  </ConceptCard>
  <ConceptCard title="Reason">
    A plain explanation of why one works and the other fails.
  </ConceptCard>
</FrameworkBlock>

## Side-by-Side Comparison

| Prompt ingredient | Weak version | Better version |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Student direction | Explore online safety and reflect on your choices. | Read Scenario A. Circle the unsafe choice, then write one safer next step. |
| Evidence | Be ready to discuss. | Submit one sentence naming the risk and one sentence naming the safer action. |
| Teacher check | Make it grade appropriate. | Flag any safety claim, policy assumption, or student privacy issue I must verify. |
| Course voice | engaging, thoughtful, meaningful | direct, concrete, student-action focused |

## Weak Version

Non-example to avoid:

> Students will engage in a meaningful exploration of online safety and reflect on how responsible choices shape their digital lives.

Why it fails:

- no source
- no action
- no product
- no success criteria
- no teacher verification

## Better Version

Example to preserve:

> Read the two fictional messages. Circle the message that asks for private information. Underline the clue that helped you decide. Then write one safer response the student could send.

Why it works:

- students know what to use
- students know what to do
- the teacher can inspect evidence
- the scenario avoids real student disclosure

## Decision Rule

Use examples when AI keeps drifting toward generic tone.

Use non-examples when AI keeps repeating a habit you want to stop.

Always explain why the example works. Do not assume the model will infer the teaching standard from the sample alone.

## Practice

Add this block to your reusable prompt:

```text
Example of acceptable output:
[Paste a short model]

Non-example to avoid:
[Paste a weak or generic model]

Preserve these features:
[Name the useful traits]

Avoid these habits:
[Name the weak traits]
```

## Quality Check

Your example/non-example pair is ready when the difference is visible without a long explanation.

If both samples sound like they could appear on the same worksheet, sharpen the contrast.

## Safety / Accessibility / Source Note

Use fictional examples when modeling privacy, safety, behavior, identity, family details, health, or student writing. A strong example should not require real student exposure.

## Reflection

What is one AI habit you want your non-example to stop: vague verbs, fake reflection, invented policy, too much text, or generic tone?

## Capstone Connection

Save one example/non-example pair with your reusable prompt. It gives another educator a visible standard for reviewing future AI output.
