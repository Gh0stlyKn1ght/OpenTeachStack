## The Teacher Problem

Teachers do not need one perfect mega-prompt.

They need a reusable structure that keeps AI from inventing the lesson behind their back.

This artifact helps you ask for one bounded piece of course content at a time and keeps the review trail visible.

This is not a tool-comparison exercise. OTS-000 already helped you name which AI tools, accounts, and access boundaries are safe enough to use. In OTS-101, the important question is whether the prompt produces a draft tied to your source, target, classroom context, and teacher review rules.

## The Artifact

Your reusable prompt should include:

- source
- context
- task
- format
- example
- non-example
- teacher-check section

## Build Step

1. Open the learning target and lesson map from Chapter 2.
2. Copy the student-friendly target into the prompt.
3. Add the approved source or a safe teacher-created summary.
4. Add classroom context: time, allowed classroom tools, prior learning, student needs, and constraints.
5. Ask for one artifact, not the whole lesson.
6. Choose a format that will be easy to inspect.
7. Add one example and one non-example.
8. Require a teacher-check section.

## Example Output

```text
Use the source material below as the ground truth. Do not invent standards, policies, tools, facts, student data, or requirements. If something is missing, list it in the teacher-check section instead of guessing.

Context:
- Course or grade: Middle school robotics
- Time available: 35 minutes
- What students already know: They have seen the startup checklist but skip steps when excited.
- Tools or materials students can use: Approved startup checklist, fictional scenarios, powered-off robot for practice
- Accessibility or language needs: Directions should be short and concrete.
- Constraints or things to avoid: Do not create new safety rules or ask students to use powered equipment without teacher supervision.

Task:
Draft three fictional startup scenarios that help students choose the next safe action and explain which checklist step supports it.

Format:
Return a table with these columns:
- Scenario
- Student task
- Expected evidence
- Teacher-check note

Example of acceptable output:
Read Scenario A. Choose the next safe action from the checklist. Write one sentence naming the checklist step that supports your choice.

Non-example to avoid:
Discuss why robot safety is important and reflect on responsible behavior.

Teacher-check section:
List anything I must verify before students see the draft, including safety accuracy, unclear assumptions, accessibility concerns, and missing source information.

Source material:
[Teacher adds approved checklist or safe summary here.]
```

## Weak Version

Weak reusable prompt:

> Make this lesson better for students.

That prompt gives AI too much control. It does not name the source, target, task, format, or verification rules.

## Better Version

Better reusable prompt:

> Use the target, source, and context below. Draft only the student-facing practice task. Return it in the requested format and end with teacher-check notes.

That prompt keeps the job small enough to review.

## Quality Check

Before keeping the prompt, confirm:

- [ ] The source is named.
- [ ] The classroom context is concrete.
- [ ] Any AI tool, account, or access boundary comes from your OTS-000 Starter Kit or local policy, not from the model guessing.
- [ ] The task asks for one artifact.
- [ ] The format makes review easier.
- [ ] The example and non-example are specific.
- [ ] The teacher-check section is required.
- [ ] The prompt does not expose private student information.

## Safety / Accessibility / Source Note

Keep private data out of the source section. If you need to describe student needs, describe the learning support without naming the student.

Example:

> Some students benefit from chunked directions and vocabulary support.

Do not paste private plans, grades, behavior notes, or identifiable student work.
