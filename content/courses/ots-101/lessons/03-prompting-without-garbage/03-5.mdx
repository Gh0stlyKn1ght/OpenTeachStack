# Checkpoint: Does the Output Sound Like Your Course?

> OTS-101 frame: AI supports teacher-approved course content that becomes student-facing only after review.

A prompt is only useful if the output survives teacher review.

This checkpoint helps you decide whether the AI draft belongs in your course, needs revision, or should be thrown away.

Do not ask, "Is this good?"

Ask, "Can I teach from this, and can students act on it?"

<FrameworkBlock title="The teacher-owned review" eyebrow="Checkpoint routine">
  <ConceptCard title="Fit">
    Does the draft match the learning target, sequence, students, time, and tools?
  </ConceptCard>
  <ConceptCard title="Clarity">
    Can a student tell what to do, what to use, what to make, and how quality will be judged?
  </ConceptCard>
  <ConceptCard title="Truth">
    Are facts, sources, standards, policies, and software instructions accurate or flagged for verification?
  </ConceptCard>
  <ConceptCard title="Voice">
    Does this sound like your course, or does it sound like generic education paste?
  </ConceptCard>
</FrameworkBlock>

## Review question 1: Does it match the target?

Look back at the learning target.

If the target asks students to compare, the draft should not only ask students to define. If the target asks students to build, the draft should not only ask students to discuss. If the target asks students to justify, the draft should require evidence.

The verb matters.

## Review question 2: Can students act on it?

Read the student-facing part as if you are a tired student opening the course page after lunch.

Can you tell:

- where to start?
- what material to use?
- what action to take?
- what to submit or show?
- what good work looks like?

If not, revise before publishing.

## Review question 3: What did AI assume?

Find the assumptions.

AI may assume tools exist, students know vocabulary, time is available, links are safe, sources are accurate, or a school policy allows something.

Those assumptions need teacher review.

## Review question 4: Is the voice too generic?

Generic course language often sounds like this:

> Students will engage in a meaningful exploration of the topic and reflect on their learning.

That sentence may sound pleasant, but it does not carry enough instructional weight.

Course voice should sound closer to this:

> Use the two examples from yesterday. Choose the stronger one, underline the evidence that makes it stronger, and write two sentences explaining your choice.

The second version tells students what to do.

## Decide the status

After review, give the draft one of three statuses.

## Keep

The draft is accurate, specific, aligned, and ready for light teacher editing.

## Revise

The draft has useful pieces, but needs clearer directions, better evidence, stronger examples, accessibility changes, or voice cleanup.

## Discard

The draft invented too much, missed the learning target, created unsafe or inaccurate material, or would take longer to fix than to rewrite.

## Final checkpoint prompt

Use this after AI gives you a draft:

```text
Review this draft against the learning target, student clarity, source accuracy, accessibility, privacy, and course voice. Identify what to keep, what to revise, what to discard, and what I must verify as the teacher.
```

<TakeawayStrip tone="teacher">
  The best sign of a good AI workflow is not that the first draft is perfect. It is that the teacher can quickly see what is usable, what is risky, and what needs revision.
</TakeawayStrip>
