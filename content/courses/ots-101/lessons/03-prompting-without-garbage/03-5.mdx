## What You Are Checking

A prompt is useful only if the output survives teacher review.

This checkpoint helps you decide whether the AI draft belongs in your student-facing course content, needs revision, or should be discarded.

Do not ask only:

> Is this good?

Ask:

> Can I teach from this, can students act on it, and can I verify the claims?

## Review Criteria

<FrameworkBlock title="The teacher-owned review" eyebrow="Checkpoint routine">
  <ConceptCard title="Fit">
    Does the draft match the learning target, sequence, students, time, and tools?
  </ConceptCard>
  <ConceptCard title="Clarity">
    Can a student tell what to do, what to use, what to make, and how quality will be judged?
  </ConceptCard>
  <ConceptCard title="Truth">
    Are facts, sources, standards, policies, and software instructions accurate or flagged for verification?
  </ConceptCard>
  <ConceptCard title="Voice">
    Does this sound like your course, or does it sound like generic education paste?
  </ConceptCard>
</FrameworkBlock>

## Weak Version

Weak AI output:

> Students will engage in a meaningful exploration of robot safety and reflect on responsible behavior.

It sounds pleasant, but students still do not know what source to use, what action to take, what evidence to submit, or what safety rule the teacher has verified.

## Better Version

Better AI output:

> Read Scenario A. Choose the next safe action from the approved startup checklist. Write one sentence naming the checklist step that supports your choice.

This sounds less fancy and works better. Students know the source, action, product, and success condition.

## Self-Audit Checklist

- [ ] The draft matches the learning target.
- [ ] Students can tell what to do first.
- [ ] Students know what source, example, or tool to use.
- [ ] Students know what to submit, show, or explain.
- [ ] The teacher can verify facts, sources, policy, safety, and accessibility.
- [ ] The draft sounds like this course, not a generic lesson shell.

## Decision Table

| Status | Use when | Next action |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Ready | The draft is accurate, specific, aligned, and needs only light teacher editing. | Save it with a review note. |
| Revise | Useful pieces exist, but clarity, evidence, accessibility, or voice needs work. | Prompt for a narrow revision or rewrite the weak part yourself. |
| Blocked | The draft invents facts, ignores the target, creates risk, or takes longer to fix than rewrite. | Discard it and return to the source and target. |

## Evidence to Save

Save:

- the reusable prompt
- the AI output
- one accepted / revised / rejected decision
- the teacher-check note
- the next review date or trigger

## Next Step

Use this review prompt after AI gives you a draft:

```text
Review this draft against the learning target, student clarity, source accuracy, accessibility, privacy, safety, and course voice. Identify what to keep, what to revise, what to discard, and what I must verify as the teacher.
```

If the review depends on tool access, account limits, district rules, or source links, pull those facts from your OTS-000 Starter Kit or approved documentation. Do not ask the AI draft to certify its own tool safety.

## Safety / Accessibility / Source Note

If the AI output includes links, safety claims, tool steps, citations, standards alignment, grading rules, or student accommodations, verify those before students see the draft.

## Reflection

Which status did your latest AI output earn: ready, revise, or blocked? What evidence supports that decision?

## Capstone Connection

Add the decision note to your Mini Course Content Packet. A strong packet shows how AI was reviewed, not just what AI produced.
