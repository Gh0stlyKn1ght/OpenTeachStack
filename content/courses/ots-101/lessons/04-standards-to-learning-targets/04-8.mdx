# checkpoint review

## Why This Matters

A teacher building a first reusable mini-unit needs more than a tool list or a checklist. The goal in **Standards to Learning Targets** is to make a classroom system that can be used, inspected, revised, and handed to another educator without needing a meeting first.

For **checkpoint review**, the practical pressure is simple: accepting a polished lesson draft before the sources, student task, assessment evidence, and classroom routine are inspectable. This page turns that pressure into a visible update to **Standards unpacking sheet**.

## Classroom Scenario

Use this situation as the working example: an ultrasonic sensor mini-unit where students collect readings, explain uncertainty, revise a threshold, and submit a short design reflection. By the end, the work should support this habit: source-backed planning, plain-language decisions, and visible revision notes.

In this scenario, the learner should be able to point to:

- what the teacher prepares before students see the work,
- what students will read, build, submit, or discuss,
- what evidence shows the work is ready,
- what gets revised after the first use.

## Key Idea

A standard becomes teachable only when the verb, concept, student evidence, and assessment move are visible.

Watch for this mistake: Copying a standard into the plan without naming what students will actually do or submit.

## What You Will Do

Make this page concrete by writing three small decisions:

- Before class, connect checkpoint review to one setup decision in the standards unpacking sheet: what the teacher prepares, where it lives, and which source or safety check keeps it honest.
- During class, connect checkpoint review to one student action in middle school robotics and technology: what learners read, build, submit, discuss, or revise while working through an ultrasonic sensor mini-unit where students collect readings, explain uncertainty, revise a threshold, and submit a short design reflection.
- After class, review checkpoint review through the standards to learning targets lens: what evidence proves the move worked, which mistake to avoid (copying a standard into the plan without naming what students will actually do or submit), and what revision supports source-backed planning, plain-language decisions, and visible revision notes.

## Build the Artifact

Inspect **Standards unpacking sheet** and mark what is ready, what needs revision, and what must be checked against sources, safety, privacy, or accessibility before use.

Add this to your artifact:

- Classroom context: middle school robotics and technology; an ultrasonic sensor mini-unit where students collect readings, explain uncertainty, revise a threshold, and submit a short design reflection.
- Artifact entry: In **Standards unpacking sheet**, add the decision for **checkpoint review** with the student-facing evidence and the review owner.
- Example entry: Target entry: Students compare two sensor readings, identify one reliability concern, and justify a threshold choice using their data table.
- Review check: Check the entry against standards, official documentation, source notes, privacy checks, and accessibility checks, then write what should change after the first classroom use.
- Peer review check: A colleague should be able to open the standards to learning targets artifact, understand the decision behind **checkpoint review**, and know the next action without extra explanation.

## What Good Work Looks Like

A strong **checkpoint review** entry is concrete enough that another teacher can inspect it. It names the class context, the artifact location, the student product, the source or safety check, and the revision trigger.

For this lesson, a strong entry might say:

> Target entry: Students compare two sensor readings, identify one reliability concern, and justify a threshold choice using their data table.

That level of detail is enough to review. It is also small enough to revise after one classroom use.

## Quality Check

Before moving on, confirm:

- Your artifact entry is stored in **Standards unpacking sheet** rather than kept as a private reminder.
- The student-facing task or evidence is named in plain language.
- The review step names a source, safety, privacy, accessibility, or maintenance check.
- The checkpoint distinguishes ready, revise, and blocked items.
- The next revision has an owner and a realistic date or trigger.

## Reflection

What would a busy teacher misunderstand if they only saw your **Standards unpacking sheet** entry and not your explanation? Revise the entry until the classroom task, evidence, review step, and next improvement are visible.
