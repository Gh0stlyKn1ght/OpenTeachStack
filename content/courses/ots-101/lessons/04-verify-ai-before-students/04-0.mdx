# Verify AI Before Students See It

> OTS-101 frame: AI supports teacher-approved course content that becomes student-facing only after review.

AI can help draft course content quickly.

That speed is useful, but it creates a new responsibility: teachers need a clear way to decide whether an AI-assisted draft is safe, accurate, useful, and ready for students.

This chapter is about that review step.

Not a vague reminder to "check the work."

## What Teachers Should Notice

The chapter is trying to make one teacher decision visible before any AI draft gets treated as finished.

A real verification routine.

<FrameworkBlock title="Before students see it" eyebrow="Verification flow">
  <ConceptCard title="Accuracy">
    Are facts, procedures, examples, standards, links, and tool instructions correct?
  </ConceptCard>
  <ConceptCard title="Privacy">
    Does the draft avoid real student data, sensitive context, and unnecessary personal information?
  </ConceptCard>
  <ConceptCard title="Accessibility">
    Can students understand, navigate, and complete the task with reasonable supports?
  </ConceptCard>
  <ConceptCard title="Classroom fit">
    Does the draft match the learning target, time, tools, student readiness, and teacher expectations?
  </ConceptCard>
</FrameworkBlock>

## Why this chapter matters

AI drafts often sound more certain than they are.

A draft may include a broken link, an invented citation, a misleading definition, an inaccessible activity, or instructions that do not fit the tools students actually have.

None of those problems are always obvious at first glance.

The teacher needs a repeatable way to inspect the draft before it becomes part of the course.

## What you will build

By the end of this chapter, you will have an AI draft verification checklist.

That checklist will help you decide:

- what must be checked against a source
- what must be revised for student clarity
- what must be removed for privacy or safety
- what must be adapted for accessibility
- what is ready enough to publish
- what should be discarded instead of repaired

## The chapter path

1. First, we separate AI drafting from teacher approval.
2. Then we learn how to check claims, links, and instructions.
3. Then we look at privacy and student-data boundaries.
4. Then we build the verification checklist.
5. Finally, we practice making a publish, revise, or discard decision.

<TakeawayStrip tone="teacher">
  Verification is not distrust for its own sake. It is how teachers keep ownership of the learning experience.
</TakeawayStrip>

## Chapter Purpose

This chapter gives teachers one usable way to handle verify ai before students without asking AI to invent the whole course. The point is to name the teaching decision first, then use AI only where it can support that decision.

## Lessons in This Chapter

- Start by naming what the teacher is actually building.
- Separate the learning plan from the student-facing material.
- Use AI for draft support, examples, and cleanup only after the teacher has named the target.
- Save one visible artifact so the chapter ends with evidence, not just reading.

## Exit Criteria

By the end of this chapter, the teacher should be able to point to AI draft verification checklist and explain what changed because of the chapter, what still needs review, and what is safe to reuse with students.
