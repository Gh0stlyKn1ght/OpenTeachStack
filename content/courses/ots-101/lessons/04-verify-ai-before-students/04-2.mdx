> OTS-101 frame: AI supports teacher-approved course content that becomes student-facing only after review.

AI can be useful and wrong at the same time.

That is especially true when a draft includes facts, links, citations, software instructions, or references to policies.

If students need to rely on it, the teacher needs to verify it.

## What counts as a claim?

A claim is anything in the draft that says or implies something is true.

Examples:

- a definition
- a historical fact
- a science explanation
- a math rule
- a legal or policy statement
- a citation
- a link
- a software step
- a safety instruction
- a claim about what a standard requires

Some claims are low risk. Others matter a lot.

The higher the risk, the stronger the source check should be.

## Links need a real click

If the AI gives a link, do not assume it works.

Check:

- Does the link open?
- Is it the intended site?
- Is it appropriate for students?
- Does it require an account?
- Does it collect student data?
- Is the page stable enough to use in a course?

If the link matters, use a trusted source instead of an AI-invented one.

## Citations need source matching

AI may produce citations that look real but do not support the sentence they are attached to.

For classroom use, the question is not only "does the citation exist?"

The better question is:

> Does the cited source actually support this student-facing claim?

If the answer is no, remove or rewrite the claim.

## Tool instructions need current documentation

Software instructions are fragile.

Menus change. Buttons move. Free features become paid features. District settings block options. Apps update.

If OTS-000 helped you collect tool notes or official documentation links, use those notes here. Do not let the AI draft become the source of truth for how a tool works in your classroom.

If the draft tells students how to use a tool, check the current tool or official documentation before publishing.

For example, do not publish:

> Click the Share With Class button.

unless that button actually exists for your students in your environment.

## Decide the source level

Not every claim needs the same level of checking.

Use this quick guide:

## Low risk

Common classroom language, teacher-created examples, or directions that do not depend on external facts.

Teacher review is usually enough.

## Medium risk

Definitions, course concepts, links, citations, or software steps students will use.

Check against course materials or trusted sources.

## High risk

Health, legal, safety, policy, student data, copyrighted material, or anything that could mislead students in a serious way.

Use authoritative sources and consider whether the content belongs in the activity at all.

<TakeawayStrip tone="teacher">
  Verification is not about checking every word with the same intensity. It is about matching the review effort to the risk.
</TakeawayStrip>

## Classroom Scenario

An AI draft tells students to use a website, follow a menu path, and cite a statistic. All three pieces sound official.

The teacher cannot approve the draft until the link opens, the menu path works in the student environment, and the statistic is supported by a trusted source.

## Weak Version

Weak verification move:

> The AI included a citation and a link, so it is probably fine.

That trusts the appearance of evidence instead of checking whether the evidence supports the lesson.

## Better Version

Better verification move:

> I clicked the link, checked the claim against the source, tested the tool steps in a student account, and removed the unsupported statistic.

That turns verification into visible teacher work.

## Build Step

Mark three claims in one AI draft:

1. one fact or definition
2. one link or citation
3. one tool step or instruction

For each claim, write the source you checked and the decision you made: keep, revise, remove, or verify later.

If you cannot check a tool step because you do not have the student account, device, or platform access yet, mark it as blocked. Do not call the draft verified.

## Quality Check

The check is strong when the draft no longer asks students to trust a claim the teacher has not checked.

## Safety / Accessibility / Source Note

Tool steps and links are accessibility issues too. If students cannot access the page, account, button, file, or captioned media, the instruction is not ready.

## Reflection

Which kind of claim do you tend to overlook because it sounds harmless: links, definitions, citations, tool steps, or policy language?

## Capstone Connection

Save one checked claim row in the verification checklist. It shows that the final packet was source-checked, not just proofread.
