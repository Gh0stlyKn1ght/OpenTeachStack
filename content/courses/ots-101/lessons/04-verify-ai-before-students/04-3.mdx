> OTS-101 frame: AI supports teacher-approved course content that becomes student-facing only after review.

Privacy is not only a technology issue.

It is a course-content issue.

Teachers need to think about privacy before prompting AI and before publishing AI-assisted materials to students.

## Why This Matters

Privacy mistakes usually happen before the lesson looks public: in the prompt, the copied screenshot, the example file, or the draft note that still carries student details.

## The basic rule

Do not paste private student data into AI tools unless your school or organization has explicitly approved that workflow.

When in doubt, do not include it.

Use fictionalized or generalized context instead.

## What not to paste

Avoid entering:

- student names
- student ID numbers
- grades
- behavior records
- disability information
- medical information
- family information
- private messages
- identifiable writing samples
- sensitive classroom incidents
- anything that could identify a student when combined with other details

Even if your intent is helpful, the data may not belong in the tool.

## Safer prompt examples

Risky:

```text
Here is Maria Lopez's essay and IEP notes. Rewrite the assignment for her reading level.
```

Safer:

```text
I have a student who benefits from shorter directions, vocabulary support, and chunked tasks. Rewrite this assignment with those supports, without lowering the learning target.
```

Risky:

```text
Here are screenshots of my class gradebook. Which students need intervention?
```

Safer:

```text
Create a general intervention planning template for a teacher reviewing assessment trends. Do not use real student data.
```

## Privacy also applies to publishing

Check the student-facing content before publishing.

Make sure examples, screenshots, filenames, comments, and links do not accidentally reveal private information.

A course page can expose data through small details:

- a screenshot with a student name in the corner
- a shared document title containing a real class period and student initials
- a copied example from an actual student submission
- feedback text pasted into a public sample
- a file path or link with private identifiers

## Build a privacy habit

Before using AI, ask:

> Can I describe the instructional need without identifying a student?

Before publishing, ask:

> Could a student, parent, or colleague identify someone from this material?

If the answer is yes, revise before moving forward.

<TakeawayStrip tone="warning">
  AI does not need private student data to help with most course-content tasks. Give it the teaching pattern, not the student's identity.
</TakeawayStrip>

## Classroom Scenario

A teacher wants AI to simplify directions for students who struggled with a lab. The risky version is to paste names, grades, reading levels, and behavior notes into the prompt.

The safer version is to describe the support pattern: shorter directions, vocabulary support, chunked steps, and an optional check-in point.

## Weak Version

Weak privacy move:

> Here are three student submissions and my notes about why each student struggled. Rewrite the task for them.

That exposes student work and private context to solve a problem that can usually be described without identities.

## Better Version

Better privacy move:

> Rewrite this assignment with shorter directions, vocabulary support, and a checkpoint after step two. Keep the learning target the same. Do not use real student data.

That gives AI the teaching pattern without exposing the student.

## Build Step

Before prompting, rewrite one private classroom detail as a safe teaching pattern.

| Private detail to avoid | Safe pattern to use |
| --- | --- |
| Student name or ID | "A student" or "some students" |
| Gradebook screenshot | summary of the skill trend |
| IEP or accommodation text | non-identifying support need |
| Real incident | fictionalized scenario |

## Quality Check

Your prompt is safer when a parent, student, or colleague could read it without identifying who the original situation was about.

## Safety / Accessibility / Source Note

Privacy and accessibility can work together. You can ask for chunking, vocabulary support, alternate response formats, or clearer directions without revealing protected student information.

## Reflection

What private detail are you tempted to include because it feels useful? How could you describe the teaching need without that detail?

## Capstone Connection

Add one privacy-safe prompt note to the verification checklist in your Mini Course Content Packet. It proves the course content was adapted without exposing students.
