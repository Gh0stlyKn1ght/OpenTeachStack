> OTS-101 frame: AI supports teacher-approved course content that becomes student-facing only after review.

Verification should end with a decision.

Not a feeling.

Not "looks good enough."

A decision.

## Stop Before Moving On

A checkpoint is useful only if it catches the weak spot before the next lesson depends on it.

For every AI-assisted draft, choose one of three statuses: publish after teacher edits, revise and review again, or discard and restart.

<FrameworkBlock title="Three review decisions" eyebrow="Checkpoint">
  <ConceptCard title="Publish">
    The content is accurate enough, clear enough, safe enough, and aligned enough for teacher-approved use.
  </ConceptCard>
  <ConceptCard title="Revise">
    The draft has useful pieces, but students should not see it until specific problems are fixed.
  </ConceptCard>
  <ConceptCard title="Discard">
    The draft is too inaccurate, generic, unsafe, misaligned, or time-consuming to repair.
  </ConceptCard>
</FrameworkBlock>

## Choose publish when...

Publish after teacher edits when:

- the learning target is clear
- student directions are actionable
- important claims have been checked
- links and tools work
- private student data is not exposed
- accessibility needs have been considered
- the teacher can explain why this belongs in the lesson

Publishing does not mean the draft was untouched.

It means the teacher-approved version is ready for students.

## Choose revise when...

Revise and review again when:

- the core idea is useful
- the structure can be saved
- the student task needs clearer directions
- examples need to be replaced
- the draft needs better source support
- accessibility or privacy issues are fixable
- the course voice needs cleanup

Revision is normal.

A good AI workflow often produces a rough draft worth improving.

## Choose discard when...

Discard and restart when:

- the draft invents too much
- the content is aligned to the wrong target
- the activity would confuse students
- the draft relies on unsafe or unsupported claims
- privacy problems are baked into the material
- fixing it would take longer than rewriting it
- the output is generic filler with no useful instructional core

Discarding a draft is not failure.

It is teacher judgment doing its job.

## The final decision note

Write one short note before moving on.

Use this format:

```text
Decision: Publish / Revise / Discard
Reason:
Next action:
```

Example:

```text
Decision: Revise
Reason: The activity matches the target, but the directions do not tell students what evidence to submit.
Next action: Add a student-facing evidence requirement and review the source citation.
```

## Exit check

Before leaving this chapter, make sure you have:

- a reusable verification checklist
- a clear boundary between AI draft and teacher-approved content
- a habit of checking claims, links, tool steps, privacy, and accessibility
- a publish, revise, or discard decision routine

<TakeawayStrip tone="teacher">
  The question is not whether AI helped. The question is whether the teacher-approved version is ready for students.
</TakeawayStrip>

## Classroom Scenario

A teacher reviews an AI-drafted assignment. The directions are clear, but one link is broken and one safety claim is unsupported.

That draft is not a failure. It is a revise decision.

## Weak Version

Weak decision note:

> Looks mostly okay.

That does not tell the teacher what to fix before publishing.

## Better Version

Better decision note:

> Decision: Revise. Reason: directions match the target, but the source link is broken and the safety claim needs verification. Next action: replace the link and check the claim against the approved safety document.

Now the next move is clear.

## Safety / Accessibility / Source Note

Choose discard, not revise, when the draft relies on private data, unsupported high-risk claims, inaccessible core materials, or a task that does not match the target.

## Reflection

What kind of AI output do you tend to over-revise when it would be faster and safer to discard?

## Capstone Connection

Keep one publish, revise, or discard note with your verification checklist. It shows teacher judgment in the final packet.
