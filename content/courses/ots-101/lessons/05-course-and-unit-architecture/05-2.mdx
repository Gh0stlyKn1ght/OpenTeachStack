# Essential Questions

## Why This Matters

A teacher building a first reusable mini-unit needs more than a tool list or a checklist. The goal in **Course and Unit Architecture** is to make a classroom system that can be used, inspected, revised, and handed to another educator without needing a meeting first.

For **Essential Questions**, the practical pressure is simple: accepting a polished lesson draft before the sources, student task, assessment evidence, and classroom routine are inspectable. This page turns that pressure into a visible update to **Mini-unit map and lesson template**.

## Classroom Scenario

Use this situation as the working example: an ultrasonic sensor mini-unit where students collect readings, explain uncertainty, revise a threshold, and submit a short design reflection. By the end, the work should support this habit: source-backed planning, plain-language decisions, and visible revision notes.

In this scenario, the learner should be able to point to:

- what the teacher prepares before students see the work,
- what students will read, build, submit, or discuss,
- what evidence shows the work is ready,
- what gets revised after the first use.

## Key Idea

A strong teaching system turns a good idea into a visible action, artifact, review step, and revision decision.

Watch for this mistake: Keeping the idea in conversation instead of writing the evidence another teacher can inspect.

## What You Will Do

Make this page concrete by writing three small decisions:

- Before class, connect essential questions to one setup decision in the mini-unit map and lesson template: what the teacher prepares, where it lives, and which source or safety check keeps it honest.
- During class, connect essential questions to one student action in middle school robotics and technology: what learners read, build, submit, discuss, or revise while working through an ultrasonic sensor mini-unit where students collect readings, explain uncertainty, revise a threshold, and submit a short design reflection.
- After class, review essential questions through the course and unit architecture lens: what evidence proves the move worked, which mistake to avoid (keeping the idea in conversation instead of writing the evidence another teacher can inspect), and what revision supports source-backed planning, plain-language decisions, and visible revision notes.

## What Good Work Looks Like

A strong **essential questions** entry is concrete enough that another teacher can inspect it. It names the class context, the artifact location, the student product, the source or safety check, and the revision trigger.

For this lesson, a strong entry might say:

> Artifact entry: one classroom task, one student product, one review criterion, and one next revision are named together.

That level of detail is enough to review. It is also small enough to revise after one classroom use.

## Quality Check

Before moving on, confirm:

- Your artifact entry is stored in **Mini-unit map and lesson template** rather than kept as a private reminder.
- The student-facing task or evidence is named in plain language.
- The review step names a source, safety, privacy, accessibility, or maintenance check.
- The common mistake is avoided: Keeping the idea in conversation instead of writing the evidence another teacher can inspect.
- The next classroom use or revision is obvious.
