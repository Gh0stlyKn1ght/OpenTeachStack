Teacher notes can be messy.

They can be shorthand. They can say "review yesterday," "model one example," or "give students time to work." That is fine because the teacher knows what those notes mean.

Students do not.

Student-facing directions need to carry the action clearly enough that students can begin without the teacher translating every line out loud.

## Why This Matters

Students cannot act on teacher shorthand. A course page has to say the material, action, product, and success check clearly enough that the task survives without live narration.

## The common mistake

A teacher-facing plan might say:

> Students analyze examples and discuss what makes the stronger response better.

That sentence is useful for planning, but it is not enough for a student course page.

A student-facing version might say:

> Read Response A and Response B. Highlight one sentence in each response that uses evidence. Then answer: Which response is stronger, and what evidence makes it stronger?

The second version names the materials, action, and product.

## Teacher-facing language is still useful

Do not delete your planning language.

Keep it in teacher notes, lesson plans, or private planning sections.

Just do not mistake it for the directions students need.

Teacher-facing language helps with:

- pacing
- facilitation
- grouping
- discussion moves
- differentiation
- assessment notes
- reminders for the teacher

Student-facing language helps with:

- starting the task
- following steps
- using materials
- producing evidence
- checking quality
- submitting work

## Translation questions

When you see a teacher note, ask:

- What will students physically or digitally do?
- What source, example, tool, or material will they use?
- What will they write, build, choose, record, or submit?
- What does a strong response include?
- What should they do if they are unsure?

These questions turn planning into usable course content.

## Before and after

Teacher-facing note:

```text
Have students compare examples and discuss quality.
```

Student-facing direction:

```text
Open the two sample responses. Put a star next to the response you think is stronger. Underline one piece of evidence that supports your choice. Then write two sentences explaining why that response works better.
```

The student-facing version does not remove teacher judgment.

It makes the judgment visible enough for students to participate.

<TakeawayStrip tone="teacher">
  If a direction only makes sense because the teacher is standing there to explain it, it is not ready as course content yet.
</TakeawayStrip>

## Problem

Teacher Notes Are Not Student Directions matters because AI can make weak course work look complete. The teacher still has to decide what students should learn, what evidence counts, and what belongs in front of the class.

## Common Misunderstanding

The common mistake is thinking a polished AI draft means the curriculum decision is finished. A draft can be useful and still be unready for students.

## Concrete Example

Instead of asking AI to "make a lesson," the teacher names the learning target, the student task, and the evidence to check. AI can then help draft language inside those boundaries.
