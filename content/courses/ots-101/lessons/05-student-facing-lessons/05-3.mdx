> OTS-101 frame: AI supports teacher-approved course content that becomes student-facing only after review.

Students need more than directions.

They also need a picture of quality.

A clear example shows what successful work can look like. A non-example shows a common weak pattern to avoid. A success check helps students revise before submitting.

This is one of the easiest places for AI to help, as long as the teacher verifies the result.

## Why examples help

Examples reduce mystery.

A student might understand the words "support your claim with evidence" and still not know what that should look like in this course.

A model gives the student something concrete to compare against.

## Why non-examples help

Non-examples are powerful because they name the trap.

For example:

> Weak response: I think Response A is better because it is good and has more details.

This non-example is useful because it sounds like something a real student might write. It gives the teacher a chance to ask, "What is missing?"

Students can then see that the answer needs specific evidence, not just a general opinion.

Put the two responses side by side:

| Weak student response | Stronger student response | What changed |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Response A is better because it is good and has more details. | Response B is stronger because it uses the screen-time limit from the source to support the claim. | Names specific evidence from the source instead of a general opinion. |

## Why success checks help

A success check turns quality into action.

Instead of telling students to "write a strong answer," the check names the features.

Example:

```text
Before submitting, check that your response:
- names your claim
- includes one piece of evidence from the source
- explains how the evidence supports the claim
- uses complete sentences
```

That checklist gives students a path to revise.

## Ask AI for supports carefully

AI can draft examples and non-examples, but the teacher must inspect them.

Ask for exactly what you need:

```text
Using this learning target and student task, draft one strong example, one realistic non-example, and one four-item student success check. The non-example should be plausible but clearly missing one important quality. Do not invent facts beyond the source.
```

Then review:

- Does the example actually meet the target?
- Is the non-example useful without being silly?
- Does the success check match the task?
- Are facts and source details accurate?
- Is the language appropriate for students?

## Keep supports short

Examples do not need to be long.

A model that is too long can overwhelm students or invite copying.

Aim for the smallest example that makes the quality visible.

## Put it on the lesson page

A student-facing lesson can include:

```text
Strong example:
[short model]

Watch out for:
[short non-example or common mistake]

Before you submit:
[student success check]
```

This structure makes the lesson feel more like guidance and less like a wall of directions.

<TakeawayStrip tone="teacher">
  Examples show the target. Non-examples show the trap. Success checks help students improve before the teacher ever sees the work.
</TakeawayStrip>

## Practice

For one task in your student-facing lesson draft, write a strong example, a plausible non-example that is missing exactly one quality, and a three- or four-item success check. Then ask a colleague to use only the success check to spot what your non-example is missing. If they cannot, the check is not specific enough yet.
