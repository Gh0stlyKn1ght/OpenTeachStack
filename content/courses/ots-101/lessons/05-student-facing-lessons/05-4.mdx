> OTS-101 frame: AI supports teacher-approved course content that becomes student-facing only after review.

In this lesson, you will build a student-facing lesson draft.

This is the course page students would actually see, not the private teacher planning notes.

The draft should be clear enough that a student can start, work, check, and submit without guessing what the teacher meant.

## What your lesson draft needs

Your student-facing lesson draft should include:

- lesson title
- learning target in student language
- why this matters
- materials or tools
- step-by-step directions
- example or model
- non-example or common mistake
- success check
- submission instructions
- teacher verification notes

## Step 1: Write the student learning target

Use student language.

```text
Today I will learn how to [skill] so I can [purpose or product].
```

Example:

```text
Today I will learn how to choose evidence from a source so I can explain why one response is stronger than another.
```

## Step 2: Add a short why

Keep it brief.

```text
This matters because strong answers do more than state an opinion. They show evidence and explain why that evidence matters.
```

Students do not need a motivational speech every time. They need enough purpose to understand the task.

## Step 3: List materials

Name what students need.

```text
You will use:
- Response A
- Response B
- Evidence comparison chart
```

If something is a link, file, handout, or tool, name it clearly.

## Step 4: Write Use-Do-Make-Check directions

Use the pattern from this chapter.

```text
1. Open Response A and Response B.
2. Highlight one sentence in each response that uses evidence.
3. Choose the response you think is stronger.
4. Write a three-sentence explanation of your choice.
5. Before submitting, check that you named the response, included evidence, and explained why the evidence matters.
```

## Step 5: Add an example and non-example

Keep them short.

```text
Strong example:
Response B is stronger because it uses a detail from the source about screen time limits. The evidence helps explain why the claim is not just an opinion. It shows a reason the reader can check.

Watch out for:
Response B is better because it sounds good and has more stuff.
```

Then add a note:

```text
The weak example gives an opinion, but it does not name evidence.
```

## Step 6: Add the success check

```text
Before submitting, check that your work:
- names the stronger response
- includes one piece of evidence
- explains why the evidence supports your choice
- uses complete sentences
```

## Step 7: Add submission instructions

Students should know exactly what to turn in.

```text
Submit your three-sentence explanation in the assignment box. You do not need to submit your highlights unless your teacher asks for them.
```

## Step 8: Add teacher verification notes

This part can be private or visible only to the teacher.

```text
Teacher verification notes:
- Check that Response A and Response B are linked correctly.
- Confirm the examples use source details accurately.
- Review reading level and accessibility.
- Confirm submission instructions match the course platform.
```

## Build Step

Hand your draft to someone who is not in your class: another teacher, a family member, a student from a different section. Ask them to complete the first step using only what is on the page. Fix whatever they had to ask you about before it reaches real students.

## Quality Check

The draft is ready when a student could start, work, check their own quality, and submit without hearing you explain it out loud.

## Capstone Connection

This draft is the student-facing centerpiece of the capstone packet. Keep the teacher verification notes attached to it so the packet shows what you checked, not just the polished page.
