# Build a Small Rubric With Observable Criteria

> OTS-101 frame: AI supports teacher-approved course content that becomes student-facing only after review.

A rubric should make quality visible.

It should not just make grading look official.

For this course, keep rubrics small. Three criteria are usually enough for one assignment or lab.

## What makes a rubric useful?

A useful rubric names qualities the teacher can actually see in the student work.

Weak criterion:

> Shows good understanding.

Stronger criterion:

> Explains why the selected evidence supports the claim.

The stronger criterion is observable. The teacher can point to the explanation and decide whether it is there.

## Avoid vague quality words

Rubrics often hide behind words like:

- excellent
- thoughtful
- complete
- creative
- strong
- weak
- good effort
- demonstrates understanding

These words may be useful as summaries, but they need observable details.

Instead of:

```text
Excellent explanation
```

write:

```text
Explanation names the chosen source, gives two credibility clues, and explains how those clues support the choice.
```

## Use three criteria

For a short assignment, try this structure:

| Criterion | What the teacher looks for |
|---|---|
| Target skill | Does the work show the skill named in the learning target? |
| Evidence | Does the student use required source details, examples, data, or reasoning? |
| Clarity | Can the reader understand the student's decision or product? |

You can adjust the labels, but keep the rubric tied to visible evidence.

## Write levels only if you need them

Sometimes a checklist is enough.

For early drafts, use:

```text
Meets target / Needs revision
```

or:

```text
Yes / Not yet
```

A giant four-level rubric is not automatically better.

If the assignment is small, the rubric should be small too.

## Ask AI for rubric help carefully

Use your prompt pattern:

```text
Using this learning target and assignment directions, draft a three-criterion rubric. Each criterion must describe observable evidence in student work. Avoid vague words like good, excellent, thoughtful, or complete unless they are explained with visible details.
```

Then revise the rubric yourself.

AI can suggest language, but the teacher decides what counts.
