> OTS-101 frame: AI supports teacher-approved course content that becomes student-facing only after review.

Before publishing an assignment or lab, check the chain.

The target, task, evidence, rubric, and feedback should all point in the same direction.

If one piece points somewhere else, students may work hard on the wrong thing.

## Run the alignment test

Write the learning target at the top of the page.

Then ask each question:

- Does the assignment require the target skill?
- Does the student evidence show the target skill?
- Does the rubric score the target skill?
- Does the feedback help students improve the target skill?
- Are any criteria scoring unrelated things?

If the answer is no, revise before publishing.

## Watch for drift

Assignment packets often drift in three ways.

## Drift 1: The task is fun but unrelated

Students may enjoy the activity, but the evidence does not prove the target.

Fix it by adding a product or explanation that shows the target skill.

## Drift 2: The rubric scores the wrong thing

The rubric may score neatness, effort, creativity, or length when the target is really about reasoning, evidence, accuracy, or process.

Fix it by replacing vague criteria with observable target-aligned criteria.

## Drift 3: Feedback is only praise or correction

Feedback may say "great job" or "try harder" without telling students what to revise.

Fix it by naming the next move.

Example:

```text
Your source choice is clear. Now add one credibility clue from the source and explain why that clue supports your decision.
```

## Final packet review note

Use this note before moving on:

```text
Packet review:
- Target is visible: yes/no
- Task requires the target skill: yes/no
- Evidence is inspectable: yes/no
- Rubric criteria are observable: yes/no
- Feedback supports revision: yes/no
- Teacher verification complete: yes/no
- Status: ready / revise / not ready
```

## Classroom Scenario

A teacher builds a source-evaluation assignment. The task asks students to compare two sources, but the rubric mostly scores neatness and complete sentences.

Students could earn points while barely showing the target skill.

## Weak Version

Weak packet review:

> The assignment has a rubric, so it is ready.

A rubric can exist and still measure the wrong thing.

## Better Version

Better packet review:

> The task asks students to choose the more trustworthy source, the evidence is a four-sentence explanation, and the rubric scores source choice, credibility clues, and explanation.

Now the chain points at one target.

## Safety / Accessibility / Source Note

Check whether the task gives all students access to the sources, tools, materials, and response format needed to show the target skill.

## Reflection

Which part of your packet is most likely to drift away from the target: task, evidence, rubric, or feedback?
