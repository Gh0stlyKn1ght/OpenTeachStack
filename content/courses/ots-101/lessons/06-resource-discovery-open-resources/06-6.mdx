# Building a Source Bank

## Why This Matters

A teacher building a first reusable mini-unit needs more than a tool list or a checklist. The goal in **Resource Discovery and Open Resources** is to make a classroom system that can be used, inspected, revised, and handed to another educator without needing a meeting first.

For **Building a Source Bank**, the practical pressure is simple: accepting a polished lesson draft before the sources, student task, assessment evidence, and classroom routine are inspectable. This page turns that pressure into a visible update to **Resource evaluation sheet**.

## Classroom Scenario

Use this situation as the working example: an ultrasonic sensor mini-unit where students collect readings, explain uncertainty, revise a threshold, and submit a short design reflection. By the end, the work should support this habit: source-backed planning, plain-language decisions, and visible revision notes.

In this scenario, the learner should be able to point to:

- what the teacher prepares before students see the work,
- what students will read, build, submit, or discuss,
- what evidence shows the work is ready,
- what gets revised after the first use.

## Key Idea

Open work needs reuse instructions, source notes, license decisions, exclusions, and a way for another educator to suggest improvements safely.

Watch for this mistake: Treating a public link as permission to copy or treating a repository as self-explanatory.

## What You Will Do

Make this page concrete by writing three small decisions:

- Before class, connect building a source bank to one setup decision in the resource evaluation sheet: what the teacher prepares, where it lives, and which source or safety check keeps it honest.
- During class, connect building a source bank to one student action in middle school robotics and technology: what learners read, build, submit, discuss, or revise while working through an ultrasonic sensor mini-unit where students collect readings, explain uncertainty, revise a threshold, and submit a short design reflection.
- After class, review building a source bank through the resource discovery and open resources lens: what evidence proves the move worked, which mistake to avoid (treating a public link as permission to copy or treating a repository as self-explanatory), and what revision supports source-backed planning, plain-language decisions, and visible revision notes.

## What Good Work Looks Like

A strong **building a source bank** entry is concrete enough that another teacher can inspect it. It names the class context, the artifact location, the student product, the source or safety check, and the revision trigger.

For this lesson, a strong entry might say:

> README entry: This handout may be adapted under CC BY-NC-SA 4.0; screenshots are excluded; sources are listed in source-notes.md.

That level of detail is enough to review. It is also small enough to revise after one classroom use.
