# Rubrics With Observable Criteria

## Why This Matters

A teacher building a first reusable mini-unit needs more than a tool list or a checklist. The goal in **Assessment, Rubrics, and Feedback** is to make a classroom system that can be used, inspected, revised, and handed to another educator without needing a meeting first.

For **Rubrics With Observable Criteria**, the practical pressure is simple: accepting a polished lesson draft before the sources, student task, assessment evidence, and classroom routine are inspectable. This page turns that pressure into a visible update to **Assessment and rubric draft**.

## Classroom Scenario

Use this situation as the working example: an ultrasonic sensor mini-unit where students collect readings, explain uncertainty, revise a threshold, and submit a short design reflection. By the end, the work should support this habit: source-backed planning, plain-language decisions, and visible revision notes.

In this scenario, the learner should be able to point to:

- what the teacher prepares before students see the work,
- what students will read, build, submit, or discuss,
- what evidence shows the work is ready,
- what gets revised after the first use.

## Key Idea

Assessment evidence should show the specific decision students made, the criteria used to judge it, and the revision opportunity that follows.

Watch for this mistake: Scoring effort or completion when the learning target requires evidence, reasoning, or revision.

Make this page concrete by writing three small decisions:

- Before class, connect rubrics with observable criteria to one setup decision in the assessment and rubric draft: what the teacher prepares, where it lives, and which source or safety check keeps it honest.
- During class, connect rubrics with observable criteria to one student action in middle school robotics and technology: what learners read, build, submit, discuss, or revise while working through an ultrasonic sensor mini-unit where students collect readings, explain uncertainty, revise a threshold, and submit a short design reflection.
- After class, review rubrics with observable criteria through the assessment rubrics and feedback lens: what evidence proves the move worked, which mistake to avoid (scoring effort or completion when the learning target requires evidence, reasoning, or revision), and what revision supports source-backed planning, plain-language decisions, and visible revision notes.

## What Good Work Looks Like

A strong **rubrics with observable criteria** entry is concrete enough that another teacher can inspect it. It names the class context, the artifact location, the student product, the source or safety check, and the revision trigger.

For this lesson, a strong entry might say:

> Rubric row: Threshold choice uses at least three readings, explains one unreliable value, and revises after test feedback.

That level of detail is enough to review. It is also small enough to revise after one classroom use.
