> OTS-101 frame: AI supports teacher-approved course content that becomes student-facing only after review.

Safety is easier to handle before the content is published.

Once students can see the page, a bad link, private detail, confusing instruction, or risky task becomes part of the learning environment.

## Why This Matters

Safety review protects the classroom before the course page becomes real to students. It is easier to remove a risky prompt, link, screenshot, or task before publishing than after students have used it.

## The practical teacher move

Do the safety check while the draft is still easy to change.

Before polishing the page, ask what students are being asked to open, share, download, post, describe, or trust. That is where the risk usually lives.

The goal is not to make teachers afraid of every draft. The goal is to catch the parts that could harm students, expose private information, or create avoidable classroom trouble.

## What safety means here

In this course, safety means the content does not create unnecessary risk for students, teachers, or the school community.

That includes:

- private student data
- sensitive personal prompts
- unsafe external links
- inappropriate media
- unverified instructions
- risky tool use
- confusing directions around accounts or sharing
- activities that ask students to disclose more than needed

## Watch for accidental disclosure

AI-assisted drafts can accidentally carry private context if the prompt included it.

Before publishing, check for:

- names
- email addresses
- student initials when they are identifiable
- screenshots with student information
- filenames that include private details
- copied student work
- comments or feedback from real submissions
- class incidents described too specifically

If a detail could identify a student, remove or fictionalize it.

## Watch for sensitive prompts

Some prompts ask students to share personal experiences, family details, health information, trauma, beliefs, or identity details.

Those topics may belong in some courses with care, but they should never appear accidentally because AI generated a generic reflection prompt.

## Replace Disclosure Prompts

Instead of:

```text
Describe a personal time when you felt unsafe online.
```

use:

```text
Choose one fictional scenario below. Identify the safety risk and recommend a safer next step.
```

The second version lets students practice safety reasoning without requiring personal disclosure.

## Watch for risky links and tools

Click every link before publishing.

Check whether students need:

- an account
- age permission
- paid access
- downloads
- public posting
- camera or microphone access
- personal data entry

If the task depends on a tool your students cannot safely access, revise the task.

## Add a safety note when needed

Some lessons need a simple student-facing safety boundary.

Example:

```text
Use the fictional scenario only. Do not share real names, passwords, private messages, or personal accounts in this activity.
```

That note is not fear-based.

It tells students the safe lane for the task.

<TakeawayStrip tone="warning">
  If the activity can teach the skill with fictional or generalized context, do not require students to share private real-life details.
</TakeawayStrip>

## Classroom scenario

A teacher asks AI to create an online safety reflection. The draft asks students to describe a real time they felt unsafe online and explain what happened.

That question may look thoughtful, but it asks students to disclose personal experiences the lesson does not need.

## Weak version

```text
Write about a real unsafe online experience. Include what happened, who was involved, and how you responded.
```

This version creates privacy risk and may push students into sharing information the teacher does not need to collect.

## Better version

```text
Read the fictional scenario. Identify two safety risks, explain why they matter, and recommend one safer next step.
Do not include real names, passwords, private messages, or personal accounts.
```

This version keeps the learning target while protecting students from unnecessary disclosure.

## Build step

Add a safety row to your course content safety and access checklist.

For one student-facing activity, record:

- what students are asked to share
- whether the activity can use fictional or generalized information instead
- what links, tools, or files students must open
- what safety note students need before they begin
- which OTS-000 boundary, district note, or approved source supports the decision

## Quality check

Before publishing, ask:

- Can students complete the activity without revealing private information?
- Are all links and tools safe for the class context?
- Are account, sharing, download, camera, or microphone requirements named clearly?
- Would a family or administrator understand why the activity is safe?

## Safety and accessibility note

Safety and accessibility often overlap. A confusing platform direction, hidden permission requirement, or unexpected account prompt can become a safety problem because students improvise.

Name the safe path before students have to guess.

## Reflection

Where does your current lesson ask students to share more than the learning target requires?

Rewrite that part so students can practice the skill without exposing private details.

## Capstone connection

Your final Mini Course Content Packet should include the safety decision, not just the revised lesson. A reviewer should be able to see what risk you noticed and how you reduced it before publication.
