# Safety Starts Before Students See the Page

> OTS-101 frame: AI supports teacher-approved course content that becomes student-facing only after review.

Safety is easier to handle before the content is published.

Once students can see the page, a bad link, private detail, confusing instruction, or risky task becomes part of the learning environment.

## Why This Matters

Safety review protects the classroom before the course page becomes real to students. It is easier to remove a risky prompt, link, screenshot, or task before publishing than after students have used it.

## Bring It Back to Students

The concept matters because it changes what students receive or what teachers can verify.

That does not mean teachers need to be afraid of every draft.

## The Teacher Move

The teacher move is to make the hidden decision visible before polishing the language.

It means safety review belongs in the workflow.

## What safety means here

In this course, safety means the content does not create unnecessary risk for students, teachers, or the school community.

That includes:

- private student data
- sensitive personal prompts
- unsafe external links
- inappropriate media
- unverified instructions
- risky tool use
- confusing directions around accounts or sharing
- activities that ask students to disclose more than needed

<FrameworkBlock title="Safety review zones" eyebrow="Before publishing">
  <ConceptCard title="Data">
    Does the content expose real student names, grades, work, images, or private context?
  </ConceptCard>
  <ConceptCard title="Links">
    Do links open, match the intended site, and fit the age and school context?
  </ConceptCard>
  <ConceptCard title="Tasks">
    Do student activities avoid unnecessary disclosure, unsafe tool use, or unmanaged risk?
  </ConceptCard>
  <ConceptCard title="Instructions">
    Are tool steps, account directions, and sharing instructions accurate and safe?
  </ConceptCard>
</FrameworkBlock>

## Watch for accidental disclosure

AI-assisted drafts can accidentally carry private context if the prompt included it.

Before publishing, check for:

- names
- email addresses
- student initials when they are identifiable
- screenshots with student information
- filenames that include private details
- copied student work
- comments or feedback from real submissions
- class incidents described too specifically

If a detail could identify a student, remove or fictionalize it.

## Watch for sensitive prompts

Some prompts ask students to share personal experiences, family details, health information, trauma, beliefs, or identity details.

Those topics may belong in some courses with care, but they should never appear accidentally because AI generated a generic reflection prompt.

## Bring It Back to Students 2

The concept matters because it changes what students receive or what teachers can verify.

Instead of:

```text
Describe a personal time when you felt unsafe online.
```

use:

```text
Choose one fictional scenario below. Identify the safety risk and recommend a safer next step.
```

The second version lets students practice safety reasoning without requiring personal disclosure.

## Watch for risky links and tools

Click every link before publishing.

Check whether students need:

- an account
- age permission
- paid access
- downloads
- public posting
- camera or microphone access
- personal data entry

If the task depends on a tool your students cannot safely access, revise the task.

## Add a safety note when needed

Some lessons need a simple student-facing safety boundary.

Example:

```text
Use the fictional scenario only. Do not share real names, passwords, private messages, or personal accounts in this activity.
```

That note is not fear-based.

It tells students the safe lane for the task.

<TakeawayStrip tone="warning">
  If the activity can teach the skill with fictional or generalized context, do not require students to share private real-life details.
</TakeawayStrip>

## Problem

Safety Starts Before Students See the Page matters because AI can make weak course work look complete. The teacher still has to decide what students should learn, what evidence counts, and what belongs in front of the class.

## Common Misunderstanding

The common mistake is thinking a polished AI draft means the curriculum decision is finished. A draft can be useful and still be unready for students.

## Concrete Example

Instead of asking AI to "make a lesson," the teacher names the learning target, the student task, and the evidence to check. AI can then help draft language inside those boundaries.
