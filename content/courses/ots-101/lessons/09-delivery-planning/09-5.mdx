# The One-Day Lesson Site Workflow

## Why This Matters

A teacher building a first reusable mini-unit needs more than a tool list or a checklist. The goal in **Delivery Planning** is to make a classroom system that can be used, inspected, revised, and handed to another educator without needing a meeting first.

For **The One-Day Lesson Site Workflow**, the practical pressure is simple: accepting a polished lesson draft before the sources, student task, assessment evidence, and classroom routine are inspectable. This page turns that pressure into a visible update to **One-day lesson site plan**.

## Classroom Scenario

Use this situation as the working example: an ultrasonic sensor mini-unit where students collect readings, explain uncertainty, revise a threshold, and submit a short design reflection. By the end, the work should support this habit: source-backed planning, plain-language decisions, and visible revision notes.

In this scenario, the learner should be able to point to:

- what the teacher prepares before students see the work,
- what students will read, build, submit, or discuss,
- what evidence shows the work is ready,
- what gets revised after the first use.

## Key Idea

A course site is a maintained teaching surface. It needs clear navigation, safe public content, accessible pages, source notes, and a review rhythm.

Watch for this mistake: Publishing a site because it loads while broken links, private details, and ownership questions remain unresolved.

## What You Will Do

Make this page concrete by writing three small decisions:

- Before class, connect the one-day lesson site workflow to one setup decision in the one-day lesson site plan: what the teacher prepares, where it lives, and which source or safety check keeps it honest.
- During class, connect the one-day lesson site workflow to one student action in middle school robotics and technology: what learners read, build, submit, discuss, or revise while working through an ultrasonic sensor mini-unit where students collect readings, explain uncertainty, revise a threshold, and submit a short design reflection.
- After class, review the one-day lesson site workflow through the delivery planning lens: what evidence proves the move worked, which mistake to avoid (publishing a site because it loads while broken links, private details, and ownership questions remain unresolved), and what revision supports source-backed planning, plain-language decisions, and visible revision notes.

A strong **the one-day lesson site workflow** entry is concrete enough that another teacher can inspect it. It names the class context, the artifact location, the student product, the source or safety check, and the revision trigger.

That level of detail is enough to review. It is also small enough to revise after one classroom use.
