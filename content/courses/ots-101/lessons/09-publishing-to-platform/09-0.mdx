# Publishing to a Platform

> OTS-101 frame: AI supports teacher-approved course content that becomes student-facing only after review.

Publishing is not just copying text into a box.

When course content moves into Canvas, Google Classroom, Moodle, a website, a document, slides, or a printed packet, the teacher has to preserve the instructional decisions that made the content usable.

The platform should deliver the course content.

It should not quietly become a second source of truth.

<FrameworkBlock title="Publishing without losing control" eyebrow="Delivery bridge">
  <ConceptCard title="Source">
    Start from teacher-approved content, not from an unreviewed AI draft.
  </ConceptCard>
  <ConceptCard title="Adapt">
    Adjust headings, links, files, submissions, and formatting for the platform.
  </ConceptCard>
  <ConceptCard title="Check">
    Confirm students can open, read, complete, and submit the work.
  </ConceptCard>
  <ConceptCard title="Label">

  </ConceptCard>

## Why this chapter matters

A lesson can be strong in the source file and broken in the platform.

Maybe bullets disappear. Maybe links point to private files. Maybe the submission box is missing. Maybe the heading structure collapses. Maybe the rubric is copied but the feedback directions are not.

Publishing needs its own review.

Not because platforms are bad.

Because every delivery surface changes the shape of the content.

## What you will build

By the end of this chapter, you will have a platform publishing checklist.

It will help you verify:

- the content came from the approved source
- headings and lists survived the move
- links and files open for students
- submission instructions match the platform
- rubric or success criteria are visible
- safety and accessibility checks still hold
- the platform copy is labeled as an export

## The chapter path

1. First, we identify what can break during platform publishing.
2. Then we prepare content for the platform without changing the source of truth.
3. Then we check the student view.
4. Then you build a platform publishing checklist.
5. Finally, you decide whether the platform copy is ready to release.

<TakeawayStrip tone="teacher">
  Publishing is a translation step. The goal is to preserve the lesson while adapting it to the place students will actually use it.
</TakeawayStrip>
