This is the capstone.

Not a fake full course.

Not a pile of AI-generated pages.

Not a polished-looking export with missing teaching decisions.

The Mini Course Content Packet proves the teaching work itself is organized, verified, student-facing, and ready for review.

## What the packet has to prove

The goal is a small Mini Course Content Packet that a teacher can inspect, revise, and eventually publish with confidence.

## Why this chapter matters

A course does not become trustworthy because it has many files.

It becomes trustworthy because the important decisions are visible:

- what students are learning
- what students will do
- what evidence proves learning
- what materials are used
- what AI helped draft
- what the teacher verified
- what is ready for students
- what still needs revision

This chapter asks you to assemble those decisions into one coherent packet.

## What you will build

By the end of this chapter, you will have a Mini Course Content Packet with:

- course content inventory
- learning target and lesson map
- reusable course-content prompt
- AI draft verification checklist
- student-facing lesson draft
- assignment and feedback packet
- course content system map
- safety and access checklist
- platform publishing checklist
- final teacher review note

## The chapter path

1. First, we define what belongs in the packet and what does not.
2. Then we assemble the packet in a clean order.
3. Then we run a final review for alignment, student clarity, safety, and source control.
4. Then you create the final Mini Course Content Packet.
5. Finally, you decide what is ready now and what belongs in the next revision cycle.

<TakeawayStrip tone="teacher">
  The capstone is not about pretending the course is finished. It is about proving the course content is real enough to review, revise, and teach from.
</TakeawayStrip>

## Local Context Note

Attach or reference local tool, access, safety, file, source, or platform notes only when they help the reviewer understand the packet. The OTS-101 packet should still stand on its own as course content evidence.

## What done looks like

This is the only chapter where you stop building new artifacts and start judging the ones you already have.

You are done when you can hand the packet to another teacher and they can answer, without opening your AI chat history:

- what the packet teaches and who it is for
- where the source of truth lives, and what is draft versus student-ready
- what students will do and what evidence they produce
- what you verified, and what still needs a human check
- whether the packet is ready for review, ready for a limited pilot, needs revision, or should not be published yet

If they cannot, you have found your next revision target instead of a finished course. That is a real result, not a failure.
