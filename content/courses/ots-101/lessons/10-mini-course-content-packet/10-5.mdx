# Checkpoint: What Is Ready, and What Needs Revision?

The final step is honesty.

Do not call the mini-course packet finished because the folder is full.

Call it ready only if the packet has enough source control, student clarity, evidence, safety review, and teacher judgment to support actual use.

<FrameworkBlock title="Final packet decision" eyebrow="Course closeout">
  <ConceptCard title="Ready for review">
    The packet is coherent enough for a teacher or teammate to inspect.
  </ConceptCard>
  <ConceptCard title="Ready for pilot">
    The student-facing pieces are safe and clear enough for limited classroom use with teacher monitoring.
  </ConceptCard>
  <ConceptCard title="Needs revision">
    The packet has useful pieces, but a known issue must be fixed first.
  </ConceptCard>
  <ConceptCard title="Do not publish">
    The packet contains unresolved risk, unclear evidence, fake completeness, or broken source control.
  </ConceptCard>
</FrameworkBlock>

## Make the decision visible

Use this final status note:

```text
Final mini-course packet status:
[ ] Ready for teacher review
[ ] Ready for limited classroom pilot
[ ] Needs revision before use
[ ] Do not publish

Reason:

Evidence for this decision:

Next revision action:

Next review date:
```

A visible decision prevents the packet from drifting into use without review.

## Ready for teacher review

Choose this when:

- all required artifacts are present
- the source of truth is clear
- the learning path is coherent
- review notes are included
- the packet is honest about unresolved questions

This is a strong capstone status.

It does not mean every classroom detail is final.

## Ready for limited classroom pilot

Choose this only when:

- student-facing content is clear
- safety and access checks are complete
- links and tools work
- assignment and submission path are ready
- the teacher is prepared to monitor and revise after use

A pilot is not a full release. It is a controlled first use.

## Needs revision before use

Choose this when:

- the target and task are not aligned
- student directions are unclear
- evidence is weak
- rubric criteria are vague
- source or safety checks are incomplete
- platform publishing is not checked

This is not failure. It is responsible course building.

## Do not publish

Choose this when:

- the packet contains fake or placeholder content
- private student data is visible
- source control is broken
- major links or materials fail
- the teacher cannot tell what is approved
- the packet would confuse or mislead students

Do not publish is sometimes the best professional decision.

## What OTS-101 should leave you with

By the end of OTS-101, you should not just have content.

You should have a workflow:

- inventory the course content
- define the target and lesson path
- prompt AI with source, context, task, format, and checks
- verify AI drafts before students see them
- write student-facing lessons
- create assignments, rubrics, and feedback
- organize source, drafts, exports, and archives
- check safety, accessibility, copyright, and source quality
- publish carefully to platforms
- assemble an honest mini-course packet

<TakeawayStrip tone="teacher">
  The win is not that AI made a course. The win is that the teacher can now build, inspect, revise, and publish course content with more control.
</TakeawayStrip>
