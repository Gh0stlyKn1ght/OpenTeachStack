# Verify Standards and Sources

## Why This Matters

A teacher building a first reusable mini-unit needs more than a tool list or a checklist. The goal in **Mini-Unit Capstone** is to make a classroom system that can be used, inspected, revised, and handed to another educator without needing a meeting first.

For **Verify Standards and Sources**, the practical pressure is simple: accepting a polished lesson draft before the sources, student task, assessment evidence, and classroom routine are inspectable. This page turns that pressure into a visible update to **Verified mini-unit capstone**.

## Classroom Scenario

Use this situation as the working example: an ultrasonic sensor mini-unit where students collect readings, explain uncertainty, revise a threshold, and submit a short design reflection. By the end, the work should support this habit: source-backed planning, plain-language decisions, and visible revision notes.

In this scenario, the learner should be able to point to:

- what the teacher prepares before students see the work,
- what students will read, build, submit, or discuss,
- what evidence shows the work is ready,
- what gets revised after the first use.

## Key Idea

A standard becomes teachable only when the verb, concept, student evidence, and assessment move are visible.

Watch for this mistake: Copying a standard into the plan without naming what students will actually do or submit.

## What You Will Do

Make this page concrete by writing three small decisions:

- Before class, connect verify standards and sources to one setup decision in the verified mini-unit capstone: what the teacher prepares, where it lives, and which source or safety check keeps it honest.
- During class, connect verify standards and sources to one student action in middle school robotics and technology: what learners read, build, submit, discuss, or revise while working through an ultrasonic sensor mini-unit where students collect readings, explain uncertainty, revise a threshold, and submit a short design reflection.
- After class, review verify standards and sources through the mini-unit capstone lens: what evidence proves the move worked, which mistake to avoid (copying a standard into the plan without naming what students will actually do or submit), and what revision supports source-backed planning, plain-language decisions, and visible revision notes.

## What Good Work Looks Like

A strong **verify standards and sources** entry is concrete enough that another teacher can inspect it. It names the class context, the artifact location, the student product, the source or safety check, and the revision trigger.

For this lesson, a strong entry might say:

> Target entry: Students compare two sensor readings, identify one reliability concern, and justify a threshold choice using their data table.

That level of detail is enough to review. It is also small enough to revise after one classroom use.
