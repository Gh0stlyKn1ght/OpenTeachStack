# Revision Log

## Why This Matters

A teacher building a first reusable mini-unit needs more than a tool list or a checklist. The goal in **Mini-Unit Capstone** is to make a classroom system that can be used, inspected, revised, and handed to another educator without needing a meeting first.

For **Revision Log**, the practical pressure is simple: accepting a polished lesson draft before the sources, student task, assessment evidence, and classroom routine are inspectable. This page turns that pressure into a visible update to **Reflection and revision log**.

## Classroom Scenario

Use this situation as the working example: an ultrasonic sensor mini-unit where students collect readings, explain uncertainty, revise a threshold, and submit a short design reflection. By the end, the work should support this habit: source-backed planning, plain-language decisions, and visible revision notes.

In this scenario, the learner should be able to point to:

- what the teacher prepares before students see the work,
- what students will read, build, submit, or discuss,
- what evidence shows the work is ready,
- what gets revised after the first use.

## Key Idea

A capstone proves the system can be inspected by someone else. Every artifact needs purpose, location, evidence, review status, and maintenance owner.

Watch for this mistake: Submitting a folder of finished-looking files without release status, review notes, or a maintenance plan.

## What You Will Do

Make this page concrete by writing three small decisions:

- Before class, connect revision log to one setup decision in the reflection and revision log: what the teacher prepares, where it lives, and which source or safety check keeps it honest.
- During class, connect revision log to one student action in middle school robotics and technology: what learners read, build, submit, discuss, or revise while working through an ultrasonic sensor mini-unit where students collect readings, explain uncertainty, revise a threshold, and submit a short design reflection.
- After class, review revision log through the mini-unit capstone lens: what evidence proves the move worked, which mistake to avoid (submitting a folder of finished-looking files without release status, review notes, or a maintenance plan), and what revision supports source-backed planning, plain-language decisions, and visible revision notes.

## Build the Artifact

Use **Revision Log** to make one classroom decision visible inside **Reflection and revision log**.

Add this to your artifact:

- Classroom context: middle school robotics and technology; an ultrasonic sensor mini-unit where students collect readings, explain uncertainty, revise a threshold, and submit a short design reflection.
- Artifact entry: In **Reflection and revision log**, add the decision for **Revision Log** with the student-facing evidence and the review owner.
- Example entry: Artifact index row: Sensor mini-unit map / ready / proves sequence and assessment alignment / reviewed by peer / revisit after first classroom use.
- Review check: Check the entry against standards, official documentation, source notes, privacy checks, and accessibility checks, then write what should change after the first classroom use.
- Peer review check: A colleague should be able to open the mini-unit capstone artifact, understand the decision behind **Revision Log**, and know the next action without extra explanation.

## What Good Work Looks Like

A strong **revision log** entry is concrete enough that another teacher can inspect it. It names the class context, the artifact location, the student product, the source or safety check, and the revision trigger.

For this lesson, a strong entry might say:

> Artifact index row: Sensor mini-unit map / ready / proves sequence and assessment alignment / reviewed by peer / revisit after first classroom use.

That level of detail is enough to review. It is also small enough to revise after one classroom use.

## Quality Check

Before moving on, confirm:

- Your artifact entry is stored in **Reflection and revision log** rather than kept as a private reminder.
- The student-facing task or evidence is named in plain language.
- The review step names a source, safety, privacy, accessibility, or maintenance check.
- The common mistake is avoided: Submitting a folder of finished-looking files without release status, review notes, or a maintenance plan.
- The next classroom use or revision is obvious.
