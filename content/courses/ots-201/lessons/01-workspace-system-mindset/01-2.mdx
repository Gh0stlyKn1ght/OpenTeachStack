# Teacher Workflows Google Can Support

## Why This Matters

Google Workspace can support many teaching routines, but not every frustration should become a folder, Form, Sheet, or automation. The strongest workspace systems begin with repeated teacher work that has a clear trigger, clear evidence, and a clear next action.

If you choose the wrong workflow, you may organize something that does not matter or build a complicated system for a problem that only needed one better file name.

A workspace system works when it supports a real repeated teaching workflow instead of adding structure for its own sake.

## What You'll Learn

- Identify teacher workflows that are worth systematizing.
- Sort workflows by classroom impact, frequency, and risk.
- Avoid turning every annoyance into a Workspace project.
- Choose one workflow that belongs in your audit.

## Core Idea

A good Workspace candidate has three traits:

1. It happens more than once.
2. It involves files, links, responses, evidence, or collaboration.
3. It creates confusion, lost time, privacy risk, or inconsistent student experience when it is unmanaged.

Examples include unit launch materials, lab setup directions, exit-ticket review, assessment feedback, substitute plans, parent update templates, resource approval, and end-of-unit archiving.

## Classroom Scenario

A teacher wants to improve three things:

- The bell-ringer slides are hard to find.
- Students submit project reflections in several places.
- The department wants a shared resource folder.

All three could use Workspace, but the reflection workflow is the best first audit. It affects students directly, produces evidence the teacher must review, and currently creates grading confusion.

## Model Artifact or Example

| Candidate workflow | Frequency | Impact | Risk if unclear | Workspace support | Priority |
| --- | --- |
| Bell-ringer slides | Daily | Medium | Teacher loses time opening class | Slides folder and naming rule | Later |
| Project reflections | Weekly during projects | High | Student evidence lands in different places | Form, response Sheet, tracker link | First |
| Department resources | Monthly | Medium | Teachers reuse outdated files | Shared Drive and archive rule | Later |

## Common Mistake

The common mistake is choosing the most annoying workflow instead of the most important workflow. A messy folder may irritate you every day, but a broken evidence-collection workflow may affect grading, feedback, and student support.

Another mistake is choosing a workflow that is too large, such as "my whole course." Start with one repeated routine.

## Verification Checklist

- The selected workflow happens repeatedly.
- It affects teaching, student access, evidence, collaboration, or maintenance.
- The workflow is narrow enough to audit in one row or a small set of rows.
- The selected tools match the workflow rather than adding extra complexity.
- No private student data is copied into the audit; describe the evidence location, not individual students.

## Reflection / Handoff

Write one sentence explaining why this workflow deserves attention before the others.

Example:

> I am auditing project reflections first because students submit them in multiple places, and that makes feedback inconsistent.
