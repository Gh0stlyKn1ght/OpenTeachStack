# Chapter Plan

## Why This Matters

Sheets become powerful only when teaching decisions are not hidden in memory.  

For a repeatable unit, one tracker should show what is live, what needs fixes, and what happens after class.

In OTS-201 Chapter 04, the workspace goal is not “a full spreadsheet.” It is a **curriculum operations sheet** that helps teams teach safely and consistently.

## Core Idea

Your sheet should model teacher work:

- which lesson is teaching-ready this week
- what standard or competency it supports
- what students actually submit
- who will review and when

## Classroom Situation

Working example for this chapter: a middle school robotics unit where each lesson uses reusable Docs, a shared launch deck, and one evidence sheet.

Use this tracker pattern:

1. One row per lesson run
2. One status per row (Draft, Review, Active, Ready, Archived)
3. One owner and one next-review date per row
4. One evidence destination that stays stable while files rotate

## Common Mistake

The most common failure is a sheet with content but no **operational meaning**.

A usable tracker must let a colleague answer:

- What is teaching this week?
- What is blocking launch today?
- What changed after last class?

## Reflection

What row in your current planning process is updated only when someone says, “I remember where that is”? Make that row owner-tied and deadline-bound.
