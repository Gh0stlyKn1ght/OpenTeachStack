# checkpoint review

## Why This Matters

A teacher team making Google Workspace reliable for weekly planning needs more than a tool list or a checklist. The goal in **Sheets as Curriculum Trackers** is to make a classroom system that can be used, inspected, revised, and handed to another educator without needing a meeting first.

For **checkpoint review**, the practical pressure is simple: building one more folder or tracker without deciding who updates it, when it closes, and how old versions leave the active workspace. This page turns that pressure into a visible update to **Curriculum tracker sheet**.

## Classroom Scenario

Use this situation as the working example: a ninth grade technology team maintaining a current-unit folder, shared tracker, response sheet, delivery deck, and command center. By the end, the work should support this habit: clear ownership, predictable file locations, and closeout routines.

In this scenario, the learner should be able to point to:

- what the teacher prepares before students see the work,
- what students will read, build, submit, or discuss,
- what evidence shows the work is ready,
- what gets revised after the first use.

## Key Idea

A workspace system works when another teacher can find current files, understand ownership, close the loop after class, and avoid exposing private materials.

Watch for this mistake: Leaving the newest copy, answer key, and student responses mixed together in a shared folder.

## What You Will Do

Make this page concrete by writing three small decisions:

- Before class, connect checkpoint review to one setup decision in the curriculum tracker sheet: what the teacher prepares, where it lives, and which source or safety check keeps it honest.
- During class, connect checkpoint review to one student action in shared drive, docs, slides, forms, sheets, and calendar routines: what learners read, build, submit, discuss, or revise while working through a ninth grade technology team maintaining a current-unit folder, shared tracker, response sheet, delivery deck, and command center.
- After class, review checkpoint review through the sheets as curriculum trackers lens: what evidence proves the move worked, which mistake to avoid (leaving the newest copy, answer key, and student responses mixed together in a shared folder), and what revision supports clear ownership, predictable file locations, and closeout routines.

## Build the Artifact

Inspect **Curriculum tracker sheet** and mark what is ready, what needs revision, and what must be checked against sources, safety, privacy, or accessibility before use.

Add this to your artifact:

- Classroom context: shared Drive, Docs, Slides, Forms, Sheets, and Calendar routines; a ninth grade technology team maintaining a current-unit folder, shared tracker, response sheet, delivery deck, and command center.
- Artifact entry: In **Curriculum tracker sheet**, add the decision for **checkpoint review** with the student-facing evidence and the review owner.
- Example entry: Workspace entry: Unit 03 active folder contains current slides, handout, response sheet, and exit ticket; archived versions move out after Friday review.
- Review check: Check the entry against Google Workspace help pages, local sharing policy, accessibility checks, and file ownership decisions, then write what should change after the first classroom use.
- Peer review check: A colleague should be able to open the sheets as curriculum trackers artifact, understand the decision behind **checkpoint review**, and know the next action without extra explanation.

## What Good Work Looks Like

A strong **checkpoint review** entry is concrete enough that another teacher can inspect it. It names the class context, the artifact location, the student product, the source or safety check, and the revision trigger.

For this lesson, a strong entry might say:

> Workspace entry: Unit 03 active folder contains current slides, handout, response sheet, and exit ticket; archived versions move out after Friday review.

That level of detail is enough to review. It is also small enough to revise after one classroom use.

## Quality Check

Before moving on, confirm:

- Your artifact entry is stored in **Curriculum tracker sheet** rather than kept as a private reminder.
- The student-facing task or evidence is named in plain language.
- The review step names a source, safety, privacy, accessibility, or maintenance check.
- The checkpoint distinguishes ready, revise, and blocked items.
- The next revision has an owner and a realistic date or trigger.

## Reflection

What would a busy teacher misunderstand if they only saw your **Curriculum tracker sheet** entry and not your explanation? Revise the entry until the classroom task, evidence, review step, and next improvement are visible.
