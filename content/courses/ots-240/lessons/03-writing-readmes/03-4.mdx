# checkpoint review

## Why This Matters

An educator publishing or adapting classroom resources responsibly needs more than a tool list or a checklist. The goal in **Writing Useful READMEs** is to make a classroom system that can be used, inspected, revised, and handed to another educator without needing a meeting first.

For **checkpoint review**, the practical pressure is simple: assuming public or free-to-view material is automatically free to copy, remix, or publish. This page turns that pressure into a visible update to **Course README**.

## Classroom Scenario

Use this situation as the working example: a small cybersecurity vocabulary resource with a README, source list, license decision, excluded-media note, and maintenance owner. By the end, the work should support this habit: explain reuse rights, attribution, exclusions, and review steps.

In this scenario, the learner should be able to point to:

- what the teacher prepares before students see the work,
- what students will read, build, submit, or discuss,
- what evidence shows the work is ready,
- what gets revised after the first use.

## Key Idea

Open work needs reuse instructions, source notes, license decisions, exclusions, and a way for another educator to suggest improvements safely.

Watch for this mistake: Treating a public link as permission to copy or treating a repository as self-explanatory.

## What You Will Do

Make this page concrete by writing three small decisions:

- Before class, connect checkpoint review to one setup decision in the course README: what the teacher prepares, where it lives, and which source or safety check keeps it honest.
- During class, connect checkpoint review to one student action in open resources, github repositories, readmes, license notes, source lists, and contribution review: what learners read, build, submit, discuss, or revise while working through a small cybersecurity vocabulary resource with a README, source list, license decision, excluded-media note, and maintenance owner.
- After class, review checkpoint review through the writing useful readmes lens: what evidence proves the move worked, which mistake to avoid (treating a public link as permission to copy or treating a repository as self-explanatory), and what revision supports explain reuse rights, attribution, exclusions, and review steps.

## Build the Artifact

Inspect **Course README** and mark what is ready, what needs revision, and what must be checked against sources, safety, privacy, or accessibility before use.

Add this to your artifact:

- Classroom context: open resources, GitHub repositories, READMEs, license notes, source lists, and contribution review; a small cybersecurity vocabulary resource with a README, source list, license decision, excluded-media note, and maintenance owner.
- Artifact entry: In **Course README**, add the decision for **checkpoint review** with the student-facing evidence and the review owner.
- Example entry: README entry: This handout may be adapted under CC BY-NC-SA 4.0; screenshots are excluded; sources are listed in source-notes.md.
- Review check: Check the entry against Creative Commons license terms, GitHub documentation, source citations, and school publishing boundaries, then write what should change after the first classroom use.
- Peer review check: A colleague should be able to open the writing useful readmes artifact, understand the decision behind **checkpoint review**, and know the next action without extra explanation.

## What Good Work Looks Like

A strong **checkpoint review** entry is concrete enough that another teacher can inspect it. It names the class context, the artifact location, the student product, the source or safety check, and the revision trigger.

For this lesson, a strong entry might say:

> README entry: This handout may be adapted under CC BY-NC-SA 4.0; screenshots are excluded; sources are listed in source-notes.md.

That level of detail is enough to review. It is also small enough to revise after one classroom use.

## Quality Check

Before moving on, confirm:

- Your artifact entry is stored in **Course README** rather than kept as a private reminder.
- The student-facing task or evidence is named in plain language.
- The review step names a source, safety, privacy, accessibility, or maintenance check.
- The checkpoint distinguishes ready, revise, and blocked items.
- The next revision has an owner and a realistic date or trigger.

## Reflection

What would a busy teacher misunderstand if they only saw your **Course README** entry and not your explanation? Revise the entry until the classroom task, evidence, review step, and next improvement are visible.
