# Issues, Pull Requests, and Review

## Why This Matters

Open work needs when a teacher can point to the decision, the classroom use, the evidence, and the next review step.

In **Contribution and Review Workflows**, the work sits inside open curriculum publishing with GitHub, README files, licenses, attribution, and review routines. The risk is treating free-to-view material as automatically open, reusable, remixable, or safe to publish. This lesson narrows that risk into one visible update to **Contribution checklist**.

## Core Idea

Make the decision readable to someone else. The artifact explains the purpose, the source of truth, the review routine, and how future revisions will be recorded.

The goal is not to make a perfect document in one pass. The goal is to make the teacher decision clear enough that another educator can inspect it, reuse it, or challenge it without needing a private explanation.

## Lesson Focus

Because this is a focused section, the teacher isolates the issues pull requests decision from the rest of the course. The lesson asks what this idea changes, where it appears, and how the teacher will know it worked.

In 04.1, focus on issues pull requests. Choose one concrete classroom material or teacher decision from this list: a repository file, README section, issue, pull request, source note, or license entry. Connect it to contribution checklist, then decide whether it needs to be created, revised, tested, credited, restricted, published, or archived.

Connect issues pull requests back to contribution workflows. That connection matters because a polished artifact can still fail if the lesson purpose, boundary, evidence, and maintenance routine are not visible.
