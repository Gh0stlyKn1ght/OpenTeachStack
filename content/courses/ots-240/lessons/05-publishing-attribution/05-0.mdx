# chapter plan

## Why This Matters

An educator publishing or adapting classroom resources responsibly needs more than a tool list or a checklist. The goal in **Publishing and Attribution** is to make a classroom system that can be used, inspected, revised, and handed to another educator without needing a meeting first.

For **chapter plan**, the practical pressure is simple: assuming public or free-to-view material is automatically free to copy, remix, or publish. This page turns that pressure into a visible update to **Attribution and license file**.

## Classroom Scenario

Use this situation as the working example: a small cybersecurity vocabulary resource with a README, source list, license decision, excluded-media note, and maintenance owner. By the end, the work should support this habit: explain reuse rights, attribution, exclusions, and review steps.

In this scenario, the learner should be able to point to:

- what the teacher prepares before students see the work,
- what students will read, build, submit, or discuss,
- what evidence shows the work is ready,
- what gets revised after the first use.

## Key Idea

Open work needs reuse instructions, source notes, license decisions, exclusions, and a way for another educator to suggest improvements safely.

Watch for this mistake: Treating a public link as permission to copy or treating a repository as self-explanatory.

## What You Will Do

Make this page concrete by writing three small decisions:

- Before class, connect chapter plan to one setup decision in the attribution and license file: what the teacher prepares, where it lives, and which source or safety check keeps it honest.
- During class, connect chapter plan to one student action in open resources, github repositories, readmes, license notes, source lists, and contribution review: what learners read, build, submit, discuss, or revise while working through a small cybersecurity vocabulary resource with a README, source list, license decision, excluded-media note, and maintenance owner.
- After class, review chapter plan through the publishing and attribution lens: what evidence proves the move worked, which mistake to avoid (treating a public link as permission to copy or treating a repository as self-explanatory), and what revision supports explain reuse rights, attribution, exclusions, and review steps.

## What Good Work Looks Like

A strong **chapter plan** entry is concrete enough that another teacher can inspect it. It names the class context, the artifact location, the student product, the source or safety check, and the revision trigger.

For this lesson, a strong entry might say:

> README entry: This handout may be adapted under CC BY-NC-SA 4.0; screenshots are excluded; sources are listed in source-notes.md.

That level of detail is enough to review. It is also small enough to revise after one classroom use.
