# Checkpoint Review

## Why This Matters

Media belongs in a lesson when a teacher can point to the decision, the classroom use, the evidence, and the next review step.

In **Media With Purpose**, the work sits inside instructional media, AI image review, diagrams, slides, captions, transcripts, and accessibility checks. The risk is adding attractive media that distracts from the learning target, invents details, hides important information, or excludes students. This lesson narrows that risk into one visible update to **Media purpose map**.

## Core Idea

Run a checkpoint on the **Media purpose map**. Mark what is ready, what needs revision, what is blocked, and what evidence supports that decision.

The goal is not to make a perfect document in one pass. The goal is to make the teacher decision clear enough that another educator can inspect it, reuse it, or challenge it without needing a private explanation.

## Lesson Focus

Because this is the checkpoint for media purpose, the teacher reviews the artifact as a working system. The checkpoint looks for missing decisions, unclear ownership, weak evidence, and maintenance steps that would be forgotten during a busy week.

In 01.4, focus on checkpoint review decisions. Choose one concrete classroom material or teacher decision from this list: an image, diagram, slide, short video, transcript, caption file, or media review row. Connect it to media purpose map, then decide whether it needs to be created, revised, tested, credited, restricted, published, or archived.

Connect checkpoint review decisions back to media purpose. That connection matters because a polished artifact can still fail if the lesson purpose, boundary, evidence, and maintenance routine are not visible.

## Classroom Example

Use this working situation: a teacher is building a media packet with one explanatory diagram, one delivery deck, one short video plan, and accessibility notes for each asset.

For **Checkpoint Review**, choose one concrete classroom material or teacher decision from this list: an image, diagram, slide, short video, transcript, caption file, or media review row. Keep the example small enough to review during the lesson and focused on checkpoint review decisions.

Example entry:

| Decision | Classroom version |
| --- | --- |

| Evidence | The teacher can point to purpose notes, prompt logs, alt text, captions, transcripts, diagram labels, slide roles, and peer review notes. |
| Boundary | The teacher names what checkpoint review decisions may change and what must stay protected or reviewed. |
| Maintenance | The next update is triggered by a date, failed check, student confusion, changed source, or media purpose map review. |

## Quality Check

Before moving on, test the work against this standard: the media makes the lesson easier to understand and can be used by students who need text, audio, visual, or pacing support.

A reviewer should be able to find the checkpoint review decisions decision, inspect the media purpose map evidence, and understand how it supports media purpose.

If the work only names a tool or a file, revise it. The artifact needs to show the teaching purpose, evidence, boundary, and maintenance routine.
