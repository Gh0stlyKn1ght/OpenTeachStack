# Checkpoint Review

## Why This Matters

Media belongs in a lesson when a teacher can point to the decision, the classroom use, the evidence, and the next review step.

In **Slides and Delivery Routines**, the work sits inside instructional media, AI image review, diagrams, slides, captions, transcripts, and accessibility checks. The risk is adding attractive media that distracts from the learning target, invents details, hides important information, or excludes students. This lesson narrows that risk into one visible update to **Delivery deck**.

## Core Idea

Run a checkpoint on the **Delivery deck**. Mark what is ready, what needs revision, what is blocked, and what evidence supports that decision.

The goal is not to make a perfect document in one pass. The goal is to make the teacher decision clear enough that another educator can inspect it, reuse it, or challenge it without needing a private explanation.

## Lesson Focus

Because this is the checkpoint for slides delivery routines, the teacher reviews the artifact as a working system. The checkpoint looks for missing decisions, unclear ownership, weak evidence, and maintenance steps that would be forgotten during a busy week.

In 04.4, focus on checkpoint review decisions. Choose one concrete classroom material or teacher decision from this list: an image, diagram, slide, short video, transcript, caption file, or media review row. Connect it to delivery deck, then decide whether it needs to be created, revised, tested, credited, restricted, published, or archived.

Connect checkpoint review decisions back to slides delivery routines. That connection matters because a polished artifact can still fail if the lesson purpose, boundary, evidence, and maintenance routine are not visible.

## Quality Check

Before moving on, test the work against this standard: the media makes the lesson easier to understand and can be used by students who need text, audio, visual, or pacing support.

A reviewer should be able to find the checkpoint review decisions decision, inspect the delivery deck evidence, and understand how it supports slides delivery routines.

If the work only names a tool or a file, revise it. The artifact needs to show the teaching purpose, evidence, boundary, and maintenance routine.
