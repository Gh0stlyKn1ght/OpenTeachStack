# Accessible Media Packet

## Why This Matters

Media belongs in a lesson when a teacher can point to the decision, the classroom use, the evidence, and the next review step.

In **Accessible Lesson Media Packet**, the work sits inside instructional media, AI image review, diagrams, slides, captions, transcripts, and accessibility checks. The risk is adding attractive media that distracts from the learning target, invents details, hides important information, or excludes students. This lesson narrows that risk into one visible update to **Accessible lesson media packet**.

## Core Idea

Build the **Accessible lesson media packet** as a usable artifact, not just a note. The finished version shows the decision, the classroom context, the evidence, and the next maintenance step.

The goal is not to make a perfect document in one pass. The goal is to make the teacher decision clear enough that another educator can inspect it, reuse it, or challenge it without needing a private explanation.

## Lesson Focus

Because this is an artifact lesson, the teacher turns accessible lesson media packet into something another educator can use. The artifact needs labels, examples, status, review notes, and a next action instead of a vague promise to improve later.

In 06.3, focus on accessible media packet. Choose one concrete classroom material or teacher decision from this list: an image, diagram, slide, short video, transcript, caption file, or media review row. Connect it to accessible lesson media packet, then decide whether it needs to be created, revised, tested, credited, restricted, published, or archived.

Connect accessible media packet back to accessible lesson media packet. That connection matters because a polished artifact can still fail if the lesson purpose, boundary, evidence, and maintenance routine are not visible.

## Classroom Example

Use this working situation: a teacher is building a media packet with one explanatory diagram, one delivery deck, one short video plan, and accessibility notes for each asset.

For **Accessible Media Packet**, choose one concrete classroom material or teacher decision from this list: an image, diagram, slide, short video, transcript, caption file, or media review row. Keep the example small enough to review during the lesson and focused on accessible media packet.

Example entry:

## Build The Work

Update the **Accessible lesson media packet** with one row or section for **Accessible Media Packet**.

| Field | Your note |
| --- | --- |
| Lesson focus | accessible media packet |
| Classroom context | Where accessible lesson media packet appears in your course, unit, tool, site, media packet, safety routine, or capstone |
| Working material | Classroom material or teacher decision: an image, diagram, slide, short video, transcript, caption file, or media review row |
| Teacher decision | What accessible media packet causes you to keep, revise, test, publish, restrict, or remove |
| Artifact status | What proves accessible lesson media packet is ready or what blocks it |
| Maintenance trigger | When the accessible media packet decision should be checked again |

## Quality Check

Before moving on, test the work against this standard: the media makes the lesson easier to understand and can be used by students who need text, audio, visual, or pacing support.

A reviewer should be able to find the accessible media packet decision, inspect the accessible lesson media packet evidence, and understand how it supports accessible lesson media packet.

If the work only names a tool or a file, revise it. The artifact needs to show the teaching purpose, evidence, boundary, and maintenance routine.
