# What Is a Threat Model?

## Why This Matters

Teacher safety work should reduce exposure when a teacher can point to the decision, the classroom use, the evidence, and the next review step.

In **Teacher Threat Model**, the work sits inside teacher digital safety, account hygiene, public profile review, connection safety, and incident response. The risk is turning safety into fear instead of reducing specific exposure with practical routines. This lesson narrows that risk into one visible update to **Teacher personal risk map**.

## Core Idea

Turn the lesson idea into an inspectable decision. Name the classroom use, the evidence, the review step, and the next revision trigger.

The goal is not to make a perfect document in one pass. The goal is to make the teacher decision clear enough that another educator can inspect it, reuse it, or challenge it without needing a private explanation.

## Lesson Focus

Because this is a focused section, the teacher isolates the what threat model decision from the rest of the course. The lesson asks what this idea changes, where it appears, and how the teacher will know it worked.

In 01.1, focus on what threat model. Choose one concrete classroom material or teacher decision from this list: an account, profile, device setting, website form, repository, domain record, or incident note. Connect it to teacher personal risk map, then decide whether it needs to be created, revised, tested, credited, restricted, published, or archived.

Connect what threat model back to teacher threat model. That connection matters because a polished artifact can still fail if the lesson purpose, boundary, evidence, and maintenance routine are not visible.

## Classroom Example

Use this working situation: a teacher is auditing accounts, public profiles, device settings, repo exposure, website forms, and response steps without collecting private student information.

For **What Is a Threat Model?**, choose one concrete classroom material or teacher decision from this list: an account, profile, device setting, website form, repository, domain record, or incident note. Keep the example small enough to review during the lesson and focused on what threat model.

## Quality Check

Before moving on, test the work against this standard: the action lowers a named risk, avoids unnecessary data collection, and gives the teacher a calm next step.

A reviewer should be able to find the what threat model decision, inspect the teacher personal risk map evidence, and understand how it supports teacher threat model.

If the work only names a tool or a file, revise it. The artifact needs to show the teaching purpose, evidence, boundary, and maintenance routine.
