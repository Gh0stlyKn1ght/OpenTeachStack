# Personal Risk Map

## Why This Matters

Teacher safety work should reduce exposure when a teacher can point to the decision, the classroom use, the evidence, and the next review step.

In **Teacher Threat Model**, the work sits inside teacher digital safety, account hygiene, public profile review, connection safety, and incident response. The risk is turning safety into fear instead of reducing specific exposure with practical routines. This lesson narrows that risk into one visible update to **Teacher personal risk map**.

## Core Idea

Build the **Teacher personal risk map** as a usable artifact, not just a note. The finished version shows the decision, the classroom context, the evidence, and the next maintenance step.

The goal is not to make a perfect document in one pass. The goal is to make the teacher decision clear enough that another educator can inspect it, reuse it, or challenge it without needing a private explanation.

## Lesson Focus

Because this is an artifact lesson, the teacher turns teacher personal risk map into something another educator can use. The artifact needs labels, examples, status, review notes, and a next action instead of a vague promise to improve later.

In 01.4, focus on personal risk map. Choose one concrete classroom material or teacher decision from this list: an account, profile, device setting, website form, repository, domain record, or incident note. Connect it to teacher personal risk map, then decide whether it needs to be created, revised, tested, credited, restricted, published, or archived.

Connect personal risk map back to teacher threat model. That connection matters because a polished artifact can still fail if the lesson purpose, boundary, evidence, and maintenance routine are not visible.

## Classroom Example

Use this working situation: a teacher is auditing accounts, public profiles, device settings, repo exposure, website forms, and response steps without collecting private student information.

For **Personal Risk Map**, choose one concrete classroom material or teacher decision from this list: an account, profile, device setting, website form, repository, domain record, or incident note. Keep the example small enough to review during the lesson and focused on personal risk map.

Example entry:

| Decision | Classroom version |
| --- | --- |

| Evidence | The teacher can point to risk map rows, account inventory status, MFA notes, public profile findings, website checks, and response contacts. |
| Boundary | The teacher names what personal risk map may change and what must stay protected or reviewed. |

## Completed Sample

| Risk area | Exposure | Calm action | Evidence | Review trigger |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Public profile | Personal email appears on an old classroom website. | Replace with school contact form or role-based email. | Screenshot-free note with page URL and change date. | Recheck after website updates or each semester. |
| Account reuse | Same password pattern used on two school-adjacent tools. | Move both accounts into password manager and change passwords. | Checklist row says unique password set; do not record the password. | Recheck after any account alert. |
| Student data | Public examples could be mistaken for real student work. | Label examples fictional and keep real work private. | Sample-only note in public packet. | Recheck before publishing examples. |
| Device access | Personal laptop browser still syncs old classroom accounts. | Remove unused profiles and sign out of retired accounts. | Device settings note with date. | Recheck after device replacement or shared use. |

## Ready, Revise, Blocked

| Status | Use when | Next action |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Ready | Risk is named, exposure is reduced, and no private data is stored in the map. | Keep review trigger on calendar. |
| Revise | Risk is real but action or evidence is vague. | Add one specific action and one proof note. |
| Blocked | The map requires sensitive details, passwords, or student identifiers to explain the risk. | Redact details and record only the action needed. |

## Quality Check

Before moving on, test the work against this standard: the action lowers a named risk, avoids unnecessary data collection, and gives the teacher a calm next step.

A reviewer should be able to find the personal risk map decision, inspect the teacher personal risk map evidence, and understand how it supports teacher threat model.

If the work only names a tool or a file, revise it. The artifact needs to show the teaching purpose, evidence, boundary, and maintenance routine.
