# Multi-Factor Authentication

## Core Idea

Password-only security depends on one thing not breaking.  
MFA adds a second signal so account compromise is less likely after a leak, guessed password, or reused credential.

## Do This

- List 4 account categories and mark MFA status for each: enabled, not enabled, planned, not applicable.
- Pick three priority accounts and choose the strongest available method for each.
- Compare authenticator apps, hardware tokens, and SMS for your context; avoid SMS-only when higher risk is present.
- Perform MFA setup for one high-value account and document your verification and backup method.

## Evidence of Completion

- A table listing account category, method, and verification evidence.
- One high-value account with MFA enabled.
- One fallback/recovery path documented for a later check-in.
- Notes on why the selected method is acceptable under your school/organization constraints.

## Safety Check

- Never store live backup codes, challenge responses, or MFA secrets in shared docs.
- Use generic labels for accounts in any evidence you share (`district-email`, `learning-platform`, etc.).
- If MFA locks you out unexpectedly, contact platform support and log the recovery workflow before retrying.
- Keep private recovery details private.

## Reflection

What MFA method gives your highest-risk account the best balance of reliability and security?
