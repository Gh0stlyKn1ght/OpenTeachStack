# Phishing Response Checklist

## Why This Matters

Teacher safety work should reduce exposure when a teacher can point to the decision, the classroom use, the evidence, and the next review step.

In **Email, Phishing, and Impersonation**, the work sits inside teacher digital safety, account hygiene, public profile review, connection safety, and incident response. The risk is turning safety into fear instead of reducing specific exposure with practical routines. This lesson narrows that risk into one visible update to **Phishing response checklist**.

## Core Idea

Build the **Phishing response checklist** as a usable artifact, not just a note. The finished version shows the decision, the classroom context, the evidence, and the next maintenance step.

The goal is not to make a perfect document in one pass. The goal is to make the teacher decision clear enough that another educator can inspect it, reuse it, or challenge it without needing a private explanation.

## Lesson Focus

Because this is an artifact lesson, the teacher turns phishing response checklist into something another educator can use. The artifact needs labels, examples, status, review notes, and a next action instead of a vague promise to improve later.

In 04.5, focus on phishing response checklist. Choose one concrete classroom material or teacher decision from this list: an account, profile, device setting, website form, repository, domain record, or incident note. Connect it to phishing response checklist, then decide whether it needs to be created, revised, tested, credited, restricted, published, or archived.

Connect phishing response checklist back to email phishing impersonation. That connection matters because a polished artifact can still fail if the lesson purpose, boundary, evidence, and maintenance routine are not visible.

## Completed Sample

| Step | Teacher action | Evidence to keep | Do not do |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Pause | Stop before clicking links, opening attachments, or replying. | Time received and sender display name. | Do not forward to students or paste into public spaces. |
| Inspect | Compare sender address, link destination, request, urgency, and grammar. | Screenshot-free notes or district-approved report. | Do not collect student examples or passwords. |
| Verify | Use known district contact channel or official help desk path. | Verification note with date and contact role. | Do not use phone numbers or links from the suspicious message. |
| Report | Use district phishing/reporting process. | Report confirmation or ticket number if allowed. | Do not shame the sender or speculate publicly. |
| Recover | If clicked, change password, check MFA, and notify support. | Recovery actions completed. | Do not keep using the same session if account compromise is possible. |

## Triage Examples

| Message pattern | Decision | Reason |
| --- | --- | --- |
| "Your mailbox will close today. Sign in here." | Report | Urgent account threat and suspicious link. |
| Principal asks for gift cards from a personal email. | Verify and report | Impersonation risk; use known school contact. |
| Known vendor sends renewal notice from expected domain. | Verify if unsure | Check through official vendor portal, not email link. |

## Quality Check

Before moving on, test the work against this standard: the action lowers a named risk, avoids unnecessary data collection, and gives the teacher a calm next step.

A reviewer should be able to find the phishing response checklist decision, inspect the phishing response checklist evidence, and understand how it supports email phishing impersonation.

If the work only names a tool or a file, revise it. The artifact needs to show the teaching purpose, evidence, boundary, and maintenance routine.
