# Checkpoint Review

## Why This Matters

Teacher safety work should reduce exposure when a teacher can point to the decision, the classroom use, the evidence, and the next review step.

In **Email, Phishing, and Impersonation**, the work sits inside teacher digital safety, account hygiene, public profile review, connection safety, and incident response. The risk is turning safety into fear instead of reducing specific exposure with practical routines. This lesson narrows that risk into one visible update to **Phishing response checklist**.

## Core Idea

Run a checkpoint on the **Phishing response checklist**. Mark what is ready, what needs revision, what is blocked, and what evidence supports that decision.

The goal is not to make a perfect document in one pass. The goal is to make the teacher decision clear enough that another educator can inspect it, reuse it, or challenge it without needing a private explanation.

## Lesson Focus

Because this is the checkpoint for email phishing impersonation, the teacher reviews the artifact as a working system. The checkpoint looks for missing decisions, unclear ownership, weak evidence, and maintenance steps that would be forgotten during a busy week.

In 04.6, focus on checkpoint review decisions. Choose one concrete classroom material or teacher decision from this list: an account, profile, device setting, website form, repository, domain record, or incident note. Connect it to phishing response checklist, then decide whether it needs to be created, revised, tested, credited, restricted, published, or archived.

Connect checkpoint review decisions back to email phishing impersonation. That connection matters because a polished artifact can still fail if the lesson purpose, boundary, evidence, and maintenance routine are not visible.
