# checkpoint review

## Why This Matters

A classroom educator reducing personal and classroom-facing digital risk needs more than a tool list or a checklist. The goal in **Email, Phishing, and Impersonation** is to make a classroom system that can be used, inspected, revised, and handed to another educator without needing a meeting first.

For **checkpoint review**, the practical pressure is simple: investigating live threats alone, exposing private details in public notes, or treating security advice as one-size-fits-all policy. This page turns that pressure into a visible update to **Phishing response checklist**.

## Classroom Scenario

Use this situation as the working example: a redacted teacher safety review that maps public usernames, MFA status, suspicious-message handling, website exposure, and escalation contacts. By the end, the work should support this habit: stop, verify, document safely, escalate correctly, and revise the public surface.

In this scenario, the learner should be able to point to:

- what the teacher prepares before students see the work,
- what students will read, build, submit, or discuss,
- what evidence shows the work is ready,
- what gets revised after the first use.

## Key Idea

Teacher safety work should reduce exposure without turning private incidents into public artifacts. Use fictional examples, redaction, and escalation boundaries.

Watch for this mistake: Clicking through suspicious links, pasting private email into AI tools, or publishing screenshots with identifying details.

## What You Will Do

Make this page concrete by writing three small decisions:

- Before class, connect checkpoint review to one setup decision in the phishing response checklist: what the teacher prepares, where it lives, and which source or safety check keeps it honest.
- During class, connect checkpoint review to one student action in accounts, public profiles, email, devices, Wi-Fi, websites, repositories, domains, and incident notes: what learners read, build, submit, discuss, or revise while working through a redacted teacher safety review that maps public usernames, MFA status, suspicious-message handling, website exposure, and escalation contacts.
- After class, review checkpoint review through the email phishing and impersonation lens: what evidence proves the move worked, which mistake to avoid (clicking through suspicious links, pasting private email into AI tools, or publishing screenshots with identifying details), and what revision supports stop, verify, document safely, escalate correctly, and revise the public surface.

## Build the Artifact

Inspect **Phishing response checklist** and mark what is ready, what needs revision, and what must be checked against sources, safety, privacy, or accessibility before use.

Add this to your artifact:

- Classroom context: accounts, public profiles, email, devices, Wi-Fi, websites, repositories, domains, and incident notes; a redacted teacher safety review that maps public usernames, MFA status, suspicious-message handling, website exposure, and escalation contacts.
- Artifact entry: In **Phishing response checklist**, add the decision for **checkpoint review** with the student-facing evidence and the review owner.
- Example entry: Safety entry: Suspicious message reported through district channel; screenshot stored privately with names, links, and timestamps redacted for training notes.
- Review check: Check the entry against district IT policy, FTC/CISA/NIST guidance, platform security documentation, and escalation boundaries, then write what should change after the first classroom use.
- Peer review check: A colleague should be able to open the email phishing and impersonation artifact, understand the decision behind **checkpoint review**, and know the next action without extra explanation.

## What Good Work Looks Like

A strong **checkpoint review** entry is concrete enough that another teacher can inspect it. It names the class context, the artifact location, the student product, the source or safety check, and the revision trigger.

For this lesson, a strong entry might say:

> Safety entry: Suspicious message reported through district channel; screenshot stored privately with names, links, and timestamps redacted for training notes.

That level of detail is enough to review. It is also small enough to revise after one classroom use.

## Quality Check

Before moving on, confirm:

- Your artifact entry is stored in **Phishing response checklist** rather than kept as a private reminder.
- The student-facing task or evidence is named in plain language.
- The review step names a source, safety, privacy, accessibility, or maintenance check.
- The checkpoint distinguishes ready, revise, and blocked items.
- The next revision has an owner and a realistic date or trigger.
