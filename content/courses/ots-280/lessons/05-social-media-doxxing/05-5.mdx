# Public Profile Audit

## Why This Matters

A classroom educator reducing personal and classroom-facing digital risk needs more than a tool list or a checklist. The goal in **Social Media and Doxxing Risk** is to make a classroom system that can be used, inspected, revised, and handed to another educator without needing a meeting first.

For **Public Profile Audit**, the practical pressure is simple: investigating live threats alone, exposing private details in public notes, or treating security advice as one-size-fits-all policy. This page turns that pressure into a visible update to **Public profile audit**.

## Classroom Scenario

Use this situation as the working example: a redacted teacher safety review that maps public usernames, MFA status, suspicious-message handling, website exposure, and escalation contacts. By the end, the work should support this habit: stop, verify, document safely, escalate correctly, and revise the public surface.

In this scenario, the learner should be able to point to:

- what the teacher prepares before students see the work,
- what students will read, build, submit, or discuss,
- what evidence shows the work is ready,
- what gets revised after the first use.

## Key Idea

Teacher safety work should reduce exposure without turning private incidents into public artifacts. Use fictional examples, redaction, and escalation boundaries.

Watch for this mistake: Clicking through suspicious links, pasting private email into AI tools, or publishing screenshots with identifying details.

## What You Will Do

Make this page concrete by writing three small decisions:

- Before class, connect public profile audit to one setup decision in the public profile audit: what the teacher prepares, where it lives, and which source or safety check keeps it honest.
- During class, connect public profile audit to one student action in accounts, public profiles, email, devices, Wi-Fi, websites, repositories, domains, and incident notes: what learners read, build, submit, discuss, or revise while working through a redacted teacher safety review that maps public usernames, MFA status, suspicious-message handling, website exposure, and escalation contacts.
- After class, review public profile audit through the social media and doxxing risk lens: what evidence proves the move worked, which mistake to avoid (clicking through suspicious links, pasting private email into AI tools, or publishing screenshots with identifying details), and what revision supports stop, verify, document safely, escalate correctly, and revise the public surface.

## What Good Work Looks Like

A strong **public profile audit** entry is concrete enough that another teacher can inspect it. It names the class context, the artifact location, the student product, the source or safety check, and the revision trigger.

For this lesson, a strong entry might say:

> Safety entry: Suspicious message reported through district channel; screenshot stored privately with names, links, and timestamps redacted for training notes.

That level of detail is enough to review. It is also small enough to revise after one classroom use.
