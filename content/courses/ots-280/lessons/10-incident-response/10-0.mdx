# Chapter Plan

## Why This Matters

Teacher safety work should reduce exposure when a teacher can point to the decision, the classroom use, the evidence, and the next review step.

In **Incident Response for Teachers**, the work sits inside teacher digital safety, account hygiene, public profile review, connection safety, and incident response. The risk is turning safety into fear instead of reducing specific exposure with practical routines. This lesson narrows that risk into one visible update to **What to do if something happens plan**.

## Core Idea

Map the chapter before building. Choose one classroom material or teacher decision from this list: an account, profile, device setting, website form, repository, domain record, or incident note. Then name the classroom purpose it serves and the evidence that will show whether the work is ready.

The goal is not to make a perfect document in one pass. The goal is to make the teacher decision clear enough that another educator can inspect it, reuse it, or challenge it without needing a private explanation.

## Lesson Focus

Because this is the opening plan for incident response teachers, the teacher names the sequence before creating files or artifacts. The plan separates what will be learned, what will be built, and what evidence will prove that the build is ready.

In 10.0, focus on chapter planning decisions. Choose one concrete classroom material or teacher decision from this list: an account, profile, device setting, website form, repository, domain record, or incident note. Connect it to incident response, then decide whether it needs to be created, revised, tested, credited, restricted, published, or archived.

Connect chapter planning decisions back to incident response teachers. That connection matters because a polished artifact can still fail if the lesson purpose, boundary, evidence, and maintenance routine are not visible.

## Practice Task

Open your working **What to do if something happens plan** and add one concrete classroom row for **Chapter Plan**. Include the student-facing action, the evidence a teacher can inspect, and the exact place where the artifact or note will live.

Then label the row **ready**, **revise**, or **blocked**. Add one sentence explaining the label so a reviewer can act without needing a live explanation.

## Quality Check

Before moving on, test the work against this standard: the action lowers a named risk, avoids unnecessary data collection, and gives the teacher a calm next step.

A reviewer should be able to find the chapter planning decisions decision, inspect the incident response evidence, and understand how it supports incident response teachers.

If the work only names a tool or a file, revise it. The artifact needs to show the teaching purpose, evidence, boundary, and maintenance routine.
