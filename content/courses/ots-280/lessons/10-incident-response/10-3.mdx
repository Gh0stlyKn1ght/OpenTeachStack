# Documenting Without Making It Worse

## Why This Matters

Teacher safety work should reduce exposure when a teacher can point to the decision, the classroom use, the evidence, and the next review step.

In **Incident Response for Teachers**, the work sits inside teacher digital safety, account hygiene, public profile review, connection safety, and incident response. The risk is turning safety into fear instead of reducing specific exposure with practical routines. This lesson narrows that risk into one visible update to **What to do if something happens plan**.

## Core Idea

Turn the lesson idea into an inspectable decision. Name the classroom use, the evidence, the review step, and the next revision trigger.

The goal is not to make a perfect document in one pass. The goal is to make the teacher decision clear enough that another educator can inspect it, reuse it, or challenge it without needing a private explanation.

## Lesson Focus

Because this is a focused section, the teacher isolates the documenting without making worse decision from the rest of the course. The lesson asks what this idea changes, where it appears, and how the teacher will know it worked.

In 10.3, focus on documenting without making worse. Choose one concrete classroom material or teacher decision from this list: an account, profile, device setting, website form, repository, domain record, or incident note. Connect it to incident response, then decide whether it needs to be created, revised, tested, credited, restricted, published, or archived.

Connect documenting without making worse back to incident response teachers. That connection matters because a polished artifact can still fail if the lesson purpose, boundary, evidence, and maintenance routine are not visible.

## Classroom Example

Use this working situation: a teacher is auditing accounts, public profiles, device settings, repo exposure, website forms, and response steps without collecting private student information.

## Quality Check

Before moving on, test the work against this standard: the action lowers a named risk, avoids unnecessary data collection, and gives the teacher a calm next step.

A reviewer should be able to find the documenting without making worse decision, inspect the incident response evidence, and understand how it supports incident response teachers.

If the work only names a tool or a file, revise it. The artifact needs to show the teaching purpose, evidence, boundary, and maintenance routine.
