# Readable Pages and Links

## Why This Matters

A course site is a maintained teaching surface when a teacher can point to the decision, the classroom use, the evidence, and the next review step.

In **Accessibility and Safety**, the work sits inside course websites, static hosting, course hub structure, accessibility, privacy, and maintenance. The risk is treating a live website as finished when links, privacy, accessibility, source notes, and update routines are still unclear. This lesson narrows that risk into one visible update to **Publishing safety checklist**.

## Core Idea

Turn the lesson idea into an inspectable decision. Name the classroom use, the evidence, the review step, and the next revision trigger.

The goal is not to make a perfect document in one pass. The goal is to make the teacher decision clear enough that another educator can inspect it, reuse it, or challenge it without needing a private explanation.

## Lesson Focus

Because this is a focused section, the teacher isolates the readable pages links decision from the rest of the course. The lesson asks what this idea changes, where it appears, and how the teacher will know it worked.

In 04.1, focus on readable pages links. Choose one concrete classroom material or teacher decision from this list: a page, link, navigation item, resource entry, deployment target, or maintenance task. Connect it to publishing safety checklist, then decide whether it needs to be created, revised, tested, credited, restricted, published, or archived.

Connect readable pages links back to accessibility safety. That connection matters because a polished artifact can still fail if the lesson purpose, boundary, evidence, and maintenance routine are not visible.

## Common Mistake

A common mistake in **Readable Pages and Links** is treating the visible file or activity as proof that the teaching system is ready. The reviewer still needs to see the classroom purpose, the artifact evidence, the privacy or safety boundary, and the next revision trigger.

For this lesson, do not mark **Publishing safety checklist** ready until another educator can explain what changed, why it matters for **Accessibility and Safety**, and what would cause the work to be revised.

## Quality Check

Before moving on, test the work against this standard: students can find the current material, sensitive files are not public, links work, and the teacher knows when to update the site.

A reviewer should be able to find the readable pages links decision, inspect the publishing safety checklist evidence, and understand how it supports accessibility safety.

If the work only names a tool or a file, revise it. The artifact needs to show the teaching purpose, evidence, boundary, and maintenance routine.
