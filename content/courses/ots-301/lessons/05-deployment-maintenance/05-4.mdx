# Checkpoint Review

## Why This Matters

A course site is a maintained teaching surface when a teacher can point to the decision, the classroom use, the evidence, and the next review step.

In **Deployment and Maintenance**, the work sits inside course websites, static hosting, course hub structure, accessibility, privacy, and maintenance. The risk is treating a live website as finished when links, privacy, accessibility, source notes, and update routines are still unclear. This lesson narrows that risk into one visible update to **Maintenance plan**.

## Core Idea

Run a checkpoint on the **Maintenance plan**. Mark what is ready, what needs revision, what is blocked, and what evidence supports that decision.

The goal is not to make a perfect document in one pass. The goal is to make the teacher decision clear enough that another educator can inspect it, reuse it, or challenge it without needing a private explanation.

## Lesson Focus

Because this is the checkpoint for deployment maintenance, the teacher reviews the artifact as a working system. The checkpoint looks for missing decisions, unclear ownership, weak evidence, and maintenance steps that would be forgotten during a busy week.

In 05.4, focus on checkpoint review decisions. Choose one concrete classroom material or teacher decision from this list: a page, link, navigation item, resource entry, deployment target, or maintenance task. Connect it to maintenance, then decide whether it needs to be created, revised, tested, credited, restricted, published, or archived.

Connect checkpoint review decisions back to deployment maintenance. That connection matters because a polished artifact can still fail if the lesson purpose, boundary, evidence, and maintenance routine are not visible.

## Classroom Example

Use this working situation: a teacher is publishing a course hub with a homepage, course pages, resource pages, safety checks, release notes, and a maintenance calendar.

For **Checkpoint Review**, choose one concrete classroom material or teacher decision from this list: a page, link, navigation item, resource entry, deployment target, or maintenance task. Keep the example small enough to review during the lesson and focused on checkpoint review decisions.

## Quality Check

Before moving on, test the work against this standard: students can find the current material, sensitive files are not public, links work, and the teacher knows when to update the site.

A reviewer should be able to find the checkpoint review decisions decision, inspect the maintenance evidence, and understand how it supports deployment maintenance.

If the work only names a tool or a file, revise it. The artifact needs to show the teaching purpose, evidence, boundary, and maintenance routine.
