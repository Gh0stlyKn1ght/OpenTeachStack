# checkpoint review

## Why This Matters

An educator assembling a publishable curriculum system needs more than a tool list or a checklist. The goal in **Capstone Scope** is to make a classroom system that can be used, inspected, revised, and handed to another educator without needing a meeting first.

For **checkpoint review**, the practical pressure is simple: calling the capstone finished because files exist, even though a reviewer cannot trace the decisions without the author explaining hidden context. This page turns that pressure into a visible update to **Capstone proposal**.

## Classroom Scenario

Use this situation as the working example: a robotics troubleshooting mini-unit capstone with lesson links, source notes, fake-data evidence, privacy review, peer feedback, and final release decisions. By the end, the work should support this habit: make need, evidence, safety, feedback, release status, and maintenance traceable.

In this scenario, the learner should be able to point to:

- what the teacher prepares before students see the work,
- what students will read, build, submit, or discuss,
- what evidence shows the work is ready,
- what gets revised after the first use.

## Key Idea

A capstone proves the system can be inspected by someone else. Every artifact needs purpose, location, evidence, review status, and maintenance owner.

Watch for this mistake: Submitting a folder of finished-looking files without release status, review notes, or a maintenance plan.

## What You Will Do

Make this page concrete by writing three small decisions:

- Before class, connect checkpoint review to one setup decision in the capstone proposal: what the teacher prepares, where it lives, and which source or safety check keeps it honest.
- During class, connect checkpoint review to one student action in proposal, artifact index, system map, technical evidence, safety review, peer feedback, and maintenance plan: what learners read, build, submit, discuss, or revise while working through a robotics troubleshooting mini-unit capstone with lesson links, source notes, fake-data evidence, privacy review, peer feedback, and final release decisions.
- After class, review checkpoint review through the capstone scope lens: what evidence proves the move worked, which mistake to avoid (submitting a folder of finished-looking files without release status, review notes, or a maintenance plan), and what revision supports make need, evidence, safety, feedback, release status, and maintenance traceable.

## Build the Artifact

Inspect **Capstone proposal** and mark what is ready, what needs revision, and what must be checked against sources, safety, privacy, or accessibility before use.

Add this to your artifact:

- Classroom context: proposal, artifact index, system map, technical evidence, safety review, peer feedback, and maintenance plan; a robotics troubleshooting mini-unit capstone with lesson links, source notes, fake-data evidence, privacy review, peer feedback, and final release decisions.
- Artifact entry: In **Capstone proposal**, add the decision for **checkpoint review** with the student-facing evidence and the review owner.
- Example entry: Artifact index row: Sensor mini-unit map / ready / proves sequence and assessment alignment / reviewed by peer / revisit after first classroom use.
- Review check: Check the entry against course artifacts, source notes, release checks, peer review notes, and platform documentation, then write what should change after the first classroom use.
- Peer review check: A colleague should be able to open the capstone scope artifact, understand the decision behind **checkpoint review**, and know the next action without extra explanation.

## What Good Work Looks Like

A strong **checkpoint review** entry is concrete enough that another teacher can inspect it. It names the class context, the artifact location, the student product, the source or safety check, and the revision trigger.

For this lesson, a strong entry might say:

> Artifact index row: Sensor mini-unit map / ready / proves sequence and assessment alignment / reviewed by peer / revisit after first classroom use.

That level of detail is enough to review. It is also small enough to revise after one classroom use.

## Quality Check

Before moving on, confirm:

- Your artifact entry is stored in **Capstone proposal** rather than kept as a private reminder.
- The student-facing task or evidence is named in plain language.
- The review step names a source, safety, privacy, accessibility, or maintenance check.
- The checkpoint distinguishes ready, revise, and blocked items.
- The next revision has an owner and a realistic date or trigger.

## Reflection

What would a busy teacher misunderstand if they only saw your **Capstone proposal** entry and not your explanation? Revise the entry until the classroom task, evidence, review step, and next improvement are visible.
