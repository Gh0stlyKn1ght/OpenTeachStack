# Release Safety Review

## Why This Matters

An educator assembling a publishable curriculum system needs more than a tool list or a checklist. The goal in **Publishing and Safety Review** is to make a classroom system that can be used, inspected, revised, and handed to another educator without needing a meeting first.

For **Release Safety Review**, the practical pressure is simple: calling the capstone finished because files exist, even though a reviewer cannot trace the decisions without the author explaining hidden context. This page turns that pressure into a visible update to **Release safety review**.

## Classroom Scenario

Use this situation as the working example: a robotics troubleshooting mini-unit capstone with lesson links, source notes, fake-data evidence, privacy review, peer feedback, and final release decisions. By the end, the work should support this habit: make need, evidence, safety, feedback, release status, and maintenance traceable.

In this scenario, the learner should be able to point to:

- what the teacher prepares before students see the work,
- what students will read, build, submit, or discuss,
- what evidence shows the work is ready,
- what gets revised after the first use.

## Key Idea

A capstone proves the system can be inspected by someone else. Every artifact needs purpose, location, evidence, review status, and maintenance owner.

Watch for this mistake: Submitting a folder of finished-looking files without release status, review notes, or a maintenance plan.

Make this page concrete by writing three small decisions:

- Before class, connect release safety review to one setup decision in the release safety review: what the teacher prepares, where it lives, and which source or safety check keeps it honest.
- During class, connect release safety review to one student action in proposal, artifact index, system map, technical evidence, safety review, peer feedback, and maintenance plan: what learners read, build, submit, discuss, or revise while working through a robotics troubleshooting mini-unit capstone with lesson links, source notes, fake-data evidence, privacy review, peer feedback, and final release decisions.
- After class, review release safety review through the publishing and safety review lens: what evidence proves the move worked, which mistake to avoid (submitting a folder of finished-looking files without release status, review notes, or a maintenance plan), and what revision supports make need, evidence, safety, feedback, release status, and maintenance traceable.

## What Good Work Looks Like

A strong **release safety review** entry is concrete enough that another teacher can inspect it. It names the class context, the artifact location, the student product, the source or safety check, and the revision trigger.

For this lesson, a strong entry might say:

> Artifact index row: Sensor mini-unit map / ready / proves sequence and assessment alignment / reviewed by peer / revisit after first classroom use.

That level of detail is enough to review. It is also small enough to revise after one classroom use.
