# Presentation and Review Log

## Why This Matters

A capstone proves the system when a teacher can point to the decision, the classroom use, the evidence, and the next review step.

In **Presentation and Peer Review**, the work sits inside capstone assembly across curriculum, automation, publishing, safety, peer review, and final release. The risk is calling the capstone finished because files exist even though reviewers cannot trace the decisions. This lesson narrows that risk into one visible update to **Presentation and peer review log**.

## Core Idea

Build the **Presentation and peer review log** as a usable artifact, not just a note. The finished version shows the decision, the classroom context, the evidence, and the next maintenance step.

The goal is not to make a perfect document in one pass. The goal is to make the teacher decision clear enough that another educator can inspect it, reuse it, or challenge it without needing a private explanation.

## Lesson Focus

Because this is an artifact lesson, the teacher turns presentation peer log into something another educator can use. The artifact needs labels, examples, status, review notes, and a next action instead of a vague promise to improve later.

In 05.3, focus on presentation log. Choose one concrete classroom material or teacher decision from this list: a capstone artifact, system link, evidence entry, safety review item, presentation slide, or release checklist row. Connect it to presentation peer log, then decide whether it needs to be created, revised, tested, credited, restricted, published, or archived.

Connect presentation log back to presentation peer. That connection matters because a polished artifact can still fail if the lesson purpose, boundary, evidence, and maintenance routine are not visible.

## Classroom Example

Use this working situation: a teacher assembles a published curriculum system with linked artifacts, technical evidence, safety review, presentation notes, peer feedback, and maintenance plans.

For **Presentation and Review Log**, choose one concrete classroom material or teacher decision from this list: a capstone artifact, system link, evidence entry, safety review item, presentation slide, or release checklist row. Keep the example small enough to review during the lesson and focused on presentation log.

## Peer Review Log Rows

| Review focus | Ready evidence | Revise evidence | Blocked evidence |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Teaching need | Problem statement and student evidence are traceable. | Need is real but success signal is vague. | Reviewer cannot tell what classroom problem is being solved. |
| Artifact index | Every artifact has link, owner, status, and evidence. | One artifact is missing owner or maintenance trigger. | Public index links to private or restricted files. |
| Technical evidence | Fake-data test and rollback note are visible. | Test works but reviewer cannot see failure path. | Script uses live data or live folders during review. |
| Release safety | Privacy, license, and access checks are visible. | One license or accessibility note is missing. | Public packet exposes restricted material. |

## Presenter Follow-Up Note

After review, add one sentence that starts with the decision:

> Revise: the packet can move to final release review after the resource evaluation sheet includes a license note and access date.

## Quality Check

Before moving on, test the work against this standard: a reviewer can trace the teaching need, inspect each artifact, see the safety checks, and understand how the system will be maintained.

A reviewer should be able to find the presentation log decision, inspect the presentation peer log evidence, and understand how it supports presentation peer.

If the work only names a tool or a file, revise it. The artifact needs to show the teaching purpose, evidence, boundary, and maintenance routine.
