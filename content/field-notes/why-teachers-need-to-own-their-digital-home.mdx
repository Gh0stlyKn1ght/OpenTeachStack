# Why Teachers Need to Own Their Digital Home

I have watched three different learning platforms shut down or radically change their pricing in the last five years. Each time, teachers scrambled to migrate their materials, lost student-facing links, and spent weekends rebuilding what should have been permanent.

This is not a technology problem. It is an ownership problem.

## The Rental Trap

When you build your curriculum presence on someone else's platform, you are renting:

- **Google Sites** — Google controls the URL format and can deprecate the product (they have before)
- **Notion** — Your URL is `yourname.notion.site` — Notion owns the namespace
- **Canvas / Schoology / Google Classroom** — When you leave the district, your content stays behind
- **Teachers Pay Teachers** — They control discovery, pricing, and terms

None of these are your address. They are someone else's building where you are allowed to have a room — until the lease terms change.

## What Ownership Means

Owning your digital home means:

1. **You own the domain.** `nevarez.dev` or `mrcurriculum.com` — registered in your name, paid by you.
2. **You control the hosting.** Your files live on a platform you can switch (Vercel, Netlify, GitHub Pages).
3. **You control the content.** Your materials are in files you can move, fork, or publish anywhere.
4. **URLs are permanent.** If a student bookmarks a page in September, it works in June. And next year.

This costs roughly $12/year for a domain and $0/year for hosting on Vercel or GitHub Pages. Less than one month of most ed-tech subscriptions.

## The Career Argument

Teachers change schools. Teachers change districts. Teachers change states. Some leave teaching and come back.

If your curriculum lives on a school-provided platform, your digital work history resets every time you move. You lose:

- The resources you curated
- The assessments you refined
- The student-facing links you shared
- The professional presence you built

With your own domain, your work follows you. A curriculum site at `mrcurriculum.com` works whether you teach in Texas or Oregon, whether your district uses Canvas or Google Classroom, whether you are in year two or year twenty.

## The Practical First Step

You do not need to build a full course site today. Start with this:

1. Buy a domain (~$12/year)
2. Point it to a simple static page (Next.js on Vercel, free)
3. Put your name, your subject, and a few links to your best resources
4. Share that URL instead of platform-specific links

That is your digital home. Everything else builds from there.

## The Real Lesson

The tools will change. The platforms will change. The district-mandated systems will change.

Your domain does not change. Your files do not change. Your curriculum system — if you build it on infrastructure you own — does not change.

That is why ownership matters more than any individual tool recommendation.
