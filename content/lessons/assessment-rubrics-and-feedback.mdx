# Assessment, Rubrics, and Feedback

Assessment is not the thing you bolt onto the end of a unit. It is how you prove the unit taught what it claimed to teach.

In OTS-101, keep assessment small and honest. You are not building a full testing system. You are building enough evidence to know whether students met the learning targets.

## Start with the Target

For each learning target in your mini-unit, ask:

- What would a student have to do to prove this?
- Is that evidence observable?
- Does the task match the verb in the standard?
- Would another teacher understand what success looks like?

If the target says students will "analyze," a recall quiz is not enough. If the target says students will "identify," a long essay may be more work than the target requires.

## Build a Small Assessment Set

For the mini-unit capstone, choose one or two assessment types:

- exit ticket
- short quiz
- project checkpoint
- performance task
- discussion response
- student reflection

Each assessment should connect to a named learning target. Do not assess everything because everything feels important.

## Rubric Quality Check

A useful rubric has:

- 3 to 5 criteria
- observable language
- clear performance levels
- no mystery words like "excellent" without evidence
- room for feedback that tells students what to revise

<RealityCheck>
AI can draft a rubric quickly, but AI rubrics often sound polished while measuring vague effort. Your job is to make the criteria observable.
</RealityCheck>

## Feedback Loops

Feedback is part of the curriculum system. Decide when students will receive feedback and what they can do with it.

Good feedback routines include:

- one checkpoint before the final submission
- one short teacher comment tied to the rubric
- one required student revision decision
- one reflection question after revision

## Safety and Quality Checklist

Before adding an assessment to your mini-unit, check:

- Privacy: no student data goes into AI tools.
- Copyright: any borrowed prompt, passage, image, or scenario is allowed and attributed.
- AI verification: questions, answer keys, and rubric criteria are reviewed by you.
- Standards alignment: every assessment item maps to a learning target.
- Accessibility: instructions are clear, readable, and usable with assistive tools.
- Revision log: you can name what changed between the AI draft and your final version.
