# Mini-Unit Capstone

This is the final OTS-101 project. You will build a small curriculum system for a real topic you teach.

Not a full course site. Not an automation suite. Not a public publishing requirement. A focused mini-unit system with enough structure, evidence, and review to be usable in a classroom.

## What You Will Build

Your capstone must include:

1. **Teacher workflow audit** — the planning problem this mini-unit helps solve
2. **Standards unpacking sheet** — real standards converted into learning targets
3. **Mini-unit map** — 3 to 5 lessons with sequence, outcomes, and checks
4. **Reusable lesson template** — the pattern your lessons follow
5. **Prompt library** — prompts you used, revised, and would reuse
6. **Resource evaluation sheet** — sources checked for quality, license, accessibility, and alignment
7. **Assessment or quiz draft** — one assessment connected to learning targets
8. **Rubric** — observable criteria for evaluating student work
9. **AI verification checklist** — hallucination, citation, bias, and standards checks
10. **Accessibility/privacy/copyright checklist** — safety checks before classroom use
11. **Delivery plan** — pacing, student-facing directions, feedback, and backup plan
12. **Reflection and revision log** — what changed, why it changed, and what you still need to improve

## What Is Not Required

OTS-101 does not require:

- Apps Script automation
- GitHub publishing
- custom domains
- Next.js or Docusaurus
- a live public website
- OBS or full video production
- Codex, Claude Code, or AI coding agents

Those are later pathway courses. You can preview them, but the foundations capstone should prove curriculum judgment first.

## Phase 1: Choose the Unit

Choose a topic you teach or want to teach. It should be:

- small enough to fit in 3 to 5 lessons
- tied to real standards or course outcomes
- specific enough to assess
- familiar enough that you can judge AI output

<ReflectionPrompt>
What is one topic students often misunderstand in your class? A focused trouble spot usually makes a better first mini-unit than a broad course overview.
</ReflectionPrompt>

## Phase 2: Map the Learning

Create your standards unpacking sheet and mini-unit map:

```
Mini-Unit Title: _______________
Audience: _______________
Duration: 3 to 5 lessons

Standard or Outcome: _______________
Learning Target 1: _______________
Learning Target 2: _______________
Learning Target 3: _______________

Lesson 1: _______________
Lesson 2: _______________
Lesson 3: _______________
Optional Lesson 4: _______________
Optional Lesson 5: _______________

Assessment Evidence: _______________
```

## Phase 3: Build the Artifacts

Use the OTS-101 template system to assemble your working materials:

- lesson template
- prompt library
- resource evaluation sheet
- assessment or quiz draft
- rubric
- delivery plan

Every artifact should be small enough that another teacher could understand it without a meeting.

## Phase 4: Verify and Revise

Before calling the capstone complete, run the safety and quality checks:

| Check | Evidence |
| --- | --- |
| Privacy | No student-identifiable data was entered into AI tools |
| Copyright/licensing | Borrowed resources are permitted and attributed |
| AI verification | AI claims, examples, answer keys, citations, and standards references were checked |
| Standards alignment | Lessons and assessment map to learning targets |
| Accessibility | Directions, links, images, and materials are usable and readable |
| Revision log | You documented what changed after review |

<RealityCheck>
The goal is not a perfect unit. The goal is a unit you understand, can defend, and can improve. If you cannot explain why an artifact is in the mini-unit, revise it or remove it.
</RealityCheck>

## Evaluation Criteria

Use this rubric to self-audit or peer-review the capstone:

| Category | Strong Evidence |
| --- | --- |
| Standards alignment | Learning targets are specific, teachable, and connected to assessment |
| Curriculum architecture | Lessons form a coherent sequence instead of a pile of activities |
| Resource literacy | Resources are credible, attributed, accessible, and useful |
| AI workflow | Prompts are documented, output is revised, and teacher voice is preserved |
| Assessment quality | The assessment measures the learning target and the rubric uses observable criteria |
| Delivery readiness | Directions, pacing, materials, feedback, and backup plans are clear |
| Safety | Privacy, copyright, accessibility, and verification checks are complete |
| Reflection | The revision log shows teacher judgment and ownership |

<TeacherNote>
A small, honest mini-unit is better than an ambitious shell full of placeholders. OTS-101 should help you build something teachable, not make you perform technical mastery you have not learned yet.
</TeacherNote>

## After OTS-101

Once your mini-unit is solid, choose a next pathway step:

- OTS-201 for deeper Google Workspace systems
- OTS-220 for Apps Script automation
- OTS-240 for GitHub and open-resource workflows
- OTS-260 for AI media and delivery production
- OTS-301 for course sites, domains, and hosting
- OTS-320 for AI coding agents
- OTS-399 for the full publishable pathway capstone

You are now a teacher who builds, verifies, revises, and owns the work.
