# Mini-Unit Capstone

This is the final OTS-101 project. You will build a small curriculum system for a real topic you teach.

Not a full course site. Not an automation suite. Not a public publishing requirement. A focused mini-unit system with enough structure, evidence, and review to be usable in a classroom.

## What You Will Build

Your capstone must include:

- **Teacher workflow audit** — the planning problem this mini-unit helps solve
- **Standards unpacking sheet** — real standards converted into learning targets
- **Mini-unit map** — 3 to 5 lessons with sequence, outcomes, and checks
- **Reusable lesson template** — the pattern your lessons follow
- **Prompt library** — prompts you used, revised, and would reuse
- **Resource evaluation sheet** — sources checked for quality, license, accessibility, and alignment
- **Assessment or quiz draft** — one assessment connected to learning targets
- **Rubric** — observable criteria for evaluating student work
- **AI verification checklist** — hallucination, citation, bias, and standards checks
- **Accessibility/privacy/copyright checklist** — safety checks before classroom use
- **Delivery plan** — pacing, student-facing directions, feedback, and backup plan
- **Reflection and revision log** — what changed, why it changed, and what you still need to improve

## What Is Not Required

OTS-101 does not require:

- Apps Script automation
- GitHub publishing
- custom domains
- Next.js or Docusaurus
- a live public website
- OBS or full video production
- Codex, Claude Code, or AI coding agents

Those are later pathway courses. You can preview them, but the foundations capstone should prove curriculum judgment first.

## Phase 1: Choose the Unit

Choose a topic you teach or want to teach. It should be:

- small enough to fit in 3 to 5 lessons
- tied to real standards or course outcomes
- specific enough to assess
- familiar enough that you can judge AI output

<ReflectionPrompt>
What is one topic students often misunderstand in your class? A focused trouble spot usually makes a better first mini-unit than a broad course overview.
</ReflectionPrompt>

## Phase 2: Map the Learning

Create your standards unpacking sheet and mini-unit map:

```
Lesson 2: _______________
Lesson 3: _______________
Optional Lesson 4: _______________
Optional Lesson 5: _______________

Assessment Evidence: _______________
```

## Phase 3: Build the Artifacts

Use the OTS-101 template system to assemble your working materials:

- lesson template
- prompt library
- resource evaluation sheet
- assessment or quiz draft
- rubric
- delivery plan

Every artifact should be small enough that another teacher could understand it without a meeting.

### Source Bank Evidence

Your capstone should not include random links with no context. Use the [OpenTeachStack Source Bank](/kb/source-bank) when your mini-unit uses software, coding platforms, AI tools, open resources, cybersecurity resources, Google Workspace tools, or web-development references.

For every Source Bank item you use, record:

- resource name
- official URL
- audience
- cost model
- account requirement
- verification status
- privacy or age note
- how the source supports your learning target

If a resource is not in the Source Bank yet, you can still use it, but you must document it as a direct link that needs verification. Add enough notes that a future teacher can decide whether it belongs in the shared Source Bank.

## Phase 4: Verify and Revise

Before calling the capstone complete, run the safety and quality checks:

| Check | Evidence |
| --- | --- |
| Privacy | No student-identifiable data was entered into AI tools |
| Copyright/licensing | Borrowed resources are permitted and attributed |
| Source bank | Software, platform, and OER links were checked against the Source Bank or marked for verification |
| AI verification | AI claims, examples, answer keys, citations, and standards references were checked |
| Standards alignment | Lessons and assessment map to learning targets |
| Accessibility | Directions, links, images, and materials are usable and readable |
| Revision log | You documented what changed after review |

<RealityCheck>
The goal is not a perfect unit. The goal is a unit you understand, can defend, and can improve. If you cannot explain why an artifact is in the mini-unit, revise it or remove it.
</RealityCheck>

## Evaluation Criteria

Use this rubric to self-audit or peer-review the capstone:

| Category | Ready | Needs Revision | Not Yet |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Standards alignment | Learning targets are specific, teachable, and connected to assessment evidence. | Standards or outcomes are present, but the targets or assessment connection need tightening. | Standards are copied in without clear targets or assessment evidence. |
| Curriculum architecture | Lessons form a coherent 3 to 5 lesson sequence with a visible progression. | Lessons are mostly related, but the sequence, pacing, or transitions are unclear. | The unit is a pile of activities with no clear learning path. |
| Resource literacy | Resources are credible, attributed, accessible, and useful for the learning target. | Resources are mostly useful, but license, privacy, accessibility, or source notes are incomplete. | Links are random, unverified, unattributed, or disconnected from the target. |
| AI workflow | Prompts are documented, AI output is revised, and teacher judgment is visible. | AI use is documented, but revision decisions or rejected output are thin. | AI output appears pasted in without verification or teacher revision. |
| Assessment quality | The assessment measures the learning target and the rubric uses observable criteria. | The task is promising, but the rubric still uses vague quality words or measures effort more than learning. | The assessment does not clearly measure the learning target. |
| Delivery readiness | Directions, pacing, materials, feedback, alternate access, and backup plans are clear. | The lesson can probably be taught, but a substitute teacher or colleague would need extra explanation. | The delivery plan depends on private teacher memory or missing setup steps. |
| Safety and accessibility | Privacy, copyright, accessibility, AI verification, and source checks are complete. | Safety checks are present, but one or more risk areas need clearer evidence. | Student data, copyright, accessibility, or source risks are not addressed. |
| Reflection and revision | The revision log shows what changed, why it changed, and what should improve next. | Reflection is present, but revision decisions are mostly general. | There is no meaningful revision history. |

### Final Submission Checklist

Before sharing the capstone, make sure the final package includes:

- a single folder or document index that lists every artifact
- source links and license or terms notes for borrowed materials
- the state, district, or course outcome source used for standards alignment
- student-facing directions written in plain language
- one alternate access or submission option for students who need it
- privacy notes for any tool that collects student responses or account data
- captions, transcripts, alt text, or text alternatives for required media
- a revision log showing what changed after review

<TeacherNote>
A small, honest mini-unit is better than an ambitious shell full of placeholders. OTS-101 should help you build something teachable, not make you perform technical mastery you have not learned yet.
</TeacherNote>

Once your mini-unit is solid, choose a next pathway step:

- OTS-201 for deeper Google Workspace systems
- OTS-220 for Apps Script automation
- OTS-240 for GitHub and open-resource workflows
- OTS-260 for AI media and delivery production
- OTS-301 for course sites, domains, and hosting
- OTS-320 for AI coding agents
- OTS-399 for the full publishable pathway capstone

You are now a teacher who builds, verifies, revises, and owns the work.
