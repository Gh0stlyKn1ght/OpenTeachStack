# Creative Commons for Teachers

Copyright is automatic. The moment you create a lesson plan, a worksheet, or a slide deck, you own the copyright. No one can legally copy, distribute, or adapt your work without permission.

Creative Commons licenses are a way to grant that permission in advance, under specific conditions. They let you say: "You can use my work, as long as you follow these rules."

## The Four Elements

Every Creative Commons license is built from combinations of four elements:

| Element | Symbol | Meaning |
|---------|--------|---------|
| **Attribution (BY)** | BY | You must credit the original creator |
| **ShareAlike (SA)** | SA | Adaptations must use the same license |
| **NonCommercial (NC)** | NC | No commercial use without separate permission |
| **NoDerivatives (ND)** | ND | No modifications — share only the original |

## The Six Licenses

From most to least permissive:

1. **CC BY** — Do anything, just credit me
2. **CC BY-SA** — Do anything, credit me, share adaptations under same license
3. **CC BY-NC** — Non-commercial use only, credit me
4. **CC BY-NC-SA** — Non-commercial, credit me, share adaptations under same license
5. **CC BY-ND** — Share unchanged copies only, credit me
6. **CC BY-NC-ND** — Non-commercial, unchanged copies only, credit me

## Which License Should Teachers Use?

For most teacher-created curriculum materials, **CC BY-NC-SA 4.0** is the sweet spot:

- **BY** — Others credit you (professional recognition)
- **NC** — No one sells your work on Teachers Pay Teachers without permission
- **SA** — If someone improves your lesson, their version is also openly shared

This course uses CC BY-NC-SA 4.0 for all content.

## How to Apply a CC License

Adding a Creative Commons license to your work is simple:

1. Go to [creativecommons.org/choose](https://creativecommons.org/choose/)
2. Answer the questions about what you want to allow
3. Copy the license text or badge
4. Add it to your work (footer of a document, README of a repository, description of a shared folder)

Example attribution line:

> This work is licensed under [CC BY-NC-SA 4.0](https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-nc-sa/4.0/). You are free to share and adapt this material for non-commercial purposes, with attribution, under the same license.

## How to Use CC-Licensed Work

When you find CC-licensed materials to use:

1. **Check the license.** Not all CC licenses allow modification.
2. **Follow the terms.** If it says BY, give credit. If it says NC, do not sell it.
3. **Provide attribution.** Minimum: creator name, work title, license, link to original.

Example attribution:

> "DNS Explained for Teachers" by JC Nevarez, licensed under CC BY-NC-SA 4.0. Original: https://openteachstack.dev/lessons/dns-explained-for-teachers

<RealityCheck>
Creative Commons licenses are legally binding. "I found it on the internet" is not a license. "The website said CC BY" is. Teach students (and yourself) to check and cite licenses explicitly.
</RealityCheck>

<TeacherNote>
Understanding licensing is part of digital citizenship. When we use images without checking licenses, we model the behavior we tell students not to do. When we apply Creative Commons to our own work, we model contribution to the commons. This is not just a legal topic — it is a pedagogical one.
</TeacherNote>
