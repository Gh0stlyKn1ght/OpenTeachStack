# Delivery Planning Foundations

A good mini-unit is not finished when the documents are written. It is finished when a teacher can deliver it and students can understand what to do next.

OTS-101 focuses on delivery planning, not video production. Screen recording, OBS, advanced slide systems, and media workflows belong in the later AI Media & Lesson Delivery course.

## The Delivery Plan

For each lesson in your mini-unit, document:

- lesson title
- learning target
- estimated time
- materials needed
- student-facing directions
- teacher setup notes
- check for understanding
- feedback or revision step

This does not need to be fancy. It needs to prevent confusion during class.

## Student-Facing Directions

Good directions answer:

- What am I doing?
- Why am I doing it?
- What should I produce?
- How long do I have?
- What does good work look like?
- What should I do if I finish early or get stuck?

<TeacherNote>
If students need you to explain the assignment five different ways before they can begin, the directions are probably carrying too much hidden teacher knowledge.
</TeacherNote>

## Classroom Reality Checks

Before teaching the mini-unit, ask:

- Can this fit in the class periods I actually have?
- What technology has to work?
- What is the paper backup if the technology fails?
- Where will students submit work?
- When will I give feedback?
- What will I cut if the first lesson runs long?

## Safety and Quality Checklist

Before delivery, check:

- Privacy: examples and prompts do not expose student information.
- Copyright: handouts, images, readings, and videos are allowed and attributed.
- AI verification: teacher-facing notes and student-facing directions are reviewed.
- Standards alignment: activities still match the learning targets.
- Accessibility: directions are readable, links are descriptive, and images/videos have accessible alternatives.
- Revision log: changes after rehearsal or peer feedback are documented.
