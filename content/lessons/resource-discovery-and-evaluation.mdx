# Resource Discovery and Evaluation

Finding resources used to be the hard part. Now the hard part is filtering.

The internet is flooded with content. Blog posts written by AI that no human reviewed. Pinterest boards linking to broken worksheets. Teachers Pay Teachers listings that are repackaged versions of free resources. SEO-optimized articles that rank high on Google but contain nothing useful.

Your students deserve better than the first result on a search engine. And your curriculum deserves sources you can actually trust.

## The Source Hierarchy

Not all sources are equal. Use this hierarchy — start at the top and work down:

### Tier 1: Official Documentation

The single best resource for any tool, technology, or standard is the official documentation from the organization that created it.

- **Standards:** Your state department of education website
- **Google Workspace:** Google's official support documentation
- **Software tools:** The tool's official docs, guides, and help center
- **Laws and policies:** Government websites (.gov domains)

Official docs are not always easy to read. They are often dense and technical. But they are accurate, maintained, and authoritative. Everything else is someone's interpretation.

### Tier 2: Academic and Institutional Sources

- **University education departments** — Research papers, curriculum frameworks, and course materials from schools of education
- **Professional organizations** — ISTE, NCTE, NCTM, and subject-specific professional bodies publish standards, position statements, and curated resources
- **State education agencies** — Beyond standards, state agencies publish curriculum frameworks, sample assessments, and implementation guides
- **Federal education resources** — The Department of Education, the National Science Foundation, and similar agencies fund and publish educational resources

### Tier 3: Open Educational Resources (OER)

OER are freely available teaching materials with open licenses:

- **OER Commons** — A large repository of openly licensed educational materials across subjects and grade levels
- **MIT OpenCourseWare** — Full course materials from MIT, freely available
- **OpenStax** — Free, peer-reviewed textbooks
- **Khan Academy** — Video lessons and practice exercises
- **CK-12** — Free textbooks and adaptive practice

OER quality varies. Some resources are excellent — peer-reviewed, well-designed, and regularly updated. Others are abandoned projects with outdated content. Evaluate each resource individually.

### Tier 4: Community and Curated Sources

- **Teacher blogs and portfolios** — Individual teachers sharing what works. Quality varies enormously.
- **Education media** — Sites like Edutopia, KQED Education, and subject-specific publications
- **Social media** — Twitter/X threads, Reddit communities, Facebook groups. Occasionally useful, frequently unreliable.
- **Commercial platforms** — Teachers Pay Teachers, Teachable, etc. Some quality content exists, but it is mixed with low-effort material.

### Tier 5: General Web Search Results

Random blog posts, SEO content farms, and unattributed materials found through Google. Use with extreme caution and always verify.

<RealityCheck>
You will not always find a Tier 1 source. Sometimes a well-written blog post from a practicing teacher is the best resource available. The hierarchy is not about being rigid — it is about knowing where to start and being honest about the reliability of what you find.
</RealityCheck>

## Evaluating Credibility

Not everything that looks professional is trustworthy. Use these criteria:

### The SIFT Method

A practical framework for evaluating online sources:

1. **Stop.** Before using a resource, pause. Do you know this source? Do you trust it?
2. **Investigate the source.** Who created this? What is their expertise? What organization are they affiliated with?
3. **Find better coverage.** Can you find the same information from a more authoritative source? If a blog post claims something, does an official source confirm it?
4. **Trace claims to the original.** If a resource cites a study, find the study. If it quotes an expert, verify the quote. If it references a standard, check the standard document.

### Red Flags

Watch for these warning signs:

| Red Flag | What It Suggests |
|----------|-----------------|
| No author name or organization | No one is accountable for accuracy |
| No date or a very old date | Content may be outdated |
| No citations or references | Claims are unverified |
| Excessive marketing language | The goal is to sell, not to inform |
| Too-perfect formatting with generic content | Likely AI-generated |
| Broken links throughout | Abandoned or unmaintained resource |
| Content copied from another source without attribution | Intellectual dishonesty |
| Promises of "revolutionary" or "transformative" results | Marketing, not education |

## Spotting AI-Generated Sludge

Since 2023, the internet has been flooded with AI-generated content that no human reviewed, edited, or verified. This content is everywhere — blog posts, resource lists, even materials sold on educational marketplaces.

**Signs of AI-generated sludge:**

- Smooth, generic prose with no specific examples from real classrooms
- Listicles with 10-15 points that all say roughly the same thing in different words
- Perfect grammar but no personality, no specificity, no edge
- Introductions that start with "In today's rapidly evolving educational landscape..."
- Content that describes what something is but never explains how to actually do it
- Resources that list tools without demonstrating any of them
- Articles that reference "studies show" without naming the study

**The test:** Would this content change if you replaced every specific noun with a generic one? If an article about "teaching computer science" would read identically as an article about "teaching history" with just the nouns swapped, it is probably AI-generated filler.

</TeacherNote>

## Citation and Attribution

Using someone else's work without credit is plagiarism. Using someone else's work without a license is potentially illegal. Teachers know this in theory but often ignore it in practice — especially with online resources.

### Minimum Attribution

Every resource you use should include:

- **Creator name** (person or organization)
- **Title of the work**
- **Source** (where you found it — URL, publication, etc.)
- **License** (if applicable — Creative Commons type, public domain, etc.)
- **Date accessed** (for online resources)

**Example:**

> "Introduction to DNS" by Mozilla Developer Network. From MDN Web Docs. Licensed under CC BY-SA 2.5. Accessed January 15, 2026.

### When to Cite

- Any text, image, video, or data you did not create
- Any idea or framework you learned from someone else's work
- Any resource you link to or recommend to students
- Any adapted or modified version of someone else's material

### When You Do Not Need to Cite

- Your own original content
- Common knowledge within the field (no one needs to cite that the Earth orbits the Sun)
- Standard templates and formats (a rubric structure, a lesson plan format)

## Creative Commons Licenses: Quick Reference

Creative Commons licenses define how others can use a work. As a resource consumer, you need to understand what each license allows:

| License | Can Share? | Can Modify? | Commercial Use? |
|---------|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| CC BY | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| CC BY-SA | Yes | Yes (same license) | Yes |
| CC BY-NC | Yes | Yes | No |
| CC BY-NC-SA | Yes | Yes (same license) | No |
| CC BY-ND | Yes | No | Yes |
| CC BY-NC-ND | Yes | No | No |

If a resource has no license statement, assume full copyright. You cannot legally copy, distribute, or modify it without permission.

## The Resource Evaluation Checklist

Use this checklist every time you consider adding a resource to your curriculum:

```
RESOURCE EVALUATION CHECKLIST

Resource: ____________________
URL/Source: ____________________
Date Found: ____________________

AUTHORITY
[ ] Creator/author is identified
[ ] Creator has relevant expertise or credentials
[ ] Publishing organization is credible
[ ] Contact information or organizational about page exists

ACCURACY
[ ] Content is factually correct (spot-checked key claims)
[ ] Citations or references are provided for major claims
[ ] Content is consistent with what I know from authoritative sources
[ ] No obvious errors, typos, or broken elements

CURRENCY
[ ] Publication or last-updated date is recent enough for the topic
[ ] Links within the resource still work
[ ] Information reflects current standards, tools, or practices

PURPOSE
[ ] The resource's goal is educational (not marketing or sales)
[ ] Content is substantive, not filler
[ ] The resource is free of excessive advertising or promotion

USABILITY
[ ] Content is at the appropriate level for my students
[ ] Format works for my instructional context
[ ] Resource is accessible (works on student devices, loads properly)
[ ] License allows my intended use

OVERALL
[ ] I would stake my professional credibility on this resource
[ ] I can properly cite and attribute it
```

## Building a Resource Library

Instead of searching for resources every time you need one, build a curated library:

1. **Create a spreadsheet.** Columns: Resource Name, URL, Subject/Topic, Type (video, article, tool, dataset), License, Quality Rating, Date Reviewed, Notes.
2. **Add resources as you find them.** Only add resources that pass your evaluation checklist.
3. **Organize by topic and unit.** Tag resources by where they fit in your curriculum.
4. **Review annually.** Links break. Content gets outdated. Tools change pricing. Review your library at least once a year and remove dead links.
5. **Share with colleagues.** A curated resource library is one of the most valuable things you can share with other teachers.

## Recommended Resources to Curate

- Your state department of education website for standards, frameworks, and approved resources
- OER Commons for openly licensed educational materials
- Creative Commons search tools for finding CC-licensed images, text, and media
- Your district's approved resource list or curriculum library
- Professional organization websites for your subject area (ISTE, NCTE, NCTM, NSTA, etc.)
- University library guides on evaluating sources (many are freely available online)
